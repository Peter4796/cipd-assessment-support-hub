CIPD Reflective Writing Model Bank

Four reflective structures with ready-to-adapt sentence stems. Pick one model, follow its stages, and keep the account built on your own genuine experience.

Reflection must describe your own real experience in your own words. These structures and stems are scaffolding for your thinking, not text to copy in. Check your brief and centre guidance for any model your centre expects.

1 · Driscoll: What? So what? Now what?

The simplest structure and the hardest to get wrong. Best when you are new to reflection or short on words.

What happened

The situation I want to reflect on is…

My role in it was… and what I actually did was…

So what does it mean

Looking back, I think I responded that way because…

The effect of my actions on others was…

What this tells me about how I work is…

Now what will I do

Next time a similar situation arises, I will…

I will know this is working when…

2 · Gibbs: the reflective cycle

Adds explicit stages for feelings and evaluation. Best when your emotional reaction was part of the story. Spend most words on analysis, not description.

  • Description: Briefly, what happened was…
  • Feelings: At the time I felt… which affected how I…
  • Evaluation: What went well was… and what went badly was…
  • Analysis: The reason it unfolded this way was… My assumption was…
  • Conclusion: What I have learned specifically is…
  • Action plan: From now on I will… and I will review this by…

3 · Kolb: the experiential learning cycle

A learning loop. Best when you can show an insight tested in later practice.

  • Concrete experience: The experience that started this was…
  • Reflective observation: Standing back, what I noticed was…
  • Abstract conceptualisation: The general lesson I drew was…
  • Active experimentation: I tested this by… and the result was…

4 · Schon: reflection-in-action and on-action

Best for experienced practitioners writing about thinking on their feet.

In the moment, I noticed… so I adjusted my approach by…

Reflecting afterwards, I can see that my in-the-moment reading was…

What I would trust or change about my instinct next time is…

Stems for honest learning statements

I learned that I tend to… and that this costs me…

I had assumed… but the situation showed…

The feedback I received was… which suggests…

The specific change I have made since is…

Final checks before you submit

  • The account describes my own genuine experience, in the first person.
  • There is more analysis and learning than storytelling.
  • The learning is specific to me, not a general truth about workplaces.
  • A concrete action follows, with a way to know whether it worked.
  • The model is scaffolding in the background, not the subject of the account.
