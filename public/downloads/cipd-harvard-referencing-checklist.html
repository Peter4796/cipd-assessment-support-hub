CIPD Harvard Referencing Checklist

Run this checklist over your draft before you submit. It covers in-text citations, the reference list and the habits that keep referencing accurate.

Conventions vary slightly between study centres and style guides. Where this checklist and your centre's referencing guidance differ, your centre's guidance always wins.

1 · In-text citations

  • Every claim drawn from a source has an author and year citation beside it.
  • Direct quotes are in quotation marks with a page number where one exists.
  • Organisational sources (like the CIPD) are cited by organisation name as author.
  • Citations sit next to the specific claims they support, not dumped at paragraph ends.
  • Sources I only met inside another source are cited as secondary ('cited in').

2 · Quoting and paraphrasing

  • Most sources are paraphrased in my own words, not quoted.
  • Every direct quote earns its place (definitions or wording that matters).
  • Paraphrases genuinely restate the idea; they are not near-copies with swapped words.

3 · The reference list

  • Every in-text citation has a matching reference-list entry.
  • Every reference-list entry is cited somewhere in the text.
  • Entries are alphabetical by author surname (or organisation name).
  • Each entry carries the details its source type needs (author, year, title, publication or site, publisher or URL).
  • Online sources include an 'Accessed' date.
  • Formatting (italics, punctuation, capitalisation) is consistent across every entry.

4 · Source quality

  • My sources are credible: CIPD materials, textbooks, journals, official statistics.
  • I have actually read everything I cite.
  • Secondary references are kept to a minimum; I found originals where I could.
  • The mix of sources suits my level (higher levels lean on journals and wider reading).

5 · Final referencing review

  • I checked my centre's referencing guide for its specific conventions.
  • I re-read the draft once looking only at citations and references.
  • Reference-list entries match their in-text years and spellings exactly.
  • Nothing in the draft presents someone else's idea as unsourced.
