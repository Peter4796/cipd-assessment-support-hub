Most CIPD assignments that lose referencing marks do not lose them to obscure rules. They lose them to the same short list of Harvard referencing mistakes, repeated across cohorts and levels, and almost all of them are quick to fix once you can recognise them. Referencing errors are also the most visible errors in your work, because a marker can spot a mismatched citation or a missing accessed date at a glance, before they have judged a single idea.

This guide works through the ten mistakes markers see most often. For each one you will see what it looks like on the page, why it costs marks, and the fix. One caveat before you start: referencing conventions vary in fine detail between centres and style guides, so where anything here differs from your centre's referencing guide, your centre wins. All example authors in this guide, such as Mensah, are invented for illustration.

## In this guide you'll learn

- The ten Harvard referencing mistakes that appear most often in CIPD submissions
- Why each one costs marks, from lost credibility to academic integrity concerns
- The specific fix for each, plus a five-minute pre-submission check

## 1. Citations and reference list entries that do not match

This mistake runs in both directions: a citation shaped like (Mensah, 2024) in the text with no Mensah in the reference list, or a reference list entry that is never cited anywhere in the writing. It usually happens when paragraphs are moved or deleted during editing and the list is not updated, though some learners pad the list deliberately, hoping length reads as wider reading. It costs marks because the list is the marker's map of your evidence, a citation that leads nowhere makes every other citation less trustworthy, and markers recognise padding instantly.

The fix is procedural rather than intellectual. Add the full entry the moment you insert a citation, and before submission run a two-way check: read the text and tick each citation off against the list, then read the list and find each entry's citation in the text. Anything unmatched gets fixed or removed.

## 2. Inconsistent formatting between entries

One journal article has its title in quotation marks and the next does not. One year sits in brackets and another floats free. Dates appear in three formats down the same list. No single slip is fatal, but the cumulative effect tells the marker the work was not checked, and precision is part of what a professional qualification assesses.

Pick one pattern for each source type, ideally the version in your centre's referencing guide, and apply it to every entry of that type. Formatting the list in one sitting, source type by source type rather than entry by entry, makes inconsistencies far easier to spot.

## 3. Missing accessed dates on online sources

Web pages change and disappear, so Harvard asks for the date you accessed each online source, and factsheets, reports and web pages make up a large share of a typical CIPD reference list. Entries that stop at the title, with no indication of where the source lives or when you used it, are incomplete, and markers treat them as such.

Capture the accessed date on the day you first use the source, not from memory during a final edit. If you already have a list with gaps, revisit each online source, confirm it still says what you cited, and record today's date as the accessed date.

## 4. Citing a website by its address instead of its author

The entry, or worse the in-text citation, is essentially a web address. Harvard cites authors, and for most credible online sources the author is an organisation: a professional body, a government department, a research institute. Citing an address instead signals that you never worked out who is accountable for the content, which is exactly the credibility judgement your assessment wants to see.

Identify the organisation behind the page and cite it as a corporate author, shaped like (CIPD, 2025) in the text, with a full entry under the organisation's name giving the title, an online marker and the accessed date.

## 5. Over-quoting instead of paraphrasing

Whole sentences and paragraphs lifted verbatim, in quotation marks, stitched together with connecting phrases. It is honest, but it earns little, because quotation only proves you can copy accurately. Paraphrase proves you understood, and understanding is what carries the marks. Heavy quotation also spends your word count on words that are not yours.

Reserve quotation for precise definitions, legislation and genuinely distinctive phrasing. Convert everything else into your own words, keep the citation, and check that quotations are rare in the final draft rather than a paragraph-by-paragraph habit.

## 6. Citing sources you have not actually read

A reference list stocked with classic names and seminal studies that the learner met only as summaries inside a textbook. It misrepresents your reading, it inherits any errors in the summary you relied on, and it unravels quickly if a tutor asks a probing question about a source you supposedly used.

Cite the path you actually took. If you read about one writer's work inside another's, either find and read the original, or use the cited in convention and list only the source you read.

> Integrity note: a citation is a factual claim that you read something. Never let your reference list claim reading you have not done; honesty about a modest evidence trail is respected, while the pretence of a grander one is what integrity processes exist for.

## 7. Secondary sources cited as if primary

The mechanical version of the previous mistake. Suppose a textbook by an invented author, Mensah, summarises an earlier model by another invented author, Virtanen. The error is citing Virtanen directly, with a full reference list entry, when you only ever read Mensah. The original may not say quite what the summary claims, and your list now points the reader to a work you cannot vouch for.

Use the cited in construction, shaped like (Virtanen, 1998, cited in Mensah, 2024), and list only Mensah. The companion guide Secondary Referencing in Harvard Style, Explained covers the convention in full, including when it is acceptable and how to track down originals.

## 8. References dumped at the ends of paragraphs

A paragraph makes four or five claims, then closes with a cluster of citations, leaving the marker to guess which source backs which claim. It reads as decoration rather than evidence, and it makes your reasoning impossible to verify, so the analysis marks the paragraph should earn quietly drain away.

Attach each citation to the specific claim it happened to sit behind when you were reading. One claim, one citation, at the point in the sentence where the borrowed idea appears.

## 9. Relying on sources that are not credible

Anonymous blogs, essay-help websites, consultancy marketing pages and unattributed summaries, cited as though they carry the same weight as research. CIPD qualifications are explicitly about evidence-based practice, so the credibility of your sources is itself being assessed. Weak sources drag down strong arguments.

Build your list around peer-reviewed journal articles, CIPD publications, respected textbooks and official statistics. Google Scholar is the fastest free route to the first of these, and a small set of strong sources used purposefully beats a long list of weak ones every time.

## 10. Leaving referencing to the last hour

Everything above becomes more likely when referencing is treated as a formatting job for the night before submission. Sources get reconstructed from memory, page numbers get guessed, accessed dates get invented, and the two-way check never happens because there is no time left.

Reference as you write. Capture full source details at the moment of reading, insert citations while drafting, and grow the list alongside the text. The pillar guide CIPD Harvard Referencing: The Complete Guide sets out this workflow step by step.

## A five-minute pre-submission check

1. Read the text and tick every citation off against the reference list.
2. Read the list and find each entry's citation in the text; fix or delete anything unmatched.
3. Confirm every online entry has an accessed date and an identifiable author or organisation.
4. Scan the list for formatting consistency, one pattern per source type, in alphabetical order.
5. Count your direct quotations, and convert any that are not earning their place into paraphrase.
