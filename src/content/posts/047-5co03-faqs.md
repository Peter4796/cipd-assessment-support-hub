5CO03, Professional behaviours and valuing people, generates more nervous questions than almost any other unit in the Level 5 Associate Diploma in People Management, mostly because of its reflective component. Writing about your own behaviour feels unfamiliar, and the usual academic habits do not quite fit. Here are straight answers to the questions learners ask most. As always, treat your own current assessment brief and your study centre's guidance as the final word, because task formats and word counts vary between centres.

### What is 5CO03 actually about?

It is the unit where you demonstrate the behaviours and values of an effective people professional: ethics, inclusion, wellbeing, building relationships and influence, and commitment to your own development. Unlike knowledge-based units, it asks for evidence of how you work, drawn from your own practice, which is why it usually includes a reflective element. Think of it as the unit that tests professionalism as something you do rather than something you claim.

The themes run wider than ethics alone. Expect to engage with championing inclusion and wellbeing in practical terms, with how you build professional relationships and exercise influence, and with honest reflection on your own strengths, gaps and development. The connecting thread is evidenced self-reflection: every claim you make about how you work should be anchored in something you have actually done.

### Can I write in the first person?

In the reflective sections, yes, and you should. Reflection is analysis of your own experience, so I is the natural and expected voice. Sentences like I assumed, I decided and I misjudged are exactly what assessors want to see. More discursive tasks may still suit a formal register, so follow the wording of each task and your centre's guidance on style.

If first person writing feels uncomfortable after years of being told to avoid it, remember what the assessor needs from a reflective account: your choices, your assumptions and your learning, visibly owned by you. Passive constructions such as it was decided that hide exactly the agency being assessed, so writing around yourself usually costs marks rather than adding polish.

### Which reflective model should I use?

Any of the well-known models can work: Kolb's learning cycle, Gibbs' reflective cycle, or Driscoll's What, So what, Now what questions. Driscoll is the simplest to apply and hard to get wrong; Gibbs suits situations where your emotional reaction was part of the story; Kolb suits accounts centred on learning and experimentation. What matters is not the choice but the application. Use the model as invisible scaffolding for your paragraphs rather than spending your word count describing it.

### What counts as evidence in a reflective account?

Specific, first-hand detail from your own experience: what the situation was, what you did, why, what happened as a result, and what you took from it. Evidence in reflection is not documents or references so much as concrete particulars that only someone who was there could give. A dated example with named consequences, such as a meeting you handled badly and what it cost, is evidence. A general claim that you always communicate well is not.

You can strengthen this kind of evidence by showing its aftermath: feedback you received, a change you made and kept, or a later situation you handled differently because of the earlier one. Cause and effect across time is very hard to fake and very convincing to read, which is why honest, specific accounts consistently outperform polished generalities.

### Do I need a CPD plan?

The unit includes reflecting on and planning your own development, so expect your brief to ask for development planning in some form, whether it is called a CPD plan or something else. Whatever the format, the same principle applies: the plan should grow out of the gaps your reflection actually uncovered. A plan that lists generic courses with no connection to your reflective account reads as an afterthought, and assessors notice.

A credible plan is specific and reviewable. For each development area, say what you will do, by when, what resources or people you will draw on, and how you will judge whether it worked. Two or three well-chosen priorities with clear actions carry more weight than a long wish list.

### How personal should my examples be?

Personal enough to be real, professional enough to be appropriate. You need genuine situations where your behaviour was tested, including ones that did not go well, because those are where the learning is. You do not need to share private matters, and you should anonymise colleagues and avoid anything confidential or identifying about individuals. If in doubt, change names and identifying details while keeping your own actions and thinking accurate.

### What if I have limited HR experience?

Use the experience you do have. Behaviours like integrity, inclusion, influence and self-development show up in any role, in volunteering, and even in group work during your studies. A small real example analysed with honesty will score better than an impressive-sounding scenario you cannot examine in depth. Inventing experience is the one option to rule out entirely: fabricated reflection undermines the purpose of the unit and tends to read as generic and thin. If you are truly short of material, speak to your tutor early about suitable sources of evidence.

### Why do people get referred on 5CO03?

The most common reasons are descriptive reflection that recounts events without analysing behaviour, claims about values with no real examples attached, word counts spent describing reflective models instead of using them, and development plans disconnected from the reflection. Underneath all of these is the same root cause: treating 5CO03 like a knowledge unit and keeping yourself out of the writing. The unit is about you, and referred work is usually work in which the writer never really appears.

A referral on this unit is rarely about intelligence or effort; it is about register. Learners who resubmit successfully usually change the voice of the work, putting themselves back into the account and replacing safe generalities with specific, slightly imperfect real examples. If you have been referred, that shift is the place to start.

### How is ethics assessed in this unit?

Through application, not declaration. Assessors are not checking whether you believe in fairness; they are looking for evidence of how you act when ethical principles are tested, such as handling a request that conflicted with policy, challenging bias, or raising an unpopular concern. The strongest answers pick moments of genuine tension from real practice and analyse the choices made. Frameworks like the CIPD Profession Map can help you frame professional values, but use them as a lens on your own conduct rather than quoting them at length.

### How should I handle the word count?

Check your own brief, because word counts and tolerances are set by centres and vary. Once you know the limit, share it out roughly in proportion to the criteria each task carries, and protect space for the reflective sections, which learners routinely under-allocate. Depth on fewer, better examples beats breadth: two situations analysed properly will serve you better than five described in passing.
