## What 5CO03 is about

5CO03, Professional behaviours and valuing people, is the Level 5 unit that asks you to demonstrate the professional behaviours and values of an effective people professional. Where other units in the Level 5 Associate Diploma in People Management test what you know about organisations, employment relations or people practice, this one tests how you work: your ethics, your commitment to inclusion, the way you build relationships and influence others, and how seriously you take your own development. The unit treats professionalism as something you do and evidence, not something you claim.

That is why 5CO03 usually includes a reflective component. You cannot demonstrate a behaviour in the abstract, so the unit asks you to look at your own practice, pull out real situations, and show what they reveal about how you work and what you are learning. Evidenced self-reflection is the thread that runs through the whole assignment.

## The main themes

- Professional and ethical behaviours: acting with integrity, handling ethical tension in real situations, and knowing where your professional values come from
- Championing inclusion and wellbeing: going beyond policy statements to show how you make work fairer and healthier for real people
- Building professional relationships and influence: how you earn trust, work with stakeholders and get things done through others
- Reflecting on and planning your development: honest self-assessment, meaningful learning from experience, and a credible plan for what comes next

## How 5CO03 is assessed

Like most Level 5 units, 5CO03 is assessed through written work marked against the criteria in your brief. The exact format varies by study centre, so always work from your own current assessment brief and your centre's guidance rather than a generic task list found online. Word counts and task structures differ between centres too, which is why no guide can tell you definitively how long each answer should be.

What is consistent is the expectation. Level 5 asks for application and analysis, not description, and 5CO03 raises the bar further by asking for personal evidence. A strong answer connects each behaviour or value to a specific example from your own practice and then examines it: what you did, why, what the impact was, and what it tells you about yourself as a professional. A weak answer lists behaviours like a checklist and asserts that the writer has them.

## The reflective component explained

The reflective element is where 5CO03 differs most from other units, and it is the part learners find hardest. Reflection at Level 5 means standing back from your own experience and analysing it: not a diary of events, and not a summary of a theory, but a structured examination of what you did, what happened, what you would do differently, and what that means for your development.

First person writing is expected here. Sentences like I noticed, I decided, I underestimated are exactly what a reflective account needs, even though they would look odd in a report-style answer. Reflective models such as Kolb's learning cycle, Gibbs' reflective cycle or Driscoll's What, So what, Now what questions can give your account a shape, but the model should sit in the background as scaffolding. Assessors want your thinking, not a description of the model.

> Integrity note: reflection only works if it is genuinely yours. Inventing examples or borrowing someone else's experience defeats the purpose of the unit, and fabricated reflection tends to read as thin and generic to an experienced assessor.

One more thing follows from that note. Because the reflective account has to be about your real experience, the preparation for 5CO03 starts long before you write anything: it starts with collecting honest raw material from your working life.

## Ethics and inclusion in practice

Ethics and inclusion are easy to write about badly, because it is tempting to state values everyone agrees with. The unit is not asking whether you believe in fairness; it is asking how you act on it. So look for moments of friction in your own practice. A time a manager asked you to do something that sat uncomfortably with policy or principle. A recruitment decision where you noticed bias creeping in. A wellbeing concern you raised even though it was awkward. These moments, handled honestly, are far stronger evidence than any statement of belief.

The CIPD Profession Map is a useful reference point here, because it frames professional values and behaviours in terms people professionals recognise. Use it as a lens for examining your examples rather than quoting it at length: the marks come from your analysis of your own conduct, not from restating a framework.

## How to approach 5CO03

1. Read your current brief slowly and map every task to the criteria it covers, noting which parts ask for reflection and which ask for discussion or explanation.
2. Gather raw material before drafting: list eight to ten real situations from your practice that involved ethics, inclusion, wellbeing, influence or learning, with rough notes on what happened and what you took from each.
3. Select the strongest examples, favouring situations with some tension or difficulty, because easy situations rarely reveal much about behaviour.
4. Choose one reflective model to structure your reflective sections, and commit to using it as a quiet framework rather than describing it.
5. Draft the discursive sections first, connecting each point about behaviour or values to evidence and to your organisation's context.
6. Draft the reflective sections in the first person, moving deliberately from what happened to what it meant to what you will do next.
7. Build your development planning section on the gaps your reflection actually revealed, so the plan grows out of the account rather than being bolted on.
8. Review the whole draft against every criterion in your brief, checking that each claim about your behaviour is anchored to a specific example.

## A worked example

Here is a fictional scenario to show the approach in action. Priya is an invented people adviser in a mid-sized logistics firm, studying 5CO03. A line manager asks her to quietly speed up a disciplinary process for a team member he finds difficult, hinting that the outcome is already decided. Priya feels the pull between keeping a senior stakeholder happy and protecting a fair process.

A checklist answer would say something like: I always act ethically and follow correct procedure. Priya's stronger approach is to use the situation as evidence. She describes the request briefly, then analyses her response: she declined to shortcut the process, explained the risk to the manager in business terms, and escalated when the pressure continued. She reflects honestly that she initially softened her language too much because she was worried about the relationship, and that the delay this caused taught her something about her own conflict avoidance. She then links this to her development plan: building confidence in early, direct challenge, with specific actions and review points. One incident, properly examined, gives her evidence of ethical behaviour, influence, self-awareness and development planning all at once.

## Common mistakes

- Writing about behaviours in the abstract, with no specific examples from real practice
- Producing a descriptive diary instead of reflection, so events are recounted but never analysed
- Spending the reflective word count explaining Kolb or Gibbs instead of using the model on an experience
- Choosing only success stories, which leave nothing to learn from and make reflection shallow
- Treating ethics and inclusion as statements of belief rather than evidenced actions
- Bolting on a development plan that ignores the gaps the reflection uncovered
- Ignoring the brief's structure and answering a generic version of the unit found online

## 5CO03 FAQs

### Can I really write in the first person?

Yes, in the reflective sections it is expected. Reflection is personal by definition, so I is the natural voice. Discursive sections may still suit a more formal register, so let each task's wording guide you, and check your centre's guidance if you are unsure.

### Do I need to name a reflective model?

Most learners benefit from using one, and naming it briefly is fine. The marks, though, come from the quality of your reflection, not from the model. If your account moves clearly from experience to analysis to future action, the structure is doing its job.

### What if my people-practice experience is limited?

Use what you have. Experience from any role, volunteering or study group work can evidence behaviours like integrity, inclusion and influence. Small, real examples analysed well beat impressive-sounding examples you cannot examine honestly. If you are genuinely stuck for material, talk to your tutor early.

### How long should each answer be?

Word counts vary by centre and by brief, so no external guide can give you a reliable number. Check your own brief, note any tolerance your centre allows, and share the words out roughly in proportion to the criteria each task carries.
