Here are answers to the questions learners ask most often about 5CO02, Evidence-based practice, the Level 5 unit that asks you to analyse data, think critically and connect evidence to recommendations. The answers below give you the method and the principles, but formats and requirements vary between study centres, so always check the details against your own current brief and your centre's guidance.

### What is 5CO02 about?

5CO02, Evidence-based practice, sits within the Level 5 Associate Diploma in People Management and develops your ability to make sound decisions based on evidence. Its themes are the principles of evidence-based practice, analysing data and measuring the impact of people practice, critical thinking and decision-making, and creating value for the organisation and its people.

The idea behind the unit comes from the wider evidence-based practice movement, associated with writers such as Barends and Rousseau, who argue that good decisions draw on several sources of evidence at once: research findings, organisational data, practitioner expertise and stakeholder concerns. The unit trains you to gather those sources, weigh them and act on them.

In practice, the assignment tests whether you can move from evidence to conclusion to recommendation in a way a marker can follow. That single thread runs through every task.

### Is 5CO02 hard?

It is different rather than harder. Learners who are comfortable writing essays sometimes wobble when they meet tables and calculations, while numerate learners sometimes struggle to show critical thinking in prose. The unit asks for both, which is why it feels demanding.

Treat it as a method to learn rather than a talent you either have or lack. Most learners who are referred fall down on approach, such as describing data instead of interpreting it, rather than on ability.

Time management matters too. The data tasks reward early, unhurried attention, so learners who open the figures in the first week generally find the unit far more manageable than those who leave the numbers until the end.

### What data analysis does 5CO02 require?

Typically you are given a data set, often a table of people-related figures such as turnover, absence or survey results, and asked to analyse it, present your findings and draw conclusions. That usually means simple calculations, clear tables or charts, and written interpretation of what the patterns suggest.

The exact tasks vary by centre, so check your brief. Whatever the format, the marks concentrate in the interpretation and the conclusions, not the arithmetic.

### Do I need statistics skills?

No formal statistics background is needed. The calculations are the kind you can handle with basic spreadsheet functions, such as totals, averages and simple comparisons. What the unit really tests is whether you can read figures carefully, spot patterns and limitations, and reason from them.

A helpful habit is to describe each result in three moves: what the number is, how it compares to something else, and what that comparison might mean. An average on its own says little; an average that is rising, or that is higher in one team than another, starts to tell a story a marker can credit.

If numbers make you anxious, open the data early and practise describing one pattern at a time. Confidence follows familiarity, and a basic spreadsheet will do all the heavy lifting.

### Which sources count as credible?

Peer-reviewed journal articles, CIPD research and reports, respected academic textbooks and established professional publications are safe ground. Government and official statistics bodies are also credible for labour market context.

Be cautious with consultancy marketing content, anonymous websites and unattributed summaries, because part of this unit is showing you can judge credibility. When in doubt, ask what the source's own evidence is and who is accountable for it.

Google Scholar is a quick way to find peer-reviewed material, and a small set of strong sources used thoughtfully will always beat a long list of weak ones. Aim to cite each source for a specific point rather than decorating paragraphs with references.

### Can I use my own organisation?

Often yes, and applying your analysis to an organisation you know can strengthen your answer, but follow your brief. Many centres supply a case study and data set that you must use. If you do draw on your employer, anonymise sensitive details and keep the focus analytical rather than anecdotal.

Be careful with confidentiality. Do not reproduce internal figures or documents you are not entitled to share, and check with your tutor if you are unsure. A well-analysed anonymised example earns just as much credit as a named one.

### Why do people get referred on 5CO02?

The most common reasons are opinion presented without evidence, data described but not interpreted, recommendations with no visible link to the analysis, weak sources, and thin answers to the calculation tasks. Misreading command verbs, such as explaining when asked to evaluate, is another regular cause.

All of these are avoidable with a structured approach. The 5CO02 common mistakes guide works through each one in detail, with fixes.

### How should I present tables and charts?

Keep them simple, label them clearly, and give each one a title and a number so you can refer to it in your text. Choose the format that fits the data: a table for precise values, a bar chart for comparisons, a line chart for trends over time.

Never let a visual stand alone. Every table or chart should be introduced in your writing and followed by interpretation, because presentation without commentary earns very little.

Resist the urge to decorate. A plain, well-labelled chart that makes one point clearly is worth more than a colourful dashboard that makes none, and markers read the interpretation, not the styling.

### How do I handle the word count?

Word counts and tolerances are set by your centre, so check your brief rather than relying on any figure you find online. Plan your allocation across tasks in proportion to their weight, and note that tables, charts and reference lists are often treated differently from body text, which your centre's guidance will confirm.

If you are over the limit, cut description first. Interpretation and justified recommendations are where the marks live.

### How is the critical element different from just giving my opinion?

Opinion states a conclusion. Critical thinking shows the reasoning that leads to one: the evidence considered, its strengths and limitations, the alternative explanations weighed, and why one conclusion is more convincing than the others.

A useful test is whether a sceptical reader could check your reasoning from what is on the page. If they would simply have to take your word for it, it is opinion. If they can follow your evidence and logic, it is analysis.
