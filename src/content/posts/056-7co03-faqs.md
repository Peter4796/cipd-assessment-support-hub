Here are answers to the questions learners ask most often about 7CO03, Personal effectiveness, ethics and business acumen, the Level 7 unit that asks you to examine your own practice with postgraduate rigour. Many people arrive at this page after a referral, so several answers speak directly to that situation. The answers give you principles and method, but task formats and requirements vary between study centres, so always check the details against your own current brief and your centre's guidance.

### What is 7CO03 about?

7CO03 sits within the Level 7 Advanced Diploma in Strategic People Management and develops personal effectiveness, ethical practice and commercial awareness at a senior level. Its themes are ethical values and professional courage, influencing and building credibility, championing inclusion and diversity, and business acumen in people practice.

What makes it distinctive is the direction of the lens. Most units ask you to analyse organisations and their people practices; this one asks you to analyse yourself as a practitioner, usually through reflective and applied tasks. Critical self-awareness and sound judgement are the qualities being assessed throughout.

### How is Level 7 different from Level 5?

Level 7 is postgraduate, and the standard shifts from explaining to critically engaging. At Level 5, describing a concept accurately and applying it sensibly earns credit. At Level 7, markers expect you to weigh competing perspectives from the literature, question assumptions including your own, acknowledge ambiguity, and commit to a justified position rather than a balanced summary.

Reflection changes in the same way. Level 5 reflection can be an honest account of what happened and what you learned. Level 7 reflection must be theorised and evidenced: assumptions examined, ideas from reading used as lenses on your own behaviour, and concrete changes in practice shown rather than claimed.

### Why is 7CO03 referred so often?

Three register problems account for most referrals. Descriptive reflection: narrating events with a line of learning attached, rather than questioning assumptions and evidencing change. Unevidenced claims of effectiveness: statements like I am a credible influencer with nothing a marker can inspect beside them. Thin commercial awareness: fluent writing on values and inclusion that goes vague whenever money, risk or organisational performance enters the picture.

Notice what is not on the list: ability. Experienced practitioners referred on this unit almost always have the insight the unit wants; they have presented it in a professional register rather than an academic one. That is fixable, and usually with targeted revision rather than a full rewrite.

### Can I write in the first person at masters level?

Yes, and for the reflective elements it is normally expected. The idea that academic writing forbids the first person is a hangover from other disciplines; reflective assessment requires an I who acts, doubts and learns. Rigour comes from evidence, reasoning and engagement with sources, not from grammatical distance.

The balance to strike is a first-person voice with academic discipline: claims supported, hedges honest, literature integrated. Check your centre's guidance in case it sets particular conventions for the applied, non-reflective tasks.

### How much literature does my reflection need?

Enough to show your experience being examined through ideas, and no more. A useful pattern is two or three sources per major reflective episode, each doing real work: a concept from Schön or Kolb structuring the analysis, an idea such as Mezirow's questioning of assumptions naming what happened to your thinking, and ideally one source that challenges your reading of events rather than confirming it.

Avoid the wallpaper approach, where a paragraph of citations sits next to an untouched story. One idea applied precisely to one moment of your practice is worth more than five names in a row.

### What does professional courage look like in practice?

In assessment terms, professional courage is not grand defiance; it is the willingness to raise an uncomfortable truth with someone who has more power than you, at some personal cost, because the evidence or your values require it. Challenging a popular decision, naming an inclusion problem the leadership team would rather not see, or defending an unpopular recommendation are all everyday forms.

Strong answers show the workings: how you weighed the risk, how you chose your moment and framing, what it cost, and what you learned about your own thresholds. An account that includes hesitation or partial failure usually demonstrates more courage, and more self-awareness, than a tidy victory.

### How do I show business acumen?

Ground every people argument in the realities of a business you know: how it earns money, where its costs and risks sit, what its strategy demands, and what its context is doing to it. Business acumen shows up in reasoning, not declarations, so express people decisions in terms of cost, risk, capability and performance, and show that you understand the trade-offs leaders face.

A practical test: could a finance director read your answer and recognise the organisation being discussed? If your commercial points would be true of any organisation anywhere, they are too generic for Level 7.

### What should I do if I have been referred?

First, normalise it: this is a resubmission-heavy unit, and a referral at Level 7 usually reflects register and evidencing rather than ability. Read the feedback twice, slowly, and translate every comment into a specific action in a specific section. Most 7CO03 feedback maps to one of three fixes: deepen the reflection, evidence the claims, or sharpen the commercial reasoning.

Then plan a targeted revision rather than a panicked rewrite, and use the guide How to Respond to CIPD Tutor Feedback for a structured way of decoding comments and turning them into a revision plan. Keep what worked, fix what was named, and check the whole piece for the same fault the feedback identified, because a fault named once usually appears more than once.

### How personal should my examples be?

Personal enough to be real, professional enough to be appropriate. The unit needs your genuine experience: real decisions, real feedback, real doubt, because manufactured or borrowed examples produce hollow reflection and breach academic integrity. Honesty about misjudgement is rewarded, not punished, so do not sanitise the account until nothing is left to examine.

At the same time, protect others. Anonymise colleagues and sensitive situations, avoid disclosing anything you are not entitled to share, and keep the focus on your thinking and behaviour rather than on judging named individuals. If an episode is too raw or too confidential to analyse properly, choose another one.

### How do I handle the word count?

Word counts and tolerances are set by your centre, so work from your own brief rather than any figure you find online, and check how appendices, reference lists and any tables are treated. Plan your allocation across tasks in proportion to their weight before you draft.

If you are over the limit, cut description first: narrative, scene-setting and repeated context. Analysis, evidence and justified judgement are where the marks live, and trimming story to protect thinking almost always improves a 7CO03 answer anyway.
