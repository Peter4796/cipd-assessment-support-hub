## What 3CO02 is about

3CO02, Principles of analytics, is the unit in the Level 3 Foundation Certificate in People Practice that builds your confidence in working with data and evidence. It looks at how people professionals use information and analytics to inform decisions, and at how measuring people practice helps demonstrate the value it adds to an organisation.

The unit is practical and applied rather than theoretical. You are not asked to master analytical techniques for their own sake; you are asked to show that you understand where evidence comes from, how simple data can be used and presented to help a decision, and why handling that data responsibly matters. Because the unit is applied, worked examples of using data are one of the best ways to meet the criteria in your brief.

## The main themes

- Evidence-based practice and where data and evidence come from
- Using and presenting data to help organisations make decisions
- Measuring the impact and value of people practice
- Handling data ethically and responsibly

## A word about the maths

Let us deal with the anxiety first. The numbers in 3CO02 are the kind you already use in daily life. A count is just how many, such as the number of people who left last year. A percentage is a way of saying how many out of every hundred, which you already understand from discounts in shops. An average smooths a set of numbers into one typical figure, exactly as you would when splitting a restaurant bill.

There are no formulas to memorise and no statistical tests. A calculator or spreadsheet does any arithmetic for you. What the unit rewards is not calculation but sense-making: looking at a small set of numbers, noticing what stands out, and saying in plain English what it might mean for the organisation.

## How 3CO02 is assessed at Level 3

3CO02 is assessed through written tasks marked against the criteria in your brief. At Level 3 the command verbs lean towards explain, describe and summarise. An explain question wants you to say what something is and why it matters, in your own words. A describe question wants a clear, accurate account. Some tasks will give you simple data, or ask you to use some, and want you to show what it suggests and how it could inform a decision.

As with every CIPD unit, work from your own current assessment brief and your study centre's guidance. Task formats, word counts and the exact wording of the criteria vary between centres and change over time, so treat this guide as a map rather than the territory.

## Evidence-based practice in plain English

Evidence-based practice is a grand-sounding phrase for a simple idea: making decisions based on evidence rather than guesswork, habit or the loudest voice in the room. Instead of assuming that people are leaving because of pay, an evidence-based people team would look at what leavers actually say in exit interviews, what the turnover figures show, and what managers and employees think, and then decide.

In 3CO02 you will usually be asked to explain the idea and show where evidence comes from. It helps to remember that evidence is broader than numbers. Data from the organisation's own systems is evidence, but so are the experiences of people professionals, the views of employees and managers, and published research and good-practice guidance described in general terms. A good decision draws on more than one of these.

## Common types of people data and where they come from

People data simply means information about people at work, and most organisations already collect more of it than they realise. Absence records show who has been off, how often and for how long, and usually sit in an HR or payroll system. Turnover figures show how many people leave over a period. Recruitment data covers things like the number of applicants for a vacancy and how long hiring takes. Survey results capture how people feel about their work, and performance information comes from appraisals and reviews.

For your assignment you do not need access to a real organisation's systems. You can use figures given in your brief, or invent a small, believable set of numbers for a fictional organisation, clearly presented as an example.

## Using data in your answers

The heart of the unit is the move from a number to a decision. A useful pattern has three steps: say what the data shows, in one or two plain sentences; say what it might mean, offering a sensible interpretation rather than a certainty; and say what the organisation could do next. That pattern turns a table of figures into an answer a marker can credit.

Presenting data matters too, and simple beats clever. A small table, a bar chart to compare groups, a line chart to show change over time or a pie chart to show shares of a whole are all you need, clearly labelled. The dedicated guide in this series, Using People Data in 3CO02, Explained, walks through all of this in detail.

## Handling data ethically

The unit also asks you to show that you understand handling data responsibly, and at Level 3 this stays practical and general. Keep personal information safe and secure, share it only with people who genuinely need it, and use it only for proper work purposes. When you present data, protect people's privacy, for example by reporting figures for teams rather than naming individuals, and be honest: present the numbers fairly rather than picking only the ones that suit your argument.

## How to approach 3CO02, step by step

1. Read the whole brief twice and list every task and criterion it contains, so you know exactly what needs a visible answer.
2. Underline the command verb in each task and note what it wants: explain means what and why, describe means a clear account, summarise means the short version.
3. Note which tasks involve data, and decide early where your figures will come from: the brief itself, or a small invented example organisation.
4. Set a rough word budget for each task in proportion to how much it asks for, and write the number next to it before drafting.
5. For every data task, follow the three-step pattern: what the data shows, what it might mean, and what the organisation could do next.
6. Keep any charts or tables simple and clearly labelled, with a sentence of interpretation underneath each one, because the picture alone earns little.
7. Define each term the first time you use it, in your own words, from evidence-based practice to turnover.
8. Check every answer against the criteria in your brief line by line before you submit, and close any gaps while there is still time.

> Reassurance note: nobody fails 3CO02 for simple arithmetic done honestly with a calculator. Marks are lost for missing criteria and for numbers left uninterpreted, not for the maths itself.

## A worked example: Brightcall Contact Services

Here is a fictional scenario that shows the approach in action. Brightcall Contact Services is an invented call centre with 120 employees across three teams: daytime customer service, evening customer service and a small billing team. Amira, a fictional learner, uses Brightcall as her example organisation throughout her assignment.

For a task on using data to inform a decision, Amira invents a small, believable absence dataset. Across the last year, Brightcall's average absence is 6 days per employee. Broken down by team, daytime customer service averages 5 days, billing averages 4, and evening customer service averages 9. She presents this in a simple bar chart with the teams along the bottom and days of absence up the side, clearly titled and labelled.

Then she interprets. The data shows that evening absence is noticeably higher than the other teams. It might mean several things: the evening shift pattern may be harder to sustain, supervision may be lighter at night, or a few long-term absences may be pulling the average up, so the figures need a closer look before anyone acts. Her recommendation is that Brightcall investigates the causes, starting with return-to-work conversations and a check of the shift rota, and reviews the figures again in three months to see whether things improve.

Notice the shape: what the data shows, what it might mean, what to do next. The numbers are invented and clearly framed as an example, the chart is simple, and the interpretation is cautious rather than overconfident. That is exactly the level 3CO02 is looking for.

## Common mistakes to avoid

The predictable failure patterns in 3CO02 are presenting numbers without saying what they suggest, which is the single most common gap; describing data in general terms without connecting it to any decision; overcomplicating answers with technical language the brief never asked for; and cluttered or unlabelled charts that leave the marker guessing. A quieter mistake is skipping the ethical side of the unit, which needs only a few sensible sentences but must be visibly there.

All of these are avoidable: give every number a sentence of interpretation, end every data task with a recommendation, and make sure every chart could be understood by someone reading it cold.

## 3CO02 FAQs

### Do I need to be good at maths to pass 3CO02?

No. The unit uses everyday arithmetic, such as counts, percentages and averages, and a calculator or spreadsheet can do all of it for you. The marks sit in the explanation and interpretation, not the calculation.

### Can I invent data for my assignment?

Usually yes, provided it is clearly presented as a fictional example and your brief does not instruct otherwise. Invented figures for a made-up organisation, kept small and believable, are a well-established way to show you can use data. Check your own brief and centre guidance first, as some tasks supply the data they want you to use.

### What does evidence-based practice mean?

Making decisions based on evidence rather than guesswork or habit. Evidence includes the organisation's own data, the experience of people professionals, the views of employees and other stakeholders, and published research described in general terms. The idea is to draw on several sources rather than relying on opinion alone.

### What if I have never worked in HR?

That is fine, and it is what the qualification expects. You can ground your answers in any job you have held, since absence, surveys and performance reviews exist in every workplace, or in a clearly invented organisation like Brightcall above. Markers want specific, plausible application, not insider access.
