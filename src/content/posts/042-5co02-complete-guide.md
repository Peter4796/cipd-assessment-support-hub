## What 5CO02 is about

5CO02, Evidence-based practice, develops your ability to make sound decisions based on evidence rather than habit, hierarchy or hunch. It covers analysing data, applying critical thinking to what you read and hear, and creating value for the organisation and its people. In short, it teaches you to answer the question every senior leader eventually asks a people professional: how do you know?

The thinking behind the unit draws on the wider evidence-based practice movement, associated with writers such as Barends and Rousseau. Their central idea is that good decisions rest on multiple sources of evidence: findings from research, data from the organisation, the expertise of practitioners, and the concerns of stakeholders. A strong 5CO02 submission shows you weighing these sources rather than leaning on any single one.

This also connects to the CIPD Profession Map, which places evidence-based decision-making at the core of what a people professional does. Markers are looking for exactly that behaviour on the page: claims backed by credible evidence, and a clear line from that evidence to what you recommend.

## How 5CO02 is assessed

5CO02 is assessed through a written assignment marked against the criteria in your brief. Briefs vary by study centre, but most include a mix of discussion questions and data-based tasks where you are given figures, often in a table, and asked to interpret them, present them and draw conclusions.

Strong submissions share one feature: a clear line from evidence to recommendation. The marker can see what the data or literature says, how you interpreted it, and why that interpretation leads to your proposed action. Weak submissions describe data without interpreting it, offer opinions without evidence, or analyse well but never land on a recommendation.

Always work from your own current assessment brief and your centre's guidance. Word counts, task formats and the exact wording of the criteria differ between centres and change over time, so treat any general description of the unit, including this one, as a map rather than the territory.

## Quantitative and qualitative evidence explained

5CO02 asks you to work confidently with two broad types of evidence, and to know when each is useful.

### Quantitative evidence

Quantitative evidence is numerical: turnover rates, absence figures, survey scores, cost data, headcount. Its strength is that it shows scale and trend. Its weakness is that it rarely explains cause. A rising resignation figure tells you something is happening, but not why.

In the assignment, quantitative work usually means calculating simple measures such as averages or proportions from a table you are given, presenting the results clearly, and commenting on what the pattern suggests. The maths is basic. The marks come from the interpretation.

### Qualitative evidence

Qualitative evidence is descriptive: exit interview comments, focus group themes, free-text survey answers, observations. Its strength is that it explains meaning and cause. Its weakness is that it is harder to generalise, because a handful of voices may not represent the whole workforce.

In the assignment, qualitative work means identifying themes, weighing how representative they are, and using them to explain or challenge what the numbers show.

### Using the two together

The strongest answers pair the two. Numbers show you where to look, and qualitative evidence tells you what you are looking at. If turnover is rising in one department and exit interviews from that department repeatedly mention workload, you have the beginnings of a justified conclusion rather than a guess.

## How to approach 5CO02, step by step

1. Break down the brief. List every task, note the command verb in each, and check what evidence each task expects. Analyse, evaluate and discuss demand different things, and answering the wrong verb is one of the quickest ways to lose marks.
2. Open the data early. If your brief includes a table, spend time with it before you write a word. Work out totals, comparisons and trends, and note anything surprising.
3. Gather a small set of credible sources. Peer-reviewed articles, CIPD publications and respected professional texts carry weight. A few sources used well beat a long list used superficially.
4. Analyse before drafting. For each finding, ask what it shows, what might explain it, what else could explain it, and what it means for the organisation.
5. Draft with the evidence-to-recommendation line in view. Each answer should let a marker trace evidence, interpretation, conclusion and recommendation without effort.
6. Show critical thinking on the page. Weigh the strengths and limitations of your evidence, acknowledge alternatives, and explain why your reading is the most convincing.
7. Present data professionally. Label tables and charts, refer to them in your text, and never leave a visual to speak for itself.
8. Review your draft against the criteria in your brief before submitting, checking each requirement line by line.

> Tutor tip: after every claim in your draft, ask what evidence sits behind it and where a marker can see that evidence on the page. If the answer is nowhere, you have written an opinion, not analysis.

## A worked example: Brightway Stores

Here is a fictional scenario that shows the method in action. Brightway Stores is an invented retail chain with forty branches. Its people team notices that annual staff turnover has climbed for three consecutive years and is now well above the level the business planned for, with the sharpest rise among sales assistants in city-centre branches.

A descriptive answer would stop there: turnover is rising, which is bad, so Brightway should improve retention. That earns little, because it interprets nothing and recommends nothing specific.

An evidence-based answer digs in. The analyst breaks the figures down by branch type, role and length of service, and finds that most leavers go within their first year. Exit interview themes mention unpredictable shift patterns and a lack of early training. Published labour market commentary confirms that retail turnover is generally high, which puts the numbers in context, but the concentration among new starters in city branches points to something local rather than an industry-wide pattern.

The recommendation then follows the evidence: pilot a structured first-year induction and a fairer shift-allocation approach in the worst-affected city branches, and measure the effect on early leavers over the following year. Notice the line: data, breakdown, qualitative explanation, external context, targeted recommendation, and a way to measure impact. That is the shape 5CO02 rewards, whatever your own brief asks.

## Common mistakes to avoid

Most referred 5CO02 submissions fail in predictable ways: opinion presented as fact, data described but never interpreted, recommendations that appear from nowhere, and thin answers to the calculation tasks. Each of these is avoidable once you can recognise it. ## 5CO02 FAQs

### Do I need strong maths for 5CO02?

No. The calculations involved are the kind you can do with basic spreadsheet skills, such as averages and simple comparisons. The marks sit in the interpretation, not the arithmetic. If numbers worry you, practise on the data in your brief early so the anxiety does not crowd out your analysis.

### Can I base my answers on my own organisation?

Usually yes, and it often strengthens application, but follow your brief. Some centres provide a case study and data set you must use. If you do draw on your employer, anonymise sensitive information and keep the focus on analysis rather than storytelling.

### How is critical thinking different from having opinions?

An opinion is a conclusion without visible reasoning. Critical thinking shows its working: it tests evidence for credibility, considers more than one explanation, and explains why one conclusion is stronger than the alternatives. Markers cannot reward what they cannot see, so put the reasoning on the page.

### What if my brief looks different from this guide?

Follow your brief every time. Centres word tasks differently and update them regularly. Use this guide for method and understanding, and your own current brief and centre guidance for the exact requirements.
