You open the feedback on your referred assignment hoping for a clear explanation, and instead you get a handful of short, clipped comments: 'more analysis needed', 'limited use of evidence', 'does not fully address the criteria'. It can read as cold, even dismissive, and it is easy to spiral into wondering what the assessor really thought. Here is the reassuring truth: the terseness is structural, not personal. Assessors write against criteria, not essays. Their job is to record, briefly, which requirements your work met and which it did not, often across many scripts in a sitting, so their comments are compressed technical notes rather than considered prose.

That compression is why the same phrases appear again and again across levels, units and centres. And it is good news, because a small vocabulary of recurring phrases can be learned. Once you know what each one signals, what it looks like on the page and what fixes it, referral feedback stops being a verdict and becomes a repair list. This guide decodes the phrases you are most likely to meet.

## In this guide you'll learn

- Why assessor feedback reads as terse and how to reframe it
- What the most common referral phrases are actually signalling
- The specific fix each phrase is asking for, with what it looks like in a draft
- A numbered method for turning any comment into an action

## 'More analysis needed' or 'descriptive rather than analytical'

This is the most common referral comment in CIPD study. The assessor is signalling that your answer explains what things are but never gets to what they mean. In a draft it looks like accurate summary: a model outlined, a policy described, a process narrated, all correct and none of it weighed, compared or judged.

The fix is to add judgement, not length. Take each descriptive passage and push it through three questions: how does this compare with an alternative, what are its strengths and limitations in this context, and what conclusion follows? Sentences built around because, however, whereas and therefore are usually analytical; sentences built around is, has and involves are usually descriptive. Rewrite in place rather than appending analysis after the description.

## 'Insufficient application to the organisation'

The assessor is signalling that your answer floats free of any real or case-study context. It reads as a textbook answer that could have been written by someone who has never seen a workplace: the theory is present, but nothing connects it to a named organisation, its sector, its size or its specific circumstances.

In a draft this looks like paragraphs where you could swap in any company and nothing would change. The fix is to anchor each key point to the organisation your brief asks you to use, whether that is your employer, a case study or an organisation you know well. Show how the concept plays out there specifically: what it would change, what would get in the way, what the context makes easier or harder. One concrete, relevant workplace example per point is worth more than a paragraph of general assertion.

## 'Does not fully address the criteria' or 'criterion not met'

This signals a coverage gap rather than a quality problem. Somewhere in the requirements is an element your answer skipped, answered partially or addressed so indirectly that the assessor could not credit it. Often the writing that is there is perfectly good; the issue is what is missing.

In a draft it looks like an answer that responds to the topic in general rather than to the specific requirement in the brief. The fix starts with a mapping exercise: put the wording of each requirement next to your answer and highlight exactly where each element is dealt with. Wherever you cannot highlight anything, you have found the gap. Then address a criterion marked as not met directly and visibly, using the language of the requirement itself so the assessor can find the fix without hunting.

## 'Limited use of evidence' or 'wider reading needed'

The assessor is signalling that your claims rest on your own experience and opinion alone, or on one or two sources stretched thin. In a draft it looks like page after page with no citations, or the same textbook cited for every point, or references listed at the end that never actually appear in the body.

The fix is targeted, not decorative. Identify your most important claims, the ones that carry your conclusions, and support each with a credible source: CIPD research and reports, peer-reviewed articles, respected professional texts or official statistics. A handful of strong sources, each cited for a specific point, beats a long bibliography that the body text never uses. Wider reading means reading that visibly shapes the argument, not a longer list.

## 'Assertions are not supported'

A close cousin of the evidence comment, but sharper: you have stated conclusions as though they were facts. In a draft it looks like confident sentences such as claiming an approach always improves engagement or that a practice is best practice, with nothing behind them.

The fix is to back or soften every load-bearing claim. Either attach evidence, a source or visible reasoning, or reframe the claim honestly as a possibility with a rationale. Assessors are not asking you to be timid; they are asking you to show why a reader should believe you.

## 'Answer does not address the command verb'

Every task in a CIPD brief is built around a command verb: explain, analyse, evaluate, discuss, assess, compare. This comment signals that you answered a different verb than the one asked, most often explaining when asked to evaluate. In a draft it looks like a competent answer to the wrong question: an account of what something is when the task demanded a judgement of how well it works.

The fix begins with rereading the task wording, underlining the verb and being honest about what it demands. Explain asks for how and why. Analyse asks you to break something into parts and examine relationships. Evaluate and assess ask for a judgement of worth, with criteria and a conclusion. Discuss and compare ask for more than one perspective set against each other. Restructure the answer so its shape matches the verb, and make the verb's output, especially the judgement, impossible to miss.

## 'Referencing does not follow Harvard conventions'

A mechanical comment with a mechanical fix. The assessor is signalling that your citations or reference list depart from the format your centre requires: missing in-text citations, inconsistent author-date formatting, web links pasted in place of proper references, or a reference list that does not match the citations in the body.

The fix is systematic rather than creative. Get your centre's referencing guide, since Harvard has local variations and your centre's version is the one that counts, then work through the draft checking every citation and every list entry against it. Check the pairing in both directions: everything cited appears in the list, and everything in the list is cited. This is tedious and entirely certain to succeed, which makes it the easiest referral point to fix.

## 'Insufficient depth for this level'

This signals that the work reads below the expectations of the level you are studying at. Each CIPD level expects something different: broadly, Level 3 rewards clear explanation and application, Level 5 expects analysis and evaluation with supporting evidence, and Level 7 expects critical engagement with research and strategic implications. Writing that would pass comfortably at one level can be referred at the next.

In a draft it looks like short, surface-level answers: points raised and dropped in a sentence, no engagement with complexity or competing views, conclusions without visible reasoning. The fix is to develop fewer points further rather than adding more points. Take your strongest arguments and extend each one: evidence, counterpoint, implication, conclusion. Depth is a property of how far you follow a point, not how many you mention.

## 'Word count not met' or 'exceeds tolerance'

A signal about length, but read it as a signal about substance. A submission far under the count almost always indicates underdeveloped answers, which is why it travels with depth comments. One over tolerance suggests padding, repetition or description crowding out the analysis that space should have gone to.

The fix depends on direction. If you are under, do not pad; find the criteria that are thinly answered and develop them properly, and the count will look after itself. If you are over, cut description, repeated context and long quotes before touching analysis. Remember that limits and tolerance bands are set by your study centre and vary, as does what counts towards them, so check your brief rather than assuming. The guide to resubmission word count strategy covers this reallocation in detail.

## Turning any comment into an action

Whatever the phrase, the same method converts it into something you can do.

1. Locate it: identify exactly which task, section or criterion the comment attaches to, asking your tutor if the feedback does not say.
2. Classify it: decide whether it is a depth problem, a coverage gap, an application gap, an evidence gap or a mechanical issue, using the decodings above.
3. Translate it: rewrite the comment as a plain instruction to yourself, naming the section and the change.
4. Size it: estimate what the fix needs in words and effort, from a formatting sweep to a rewritten section.
5. Verify it: after revising, reread only the fix and ask whether an assessor who wrote that comment would now consider it answered.

A page of comments processed this way becomes a short, ordered task list, which is a far better companion for a resubmission than a wounded reread of the feedback.

> Tutor tip: assessors want your resubmission to pass, and terse comments are not irritation, they are shorthand. If any comment still makes no sense after you have tried to decode it, ask. A two-line email to your tutor early in the resubmission window is the cheapest fix in this entire guide.
