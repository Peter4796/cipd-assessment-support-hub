The email lands, the result says referred, and somewhere in the message is a resubmission deadline. If you are working full-time, that date can feel like a wall rushing towards you, and the temptation is either to start rewriting frantically that same evening or to push the whole thing away for a week until the anxiety fades. Neither serves you well.

A referral is a normal, fixable part of CIPD study, and the resubmission window is almost always enough time if you use it in the right order. The learners who struggle are rarely short of hours; they spend the hours on the wrong phase at the wrong moment. This guide gives you a phased plan that works whatever the length of your window, plus realistic advice for fitting it around a full-time job.

## In this guide you'll learn

- How resubmission windows work and why the first few days matter most
- A five-phase plan from decoding feedback to the final criterion check
- How to schedule revision honestly around full-time work

## Know your window

The resubmission window is the period between receiving your referral feedback and the date your revised work is due. There is no universal length: windows are set by study centres, they vary considerably between providers and sometimes between courses at the same provider, and they can be affected by cohort schedules and marking cycles. The only version that matters is yours, so read the referral communication carefully, confirm the exact deadline in writing if it is ambiguous, and check your centre's guidance for anything else the window involves, such as booking a feedback call or submitting through a particular portal.

While you are checking, note what support is attached to the window. Many centres offer a tutor conversation, written clarification or a resubmission briefing during this period. Knowing what is available, and any cut-off for requesting it, shapes the whole plan.

## Why the first days matter

The early part of the window is disproportionately valuable, for two reasons. First, this is when decoding the feedback is easiest: the assignment is fresh in your mind, and tutor support is typically most accessible soon after results are released. If a comment is ambiguous, a question asked in the first week usually gets answered in time to act on; the same question asked three days before the deadline may not.

Second, everything downstream depends on the diagnosis. If you misread what the feedback is asking for, every hour of revision that follows is aimed at the wrong target. Spending the opening days on careful reading and clarification is not slow, it is the fastest route to a focused revision. What you should not do in the first days is rewrite. Resist the urge to open the document and start changing paragraphs before you have a plan.

## The five phases of a resubmission

A resubmission has a natural sequence, and each phase protects the one after it.

Phase one is decode and diagnose. Read the feedback twice, once to absorb it and once to annotate it. List every criterion marked as not met, translate each comment into plain English, and ask your tutor about anything unclear. The guide How to Respond to CIPD Tutor Feedback covers this translation step in depth.

Phase two is plan the revision. Turn the diagnosis into a written action list: which sections change, what each change involves, and roughly how many words each needs. Decide what you will not touch, because protecting the sections that passed is as much a part of the plan as fixing the ones that did not.

Phase three is revise section by section. Work through the action list one item at a time, finishing each fix before starting the next. Section-by-section revision keeps the work honest: you can see progress, and a half-finished draft is still coherent if life interrupts.

Phase four is the cool-off. Leave the revised draft alone for at least a day or two if the window allows. Distance is what lets you read your own work as a marker would, and errors that were invisible on the night of writing are obvious after a pause.

Phase five is the final criterion-by-criterion check. Go through each referred criterion and find the exact place in the revised draft where it is now met. If you cannot point to it, the marker cannot either. Finish with a proofread and a check of referencing and formatting requirements, then submit with time to spare rather than at the last minute.

## An example schedule in phases

Windows vary too much for a fixed day-by-day plan to be honest, so think in proportions of whatever window you have.

1. Spend roughly the first tenth of your window on phase one: reading, annotating and raising questions with your tutor while support is available.
2. Spend the next tenth on phase two: writing the action list and word budget, and deciding what stays untouched.
3. Give the middle half of the window to phase three, revising section by section, starting with the changes that carry the most referred criteria.
4. Reserve a clear pause of a day or two for phase four, placed after the drafting is genuinely finished rather than squeezed in while it continues.
5. Keep the final stretch for phase five: the criterion-by-criterion check, proofreading and an unrushed submission at least a day before the deadline.

If your window is short, compress the middle phases rather than deleting the first or last ones. Diagnosis and the final check are the two phases that most reliably prevent a second referral.

## Scheduling around full-time work

Most CIPD learners are working full-time, so the plan has to survive contact with a working week. A few principles help. Plan in blocks, not moods: two or three evening blocks of about ninety minutes plus one longer weekend block is a realistic weekly rhythm for most people, and it is far more productive than a vague intention to work on it when you can. Match tasks to energy: decoding feedback and planning suit a tired weekday evening better than heavy rewriting does, so save the demanding revision for your freshest block. Protect rest deliberately: a resubmission built on late nights produces exactly the rushed, thin writing that gets referred, so keep at least one full evening and a decent stretch of the weekend completely free. Tell someone: a partner, friend or manager who knows the deadline makes the blocks easier to defend.

Put the blocks in your calendar now, for the whole window, before the diary fills up. A printable resubmission planner is available in the resources section of this site if you want a ready-made structure to map your phases and blocks onto.

## If the deadline feels impossible

Sometimes the window collides with a work crisis, illness or family demands, and no amount of scheduling fixes it. The answer is to talk to your tutor or centre early, not to disappear and hope. Centres have extension and deferral policies for exactly these situations; the details vary widely, and most look far more kindly on a request made in the first half of the window than a plea on deadline day. Explain the situation plainly, say what you can realistically deliver and when, and ask what options exist. Even where no extension is possible, an early conversation often surfaces help you did not know was available.

## A worked example: Marcus's window

Marcus is a fictional Level 3 learner who works full-time in a busy HR shared services team. His assignment came back referred on two criteria, one about applying his answer to an organisation and one about supporting his claims with evidence, with a resubmission window of a few weeks. His first instinct was to spend the weekend rewriting the whole assignment; instead he followed the phases.

On the first two evenings he annotated the feedback and emailed his tutor one clarifying question, which was answered within days. The following evening he wrote his action list: four specific changes across two sections, with a word budget for each, and a note that the other three sections would not be touched. He then booked his revision blocks into his calendar for the rest of the window, two evenings a week and Saturday mornings, keeping Friday evenings and Sundays free.

The revision itself took five of those blocks. He left the draft alone for two days, then used his final Saturday for the criterion-by-criterion check, where he caught one flagged criterion that his new paragraph mentioned but did not clearly evidence, and fixed it with a workplace example. He submitted with two days to spare. Nothing about the process was heroic, which is exactly the point.

## Common pitfalls

- Starting to rewrite on day one, before diagnosing what the feedback actually asks for.
- Revising everything instead of the sections the feedback named, and breaking work that had already passed.
- Treating the plan as hourly and abandoning it after one bad week, instead of adjusting the remaining blocks.
- Skipping the cool-off and submitting the draft the same evening it was finished.
- Leaving the criterion-by-criterion check to the last night, when there is no time left to act on what it finds.
- Waiting until deadline week to mention a genuine clash to the centre.

> Reassurance note: a resubmission window that looks frightening as a single deadline almost always looks manageable once it is broken into phases. You do not need more talent to pass a resubmission; you need the right work in the right order.
