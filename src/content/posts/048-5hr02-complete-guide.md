## What 5HR02 is about

5HR02, Talent management and workforce planning, develops your ability to help an organisation plan for and manage the talent it needs. It spans workforce planning, resourcing and recruitment, talent management and succession, and turnover and retention. The unit asks a deceptively simple question: how does an organisation make sure it has the right people, in the right roles, at the right time, and how does it keep them?

Strong answers treat these activities as connected rather than as separate topics. Recruitment problems are often really planning problems, retention problems are often really management or reward problems, and all of them show up in the data an organisation holds on turnover, vacancies and internal moves. Markers reward learners who link these activities to organisational performance and who use relevant data and examples, because that is exactly what the unit is designed to teach.

## How 5HR02 is assessed

5HR02 is assessed through a written assignment marked against the criteria in your brief. Briefs vary by study centre, but most mix explanation tasks, such as describing approaches to workforce planning or methods of recruitment and selection, with application tasks that ask you to relate those approaches to an organisation and its context.

Strong submissions share three features. They connect people activities to business needs, so a resourcing decision is justified by what the organisation is trying to achieve. They use data, especially turnover and retention figures, as evidence rather than decoration. And they apply concepts to a specific organisation, real or clearly fictional, rather than describing theory in a vacuum. These map directly onto the difficulties learners report most often in this unit: linking planning to business needs, using data effectively, and applying concepts rather than reciting them.

Always work from your own current assessment brief and your centre's guidance. Word counts, task formats and the exact wording of the criteria differ between centres and change over time, so treat this guide as a map rather than the territory.

## Workforce planning and labour markets in brief

Workforce planning is the process of making sure an organisation has the people it needs to deliver its strategy. In outline, it means understanding the demand for people that the strategy creates, understanding the supply of people available inside and outside the organisation, identifying the gaps between the two, planning actions to close those gaps, and reviewing whether the actions worked.

The labour market is the supply side of that equation. An organisation recruiting nurses, software engineers or drivers faces a very different market from one recruiting entry-level administrators, and a good plan reflects that. Useful analysis considers both the internal labour market, meaning the skills, potential and turnover patterns of the current workforce, and the external one, meaning the availability, cost and expectations of candidates outside. Official sources such as ONS labour market statistics give you credible external evidence to cite.

Workforce planning is arguably the most important topic in the unit, and it deserves more space than a hub guide can give it. The companion article Workforce Planning in 5HR02, Explained works through the planning cycle, labour market analysis and a full worked example in detail.

## Resourcing, recruitment and talent management

### Resourcing and recruitment

Resourcing covers how an organisation attracts and obtains the people its plan says it needs. In the assignment this usually means discussing recruitment methods, such as employee referrals, job boards, social media, agencies and internal advertising, and selection methods, such as structured interviews, work sample tests and assessment centres. The trick is to evaluate rather than list: every method has strengths, weaknesses and costs, and the right choice depends on the role, the labour market and the organisation's reputation as an employer.

The idea of an employee value proposition is useful here. It describes the overall deal an organisation offers, spanning pay, development, flexibility, purpose and culture, and it shapes both who applies and who stays. An organisation struggling to attract candidates in a tight labour market often has a proposition problem rather than an advertising problem.

### Talent management and succession

Talent management covers how organisations identify, develop and deploy the people who matter most to future performance. Approaches differ: some organisations focus on a small high-potential group, while others take an inclusive view that everyone has talent worth developing, and each approach carries risks worth discussing. A well-known framework here is the nine-box grid, which maps people by performance and potential to prompt development conversations. Use frameworks like this critically, noting their limits as well as their uses.

Succession planning is talent management applied to continuity: identifying the roles the organisation cannot afford to leave empty and preparing credible internal candidates for them. In assignments, succession is a strong place to show the link to business risk, because an unplanned exit from a critical role has a visible cost.

## Turnover, retention and their costs

Turnover data is the evidence base for much of this unit, and using it well separates strong submissions from weak ones. The headline turnover figure is the number of leavers in a period as a proportion of average headcount, but the headline is rarely the story. Voluntary and involuntary turnover mean different things. Turnover concentrated in one team, one role or the first year of service points to a specific problem, while an even spread suggests something more general.

Retention is the other side of the same coin, and the costs of getting it wrong go well beyond a recruitment fee. Leavers take knowledge and relationships with them, vacancies load extra work onto the people who stay, and new starters take time to reach full performance. You do not need precise cost figures to make this argument in an assignment; reasoning through the categories of cost, anchored in a specific role, is more convincing than an unsourced statistic.

When your brief gives you figures, interpret them. Compare them over time, between teams and against any external benchmark your sources provide, and always ask what might explain the pattern before you recommend a fix.

## How to approach 5HR02, step by step

1. Break down the brief. List every task, note the command verb, and mark which tasks ask for explanation and which ask for application to an organisation.
2. Choose your organisational context early. Whether you use your employer, a case study from your centre or a fictional organisation, decide at the start so every answer can draw on the same context.
3. Sketch the connected story. Note how the organisation's strategy drives its demand for people, what its labour market looks like, and where its turnover and retention pressures sit.
4. Gather a small set of credible sources. CIPD publications, respected texts and official statistics such as ONS labour market data carry weight; a few sources used well beat a long list used superficially.
5. Answer explanation tasks with evaluation. When you describe a planning approach or a recruitment method, say what it is good for, what its limits are and when you would choose it.
6. Answer application tasks with specifics. Name the roles, the market conditions and the data points that make your recommendation fit this organisation rather than any organisation.
7. Use turnover and retention data as evidence. Interpret the figures you are given or the patterns you describe, and let your recommendations follow from them.
8. Review your draft against the criteria in your brief before submitting, checking each requirement line by line.

> Tutor tip: for every recommendation in your draft, ask which business need it serves and which piece of evidence backs it. If you cannot point to both, the marker cannot either.

## A worked example: Amberhill Hotels

Here is a fictional scenario showing the method in action. Amberhill Hotels is an invented group of twelve regional hotels. Its strategy is to move upmarket over the next three years, which means more skilled kitchen and front-of-house roles. At the same time, turnover among its chefs is high and rising, and the local hospitality labour markets are tight.

A descriptive answer would explain what workforce planning is, list some recruitment methods and note that turnover is expensive. It would earn little, because nothing in it is specific to Amberhill.

An applied answer starts from the strategy: moving upmarket creates demand for skilled chefs that the current workforce cannot fully meet. It reads the labour market: skilled chefs are scarce locally, so competing on advert volume will fail, and the employee value proposition matters more, perhaps through development pathways, predictable rotas and a better kitchen culture. It uses the data: chef turnover concentrated in the first year of service suggests problems with onboarding and early experience, which retention action can address more cheaply than replacement hiring. And it closes the loop with succession: head chef roles are critical, so each hotel needs a developing sous chef ready to step up. Every recommendation traces back to the strategy, the market or the data, and that is the shape 5HR02 rewards.

## Common mistakes to avoid

- Describing models and processes without applying them to an organisation, which reads as a textbook summary rather than an assignment
- Ignoring labour market context, so recommendations assume candidates are freely available when the market says otherwise
- Making turnover or retention claims with no data or reasoning behind them
- Treating the topics as separate essays instead of one connected argument about people and performance
- Inventing statistics or citing sources you have not read, which markers spot quickly
- Missing the command verbs, such as describing when a task asks you to evaluate

## 5HR02 FAQs

### Do I need real turnover data from my organisation?

No. If your brief supplies figures, use those. If it does not, you can reason with realistic illustrative patterns, clearly framed as such, or draw on published sources for context. What matters is that you interpret whatever data you use rather than merely reporting it.

### Which models and frameworks should I use?

Use a small number and use them critically. The workforce planning cycle, the nine-box grid for talent conversations and the idea of an employee value proposition all earn their place when applied to your organisation. Name-dropping frameworks without application earns very little.

### Can I base my answers on my own organisation?

Usually yes, and it often strengthens application, but follow your brief, since some centres supply a case study you must use. If you draw on your employer, anonymise sensitive information and check you are entitled to share any figures you include.

### What if my brief looks different from this guide?

Follow your brief every time. Centres word tasks differently and update them regularly. Use this guide for method and understanding, and your own current brief and centre guidance for the exact requirements.
