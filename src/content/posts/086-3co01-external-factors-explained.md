Somewhere in your 3CO01 brief there is a task about external factors: the things happening outside an organisation that shape what happens inside it. For learners new to business studies this can feel like being asked to explain the entire world economy, and the temptation is to write vague sentences about how everything is always changing. Markers see a lot of those sentences, and they earn very little.

The good news is that external factors are the most learnable part of 3CO01, Business, culture and change in context. There is a simple checklist for finding them, a simple formula for writing about them, and everyday examples all around you. This guide covers all three, so you can turn the scariest-looking task in the unit into the one you feel most confident about.

## What external factors are, and why organisations watch them

An external factor is anything outside an organisation's control that affects how it operates: the state of the economy, new laws, changing customer habits, new technology, the weather turning against a whole industry. Organisations cannot stop these things happening. What they can do is notice them early and adjust, which is why watching the outside world is a survival skill, not an academic exercise.

Think about a high-street cafe chain. It does not control interest rates, the minimum wage, the popularity of oat milk, delivery apps, food hygiene law or public concern about disposable cups. Yet every one of those shapes its prices, its menu, its staffing and its costs. An organisation that ignores the outside world gets surprised by it, and surprises in business are rarely pleasant. That is the whole argument for scanning external factors, and stating it clearly is often worth marks in itself.

## PESTLE: a checklist, not a theory

PESTLE is the standard tool for scanning external factors, and the most important thing to understand is how modest it is. It is not a theory you need to critique; it is a checklist of six types of factor, one per letter, that stops you forgetting a category. Here is each letter in plain English.

### Political

Political factors are about what governments do and might do: policy directions, public spending, trade rules, political stability. A delivery firm watching a government debate about the rights of gig workers is watching a political factor, because the outcome could change how it engages its couriers.

### Economic

Economic factors are about money conditions: inflation, interest rates, wage levels, how confident customers feel about spending. A cafe chain facing higher coffee bean and energy prices is dealing with an economic factor, and so is one noticing customers trading down from lattes to filter coffee.

### Social

Social factors are about people and how they live: population changes, lifestyle shifts, attitudes and expectations. Customers wanting healthier menus, workers expecting flexible hours, more people shopping from their sofas: all social factors. They move slowly, then suddenly, which is why organisations track them.

### Technological

Technological factors are about new tools and systems changing what is possible: apps, automation, online platforms, data. A delivery firm exists because of technological change, and it is also exposed to it, since better route-planning software at a rival is a threat.

### Legal

Legal factors are about what the law requires: employment rules, health and safety, data protection, consumer rights. When the minimum wage rises, every employer of lower-paid staff feels a legal factor land directly on its payroll. At Level 3 you only need to mention legislation in general terms, so name the area of law and its effect rather than reciting sections and dates.

### Environmental

Environmental factors are about the natural world and sustainability: climate, weather, waste, energy, and public expectations about all of them. A cafe chain moving away from disposable cups because customers expect it is responding to an environmental factor, and paying real money to do so.

## How external trends reach the people function

3CO01 is a people-practice unit, so the examiners care less about the factors themselves than about what they mean for people at work. Every external trend eventually lands on the people function somewhere, and the four most useful landing zones to write about are hiring, skills, wellbeing and flexible working.

Hiring feels external pressure first: a strong economy makes candidates scarce and pushes wages up, while a downturn floods every vacancy with applicants. Skills come next, because technological change constantly creates demand for abilities the current workforce does not yet have, and someone has to plan the training. Wellbeing reflects the outside world too, since cost-of-living pressure, job insecurity and constant change all follow people through the door each morning. And flexible working sits where social and technological trends meet: the technology that makes remote work possible and the social expectation that employers offer it arrived together, and people teams have been redesigning policies ever since. If you can trace one external factor into one of these four zones, you have the core of a strong answer.

## Using examples well: organisation, factor, effect

The difference between a weak answer and a strong one in this unit is almost always specificity, and there is a formula for it: specific organisation, specific factor, specific effect. Name an organisation, real or invented. Name the factor and its PESTLE type. State what the factor actually does to the organisation and its people. Every sentence built on that frame earns its place; most sentences without it are filler.

You do not need insider knowledge to do this. Any organisation you have worked for, shopped at or can plausibly invent will do, provided you keep it consistent and concrete. One well-developed example carried through an answer is worth more than five organisations mentioned once each.

## A method for answering an external-factors task

1. Read the task and note exactly how many factors it asks for and what the command verb is; explain wants what, why and effect, describe wants a clear account.
2. Choose one organisation to anchor the whole answer, real or invented, and say in one sentence what it does.
3. Run down the PESTLE checklist and pick the letters where you can name a genuinely relevant factor for that organisation, rather than forcing all six.
4. For each chosen factor, write the formula out: the factor, its type, and its specific effect on the organisation.
5. Extend at least one effect into the people function: what it means for hiring, skills, wellbeing or flexible working.
6. Reread against the criteria in your brief, checking you have answered the question asked, then check your citations for any sources you used.

## A worked example: weak versus specific

Here is the difference in practice, using Beanhill Coffee, an invented cafe chain. First, the weak version. Many external factors affect organisations like Beanhill Coffee. The economy is always changing and technology is developing quickly, so the company must keep adapting to survive in a fast-moving world. Those sentences are grammatical, vaguely true and worth almost nothing, because no actual factor, effect or person appears in them.

Now the specific version. Rising coffee and energy prices are an economic factor affecting Beanhill Coffee, an invented cafe chain. Higher costs squeeze its margins, so the chain has held back on hiring and asked existing baristas to cover more shifts, which its people team is monitoring because of the risk of burnout and rising turnover. Same topic, same length, but this version names the organisation, the factor, the PESTLE type and a chain of effects that reaches real people. That is the standard to aim at, and it is a standard of clarity, not cleverness.

## Where technology and change fit in

Your brief will also ask about change and technology, and the external picture is what connects them. Most organisational change is a response to an external factor: the supermarket launches online ordering because shopping habits shifted, the delivery firm adopts new software because a rival did. Technology plays a double role, appearing as an external trend organisations must respond to and as the internal change people then have to live through, learning new systems and sometimes new jobs. If you show that chain in an answer, from outside trend to internal change to effect on people, you are demonstrating exactly the awareness this unit is designed to test.

## Common pitfalls

The classic pitfall is listing factors without examples: naming all six PESTLE letters with a textbook definition of each and no organisation in sight. The second is giving examples with no effect stated, where a factor is named but the answer never says what it actually does to the organisation or its people. The third is treating PESTLE as the answer instead of a tool, spending precious words describing the framework itself when the marks sit in what the framework finds. A fourth, quieter pitfall is inventing dramatic statistics to sound authoritative; never do this, because a plain principle stated honestly reads far better than a made-up figure.

> Tutor tip: after drafting an external-factors answer, underline every organisation name, factor and effect. If any paragraph has no underlining, it is filler; replace it with organisation, factor, effect.
