Here are answers to the questions learners ask most often about 7HR02, Resourcing and talent management to sustain success, the Level 7 unit that takes a strategic, critical view of workforce and succession planning, talent strategies and their impact, resourcing in a global and changing context, and the measurement of resourcing effectiveness. Its assessment rewards critical evaluation supported by evidence over description of practice, and most of the questions below come back to that in one way or another. The answers give you principles and method, but task formats and requirements vary between study centres, so always check the details against your own current brief and your centre's guidance.

### What is 7HR02 about?

7HR02 sits within the Level 7 Advanced Diploma in Strategic People Management and examines resourcing and talent management from a strategic standpoint: how organisations plan their workforces and successions, what talent strategies actually change, how resourcing works in a global and changing environment, and how its effectiveness can be evaluated and measured. The through-line is the unit's title claim, that resourcing and talent management sustain success, treated not as a fact but as a proposition to be tested.

The standard is postgraduate throughout. Wider reading and critical evaluation are essential, description of tools and cycles earns little, and the marks sit in weighing evidence, surfacing assumptions and reaching justified judgements about questions on which serious commentators disagree.

### How is 7HR02 different from 5HR02?

5HR02 teaches the mechanics: the workforce planning cycle, resourcing techniques, the practical toolkit. 7HR02 assumes the mechanics and assesses something different: whether and when these strategies sustain organisational success, and how anyone would know. The same topic that asked you to explain demand forecasting at Level 5 now asks you to evaluate whether demand can be forecast at all in a volatile environment.

If your instinct on a task is to explain how something works, you are answering the Level 5 question. The Level 7 question is always some version of: does it work, under what conditions, at what cost, and compared with what?

### What is the exclusive versus inclusive talent debate?

The exclusive position treats talent as a select group: a minority of high-potential people who disproportionately drive performance and deserve disproportionate investment. The inclusive position treats talent as everyone, arguing that capability is broadly distributed and that success comes from developing the whole workforce. Each rests on assumptions the other denies, about whether performance is concentrated, whether potential can be identified in advance, and what differential treatment does to the majority who are not chosen.

Markers do not want you to pick the right side; there is not one. They want you to surface the assumptions, weigh what each position costs and delivers in a specific organisational context, and commit to a reasoned judgement rather than a diplomatic shrug.

### Is workforce planning still realistic in uncertain times?

This is one of the unit's live debates, and either conclusion can score well if argued. The case against says long-range forecasts are false precision, and that volatile environments make detailed workforce plans a comforting ritual. The case for says the alternative to planning is drift, and that planning's real product is preparedness rather than prediction.

The strongest answers usually reframe rather than pick: under uncertainty, planning shifts from single forecasts to scenarios, and from headcounts to capabilities the organisation can redeploy. Arguing when each posture fits, and what each costs, is exactly the evaluative register the unit rewards.

### How do I critique tools like the nine-box grid without dismissing them?

Treat the tool as an artefact with a job, assumptions and failure modes. The nine-box grid is widely used because it forces structured conversations about performance and potential; it is widely criticised because its ratings are subjective, potential is hard to observe, and the grid can harden provisional judgements into labels that follow people around. Both halves belong in your answer.

The evaluative move is conditional judgement: state what the tool assumes, when those assumptions hold well enough for it to be useful, and what safeguards its known weaknesses require. Dismissal and endorsement are both descriptive positions; a defended when and how is analysis.

### How do I handle the global dimension?

Anchor it in the standardise versus localise tension. Global organisations must choose how far to run one consistent resourcing approach worldwide, gaining coherence, mobility and a single employer brand, and how far to localise, respecting labour markets, institutions and cultures that genuinely differ. Every global talent strategy is a position on that line, and your task is to evaluate the trade-off in context rather than assert a best practice.

Ground the changing-context material in citable evidence. Labour market data from sources such as the ONS, the OECD and the ILO can support claims about workforce trends in general terms; invented or half-remembered statistics can only damage an answer.

### What does measuring resourcing effectiveness involve?

First it involves deciding what effectiveness means, which is harder than it sounds: speed and cost of hiring, quality and retention of hires, internal fill rates, workforce capability, or contribution to organisational performance are different definitions that can point in different directions. A strong answer makes its definition explicit before reaching for metrics.

Then it involves honesty about attribution. Organisational success has many causes, and showing that a talent strategy caused any of it is genuinely difficult; firms that fund talent programmes are often successful already, which muddies the causal water further. Markers reward answers that measure what can be measured, acknowledge what cannot, and refuse to dress correlation up as impact.

### Why do people get referred on 7HR02?

Four patterns dominate. Describing planning cycles and tools instead of evaluating them. Treating talent management as self-evidently valuable, so the central claim of the unit is assumed rather than argued. Tool-worship, where grids and models are cited as authorities that settle questions rather than artefacts to be judged. And ignoring volatility, so plans and strategies are discussed as if the environment holds still.

None of these is a knowledge problem, which is why referred learners are often surprised. They are register problems, and they are fixable: the same material, reworked so that claims are tested, tools are judged and uncertainty is faced, usually contains everything a passing answer needs.

### Can I use my own organisation in my answers?

Usually yes, if your brief allows it, either as illustration or as the subject of analysis. Applying the unit's questions to a resourcing strategy you know from the inside can produce exactly the contextual, evidence-aware evaluation the unit wants, and anonymising sensibly is straightforward.

The risk is loyalty. Evaluating your employer's talent strategy in the language of its own slide decks reproduces the descriptive default the unit penalises, so be prepared to ask the uncomfortable questions: whether the strategy demonstrably sustains success, what it assumes, and what evidence would reveal it failing.

### How do I handle the word count?

Word counts and tolerances are set by your centre, so work from your own brief rather than any figure you find online, and check how reference lists, tables and appendices are treated. Allocate words across tasks in proportion to their weight before you draft.

If you are over the limit, cut description first: explanations of how cycles and tools work, background on the organisation, and scene-setting. The marks live in evaluation, evidence and judgement, and trimming description to protect argument almost always improves a 7HR02 answer anyway.
