If your CIPD assignment has just come back referred, you are probably facing an awkward puzzle. The feedback says sections need more analysis, more evidence or more application, yet your draft is already at or near the word limit. You cannot simply write more, because the limit has not grown just because you have more to say. This is one of the most common practical problems in a resubmission, and it catches out learners at every level.

The good news is that it has a clean solution. A referred assignment almost never needs more words overall; it needs the same words spent differently. Learning to audit where your current draft spends its allowance, cut what earns nothing, and reinvest the freed space in the referred sections is a repeatable skill, and this guide walks you through it step by step. A referral is a normal, fixable part of CIPD study, and the word count is a constraint you can work with rather than against.

## In this guide you'll learn

- Why resubmissions fail again when new material is bolted onto a full draft
- How to audit where your current words are actually spent, task by task
- What to cut first, and how to turn description into analysis without adding length
- A step-by-step word audit method with a worked example

## Why bolting on more words fails

The instinct after a referral is to add. The feedback says a section is thin, so you write two more paragraphs and squeeze them in. The problem is that if your draft was already at the limit, those paragraphs push you over, and if you trim randomly to compensate, you risk weakening sections that passed. Worse, adding without removing usually means the new material sits on top of the old description rather than replacing it, so the marker reads the same descriptive passage as before with a small analytical afterthought attached.

Assessors marking a resubmission are looking at whether the criteria that were not met are now clearly met. They are not counting how much new text you added. A section that was descriptive and is now descriptive plus a little more is still likely to fall short. The winning move is substitution, not addition: weaker material comes out, stronger material goes in, and the total stays inside the limit.

There is also a quality cost to an overstuffed draft. Dense, hurried additions tend to read as exactly what they are, and they can bury the improvement the assessor is looking for in the middle of unchanged text. Space is part of what makes analysis visible.

## Audit where your words are actually going

Before you cut or add anything, find out how your current allowance is spent. Most learners have never measured this, and the results are usually surprising. Paste each task or section into a separate document and note its word count. Then set those counts against two things: the weighting or expected length the brief suggests for each task, and the feedback you received.

The pattern you are looking for is mismatch. A task that carries several criteria but received a small share of the words is underfed. A long introduction, a scene-setting section or a task that passed comfortably while consuming far more than its share is overfed. Referred sections that are already long have a different problem: the words are there but they are doing the wrong job, which is where converting description into analysis comes in.

This audit takes perhaps half an hour and transforms the revision from guesswork into a plan. You will know exactly how many words you need to find and exactly where they are hiding.

## What to cut first

Not all words earn equally. When you need to free space, cut in this order, because these categories give up the most length for the least loss of marks.

- Over-long introductions and conclusions: a task answer rarely needs more than a sentence or two of framing before it gets to the point.
- Repeated organisational context: describe your organisation or case study once, briefly, and refer back to it rather than reintroducing it in every task.
- Pure description: passages that summarise what a model or policy is, at length, without applying or evaluating it.
- Long direct quotes: a quote of several lines can usually become a short paraphrase with a citation, saving dozens of words each time.
- Generic theory summaries: markers know the textbook content; they want to see what you do with it, so compress the summary and expand the application.
- Throat-clearing phrases: constructions like 'it is important to note that' or 'in today's fast-paced business environment' can go entirely.

Cut from sections that passed only when they are clearly overweight, and even then trim padding rather than substance. The aim is to protect what worked while harvesting words from material that was never earning marks in the first place.

## Turning description into analysis at the same length

Here is the most useful discovery in resubmission editing: analysis does not need more words than description, it needs different ones. A hundred words describing a reward model and a hundred words comparing that model against an alternative and judging its fit for a specific organisation occupy the same space, but only the second earns analytical credit.

The conversion works sentence by sentence. Where a sentence states what something is, rewrite it to say what it means, what it compares to, what its limitation is, or what follows from it. Words like because, however, whereas, which suggests and therefore are the connective tissue of analysis, and they cost almost nothing. You are not lengthening the passage; you are upgrading it in place.

This matters because referred sections are often already near their sensible share of the count. If the feedback says a section is descriptive rather than analytical, the fix is usually rewriting what is there, not appending new paragraphs after it.

## Reallocating the freed words

Once the cuts have created headroom, spend it deliberately on the referred criteria. Go back to the feedback, list each criterion marked as not met, and decide what each one needs: a comparative paragraph, a workplace example, a supported justification, an evaluation of limitations. Give each addition a word budget before you write it, so the reallocation stays controlled.

Keep an eye on the tolerance rules as you work. Most centres set a word limit with a tolerance band around it, but the size of that band, what counts towards it and how strictly it is applied are all set by your study centre, so check your brief and your centre's guidance rather than relying on any general figure. Aim to land comfortably inside the stated limit so that tolerance never has to do any work for you.

## A step-by-step word audit method

1. Confirm the rules: reread your brief for the overall limit, the tolerance policy and whether elements such as tables, references or appendices are excluded from the count.
2. Measure the current state: record the word count of each task or section in a simple table.
3. Map the marks: against each section, note which criteria it serves and which of those the feedback flagged as not met.
4. Spot the mismatches: mark sections that are overweight relative to their importance and referred sections that are underfed or misdirected.
5. Harvest words: work through the cut list above, section by section, recording how many words each pass frees.
6. Budget the additions: allocate the freed words to specific fixes for specific referred criteria, in writing, before drafting anything.
7. Rewrite in place: convert descriptive passages in referred sections into analysis at roughly equal length.
8. Remeasure and check: total the revised draft, confirm it sits inside the limit, and read each referred section once more asking only whether the flagged criterion is now clearly met.

## A worked example: Priya's referred task

Priya is a fictional Level 5 learner whose assignment came back referred on one task, with feedback that her answer was descriptive and did not fully apply the material to an organisation. Her overall draft sat just under the limit of 3,900 words, so she had almost no headroom, and her referred task held 700 words while a passed, lightly weighted task held 1,100.

Her audit found the words quickly. The overweight task opened with 160 words of organisational background that was already covered in her introduction, and it contained a 90-word direct quote that she replaced with a 20-word paraphrase and citation. Trimming padding phrases across the whole draft freed another 120 words. In total she harvested around 350 words without touching any passage that was earning marks.

She then spent that budget on the referred task: a 150-word paragraph applying the framework to her named organisation's specific context, a 120-word comparison weighing it against an alternative approach, and a supported justification for her recommendation. Inside the referred task she also rewrote her existing descriptive summary as evaluation at the same length. The resubmission came in 40 words under the limit, and every change pointed directly at the criteria the feedback had named.

## Common pitfalls

- Cutting from strong sections first because they feel safest to touch, which weakens what already passed.
- Adding new material before removing anything, then panic-trimming at the end with no plan.
- Treating the tolerance band as free extra space rather than a safety margin.
- Deleting the evidence and examples that earned marks while leaving padding untouched.
- Forgetting to check what your centre includes in the count, and trimming body text while a long-winded appendix was never counted anyway.
- Judging progress by words added rather than by criteria addressed.

> Tutor tip: before you write anything new, spend one session only cutting. Learners who separate the harvesting pass from the writing pass almost always end up with a tighter, stronger resubmission than those who try to do both at once.
