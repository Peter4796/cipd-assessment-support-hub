Headings get the attention when learners think about structure, but headings only organise the shelves. Paragraphs are what sit on them, and in practice the paragraph is where a CIPD assignment is actually marked. An assessor scans your work paragraph by paragraph, asking of each one: what is this saying, which criterion does it serve, and does it reach the depth the command verb demands? A paragraph that answers cleanly collects its credit in seconds. A paragraph doing three jobs at once makes the assessor work, and material that makes the assessor work is material at risk of going uncredited.

Strong paragraphs follow a small number of learnable habits. This guide covers the shape reliable paragraphs share, one idea per paragraph, practical length, openings and links that make your argument visible, how paragraph shapes shift across the levels, and a method for rebuilding a paragraph that has grown out of control, with a worked example.

## Why paragraphs are the unit of marking in practice

Assessment criteria are met or missed in specific places, and those places are paragraphs. When an assessor records that you have analysed something, they are pointing at the paragraphs where the analysis happened. This is why two submissions with identical knowledge can score differently: in one, each paragraph makes a single findable contribution; in the other, the same ideas are smeared across paragraphs that each hold fragments of several points, and no single place ever completes the job.

## The shape of a reliable paragraph

Strong analytical paragraphs across CIPD work share a four-part shape, best described in plain terms. First comes the point: one sentence stating the claim this paragraph exists to make. Then the explanation: a sentence or two unpacking the point and giving the reasoning behind it. Then evidence or an example: a cited source, a workplace illustration, or both, showing the claim holds outside your own head. Finally the link: a sentence tying the point back to the question, so the paragraph visibly serves the task rather than floating free.

Writing guides often wrap this pattern in an acronym, and the acronym does not matter. What matters is the logic: claim, reasoning, backing, relevance. A paragraph missing the first has no point to credit; missing the middle two, it is an assertion; missing the last, it leaves the assessor guessing why it is there. When a paragraph feels weak, check it against those four jobs; one will usually be absent.

## One idea per paragraph

The discipline that keeps the shape working is one idea per paragraph. Not one topic, which is looser than it sounds, but one claim: a paragraph about reward can still be carrying three separate ideas about reward, and usually is when it runs long. The test is to ask what the paragraph argues and insist on a one-sentence answer. If your honest answer contains and twice, you are looking at three paragraphs wearing one paragraph's clothing.

Overloaded paragraphs happen because the ideas feel too connected to separate. Separating them breaks nothing: the connection survives in the linking sentences, where it becomes visible reasoning. Three clean paragraphs joined by clear links read as an argument; one dense paragraph holding the same material reads as notes.

## How long is a paragraph?

Most working paragraphs in CIPD assignments run between roughly four and eight sentences, often around a hundred to a hundred and fifty words. That is what the four-part shape naturally produces when it carries one idea, and length is a symptom to read, not a target to hit. A much shorter paragraph has usually skipped its explanation or its evidence and stands as a bare assertion. A much longer one has usually picked up a second idea, and the fix is to find the join and split it.

## Open with a point, not a topic

The opening sentence is the paragraph's contract, and the most common weakness is opening with a topic announcement rather than a point. There are many factors that affect employee motivation is an announcement: it names the subject but claims nothing, and the paragraph that follows can go anywhere. Line managers shape motivation more directly than any formal scheme because they control daily recognition is a point: it commits to a claim, and the rest of the paragraph has a defined job.

A quick test is whether the sentence could be disagreed with: announcements cannot, because they say nothing, while points can, which is exactly what makes them worth evidencing and worth writing.

> Tutor tip: read only the first sentence of every paragraph in your draft, in order. If that skeleton summarises your whole argument, your structure is working. If it reads as a list of topics, your points are hiding mid-paragraph where a skim will not find them.

## Linking paragraphs: connectives that show reasoning

Paragraphs join into an argument through their transitions, and the strongest transitions are connectives that expose the logical relationship between one point and the next. Because and therefore signal consequence. However signals a turn: the previous point is now qualified or countered. Whereas signals contrast held up for comparison. These words do real work, because they tell the assessor what kind of move your argument is making.

Additionally, furthermore and moreover, by contrast, are the connectives of a list. They reveal nothing about how the ideas relate, and an argument stitched entirely from them reads as separate observations. If you cannot replace an additionally with a connective of reasoning, ask whether the new paragraph genuinely builds on the last or has simply been placed after it.

## Paragraphs at each level

The four-part shape holds across the qualification, but the weight inside it shifts with the command verbs at each level.

Explanation paragraphs, the staple of Level 3, spend most of their length on the explanation itself: the point states what something is or why it happens, the middle makes it genuinely clear, and the example grounds it. Accuracy and clarity are the job, not depth of judgement.

Application paragraphs, central at Level 5, shift the weight to the example: the point connects a concept to a context, and the bulk of the paragraph shows the concept operating in a specific workplace situation.

Evaluation paragraphs, dominant at Level 7 and expected at Level 5, put the weight on reasoning about worth: the point takes a position, the middle weighs strengths against limitations with evidence on both sides, and the link lands a judgement. These are the longest paragraphs, because judgement needs room, and they are the ones most damaged by sharing space with a second idea.

These are general patterns: your current brief and your centre's guidance define what your unit requires, so let them settle any doubt.

## Rebuilding a rambling paragraph, step by step

1. Write down, away from the draft, a one-sentence answer to what is this paragraph arguing; if the answer needs and, write each claim separately.
2. Give each claim its own point sentence, phrased so it could be challenged.
3. Sort the original paragraph's sentences under those points, and delete anything that serves none of them.
4. Rebuild each paragraph in the four-part shape: point, explanation, evidence or example, link back to the task.
5. Join the new paragraphs with a connective that shows their real relationship: consequence, contrast, qualification or accumulation.
6. Reread only the opening sentences to check the sequence still makes the argument on its own.

## Worked example: one overloaded paragraph, rebuilt

The example below is entirely fictional. Tomas is an invented people adviser at Harlow Dean, a made-up retail chain, drafting a Level 5 answer on hybrid working. His overloaded draft paragraph reads like this. Hybrid working means employees split their time between home and a workplace, and it has become common in many organisations, and at Harlow Dean staff value it because it cuts commuting, although some managers worry that performance is harder to see, and it can also affect wellbeing because remote days can be isolating, so the organisation should offer wellbeing resources and train managers, and communication also matters.

Everything in that paragraph is defensible, and none of it lands, because a definition, an analysis and two recommendations are fighting for the same space. Asked what the paragraph argues, Tomas needs three sentences, which is the signal to split. His rebuilt version makes two paragraphs.

The first carries the analytical idea. Hybrid working changes what managers can directly observe, and that visibility gap, rather than location itself, is its main performance challenge. When output happens away from the workplace, managers who rely on visible effort lose their usual evidence and may default to assuming the worst, a tendency Harlow Dean's own engagement feedback reflected in comments about trust on remote days. Therefore the organisation's hybrid arrangements will stand or fall on how performance is defined and measured, not on where the work is done.

The second carries the application. Because the challenge is managerial rather than locational, Harlow Dean's practical priority is capability, not policy detail. Training managers to set clear outputs and run structured check-ins would address the visibility gap directly, whereas adding further rules about office days would treat the symptom. Wellbeing resources for isolated remote workers then complete the picture, protecting the benefit that made hybrid working valued in the first place.

The rebuilt pair says nothing the original did not contain, but each idea now has a point sentence, a body that builds it, and a link that lands it. An assessor can now credit an analysis paragraph and an application paragraph where before there was one doing neither visibly.

## Common pitfalls

- Opening paragraphs with topic announcements, so the points sit buried mid-paragraph where a skim never finds them
- Paragraphs carrying two or three ideas because the ideas feel too connected to separate
- Assertion paragraphs: a point stated, then restated, with no evidence ever arriving
- Evidence dropped in without a claim to back, so the assessor must infer the point
- Every paragraph joined with additionally or furthermore, hiding the reasoning that connectives should reveal
