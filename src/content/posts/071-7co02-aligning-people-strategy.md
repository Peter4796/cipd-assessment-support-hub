Alignment is the word that appears in almost every 7CO02 submission and does almost no work in the weaker ones. The people strategy must align with the business strategy: every learner writes it, most briefs invite it, and markers read it dozens of times without seeing anyone say what it means, how it happens, or how anyone would know it had been achieved. That gap is where marks are lost, because alignment is not a slogan to be repeated in this unit; it is the central idea to be unpacked, tested and evaluated.

This guide does the unpacking. It sets out what alignment actually involves, works through the theoretical debate that gives you the tools to evaluate it, confronts the measurement problem that most submissions dodge, and shows the whole approach applied to a fictional organisation. If you can write about alignment at the level this guide describes, you are writing at the level 7CO02 rewards.

## What alignment actually means

Strip the slogan away and alignment has two dimensions. Vertical fit is the relationship between the people strategy and the business strategy: the practices, investments and priorities of the people function should support what the organisation is trying to achieve in its market. A cost-focused operator and a premium innovator need different resourcing, reward and development choices, and vertical fit is the discipline of making those choices deliberately rather than by habit or fashion.

Horizontal fit is the relationship between people practices themselves. Practices can undermine each other: a development strategy built on long-term capability sits badly with a reward system that pays only for short-term individual output, and a stated commitment to teamworking is contradicted by promotion criteria that reward solo performance. Horizontal fit asks whether the bundle of practices pushes in one direction or quietly pulls in several. Weak submissions mention vertical fit only; strong ones examine both, because a people strategy can point at the business strategy perfectly and still fail through internal contradiction.

## Best fit versus best practice: the organising debate

The claim that people strategy should fit business strategy is itself a position in a long-running argument, and 7CO02 expects you to know both sides. The best-fit school holds that practices create value only when matched to strategy and context, which is the logic behind vertical fit. Its weakness is practical and conceptual: organisations rarely have one stable, clearly articulated strategy to fit to, strategies change faster than embedded people practices can, and a workforce managed strictly according to today's strategy may lack exactly the capabilities tomorrow's strategy needs.

The best-practice school argues the opposite: some ways of managing people, done well, raise performance almost anywhere, so chasing fit misses the point. Its weakness is the evidence and the logic of advantage. The research behind universal claims is contested, results differ across sectors and countries in ways a universal theory struggles to explain, and a practice every competitor can adopt cannot be a source of lasting advantage for any of them. For your assignment, the debate is not a quiz question with a right answer; it is a pair of lenses, and the analytical move markers reward is using each lens to expose what the other misses.

## The resource-based view: a third lens

The resource-based view of the firm reframes the question. Instead of asking whether practices fit the strategy or follow universal prescriptions, it asks what the organisation has that competitors cannot easily buy or copy, and argues that sustained advantage comes from such resources and capabilities. People are central to this argument, because capabilities built through culture, accumulated know-how and long development are among the hardest things for rivals to replicate quickly.

For alignment writing, the resource-based view does something subtle: it partially reverses the direction of fit. Under pure best fit, people strategy follows business strategy. Under the resource-based view, distinctive people capabilities can shape what business strategies are available at all, which means alignment is a two-way conversation rather than a one-way instruction. A submission that notices this earns credibility, because it shows you understand alignment as a live strategic question rather than an organisational chart exercise.

## How to evaluate an organisation's alignment

1. Establish the business strategy first, in plain terms: how the organisation competes, what its market is doing, and what capabilities its strategy depends on. If you cannot state this, you cannot evaluate fit against it.
2. Map the espoused people strategy: what the organisation says its people priorities are, in strategy documents, plans and public commitments.
3. Test vertical fit: for each major people practice, ask what business outcome it is supposed to serve, and whether the connection is designed or merely asserted.
4. Test horizontal fit: look for contradictions between practices, especially between reward and everything else, because pay systems reveal what an organisation actually values.
5. Look for the gap between espoused and enacted: policies describe intentions, but resourcing decisions, promotion patterns and management behaviour show what is really happening.
6. Ask the resource-based question: is anything here building a capability competitors would find hard to copy, or is the organisation assembling the same practices as everyone else?
7. Judge, with limits: conclude how well aligned the strategy is, where the weak points sit, and what evidence would strengthen or overturn your assessment.

The evidence to look for is mostly ordinary: turnover and stability patterns in strategically critical roles, where training budgets actually go, which roles are hardest to fill, what gets measured in performance reviews, and what leaders spend money on when budgets tighten. Alignment shows up in these unglamorous details far more reliably than in strategy documents.

## The measurement problem

Suppose an organisation implements a people strategy and performance improves. Did the strategy cause the improvement? This is the hardest question in the unit, and pretending it is easy is one of the fastest ways to lose marks. Performance has many drivers, most of them outside the people function's control: markets move, competitors stumble, prices shift, leadership changes. Attributing a performance change to a people strategy requires disentangling it from everything else that changed at the same time, and in a single organisation that is close to impossible to do conclusively.

A masters-level answer responds with method rather than despair. It distinguishes leading indicators, such as engagement measures, capability assessments, turnover in key roles and internal fill rates, from lagging indicators, such as productivity, quality and financial results, and argues that a credible evaluation tracks the chain between them rather than leaping from initiative to profit. It uses comparisons where they exist, between sites, units or periods, while acknowledging their imperfections. And it is honest that the result will be a reasoned judgement about probable contribution, not proof. That honesty is not weakness; it is exactly the evidence-awareness the unit is designed to assess.

> Integrity note: it can be tempting to strengthen an assignment by quoting precise performance figures from your organisation from memory, or by borrowing impressive-sounding statistics found online without a verifiable source. Do neither. Markers reward careful reasoning about imperfect evidence far more than confident numbers that cannot be checked, and unverifiable figures put your academic integrity at risk.

## A worked example: Harwick Fenn

Harwick Fenn is a fictional professional services firm invented for this example. Its business strategy is moving upmarket: fewer, larger clients, more advisory work, premium fees. The board asks whether the people strategy supports the shift.

Applying the vertical fit lens, the answer starts well: the firm has raised graduate entry standards and built an advisory skills academy, both pointing at the new strategy. The horizontal fit lens finds the contradiction: partner reward is still driven overwhelmingly by short-term billing, which pushes senior staff to protect exactly the high-volume transactional work the strategy is trying to leave behind, and the academy's graduates keep being pulled onto that work because it pays the bills this quarter. Espoused and enacted strategy have come apart, and the reward system is the fault line.

The resource-based lens sharpens the recommendation. The firm's scarce, hard-to-copy resource is a small group of advisers with deep client trust in the target market; rivals can match salaries but not relationships built over years. The people strategy should therefore protect and grow that capability deliberately: succession around key advisers, paired staffing so trust transfers, and reward that recognises capability-building alongside billing. On measurement, the evaluation proposes leading indicators, such as the mix of advisory hours, retention of the key adviser group and internal progression into it, ahead of the lagging indicator of premium fee income, with the honest caveat that a strengthening market could flatter the results and a weakening one obscure them. The conclusion is a judgement: partial alignment, one identified fault line, a capability-led repair, and a stated way of knowing in two years whether it worked.

## Common pitfalls

Three pitfalls recur in alignment writing. The first is treating alignment as automatic: assuming that because a people strategy document mentions the business strategy, fit exists, when the enacted evidence may say otherwise. The second is treating alignment as one-way: people strategy obediently following business strategy, with no recognition that capabilities shape strategic options too. The third is treating alignment as self-evidently good: perfect fit to today's strategy can become tomorrow's rigidity, and a workforce optimised for the current market may be exactly the wrong workforce when the market turns. Naming that tension does not weaken your answer; it is the kind of critical qualification that separates Level 7 writing from confident description.
