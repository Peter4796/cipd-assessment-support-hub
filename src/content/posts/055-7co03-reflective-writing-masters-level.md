Reflective writing is where 7CO03 is won and lost. Experienced practitioners can usually describe what they did with fluency and confidence; what markers at Level 7 want is something rarer: an account that questions the practitioner as closely as it recounts the practice. If your tutor feedback says descriptive rather than critical, or asserted rather than evidenced, this guide was written for you, whether you are drafting a first attempt or repairing a referral. A referral on this ground says nothing about your ability and everything about a register you have simply not been asked to use before.

This guide covers what separates masters-level reflection from description and from Level 5 reflection, which models suit senior practitioners, how to evidence something as private as reflection, and how to keep your own voice while writing academically. A worked example shows the difference on the page.

## In this guide you'll learn

## What makes reflection masters level

Think of reflective writing as a ladder with three rungs. Description recounts events: what happened, who said what, how it ended. Reflection adds a personal layer: how you felt, what you learned, what you might do differently. Critical reflection, the masters-level rung, goes further: it questions the assumptions beneath your actions, uses theory and evidence as lenses on your own behaviour, considers how others experienced the same events, and demonstrates that your practice actually changed.

At Level 5, the middle rung is broadly enough: an honest account of what happened and what you learned meets the standard. At Level 7 it is not. Postgraduate reflection is theorised, evidenced and sceptical of itself. It asks not only what did I do and what did I learn, but why did I frame the situation that way in the first place, what was I taking for granted, whose perspective was missing, and what does the literature suggest I could not see from inside the moment.

The most demanding move, and the most rewarded, is questioning your own assumptions. This is the idea at the heart of Mezirow's work on transformative learning: significant learning happens when experience forces you to examine the frames of reference you normally think with, not just the decisions you made inside them. You do not need to reproduce the theory in depth; you need to do the thing it describes, on the page, about yourself.

## Models that suit senior practitioners

Schön's distinction between reflection-in-action and reflection-on-action fits senior work particularly well, because so much of senior practice is thinking on your feet: adjusting an argument mid-meeting, reading a room, changing tack in a negotiation. Writing about how you thought while acting, and then re-examining that thinking afterwards, produces naturally masters-level material.

Kolb's experiential learning cycle suits accounts built around a repeating pattern: concrete experience, reflective observation, abstract conceptualisation, active experimentation. It works well when you can show a genuine loop, where an insight was tested in later practice and the result fed back into your thinking.

Gibbs's reflective cycle is the most structured and the most familiar, and it remains usable at Level 7, with a caution: followed mechanically, it pulls writing towards narrative and feelings and away from criticality. If you use Gibbs, spend most of your words on the analysis and evaluation stages, not the description.

Whichever you choose, one model used deeply beats several used cosmetically, and the model is scaffolding rather than the building. Markers reward the quality of thought, and they also reward a sentence or two acknowledging what your chosen model does not capture.

## Evidencing your reflection

Reflection feels private, but at masters level it must still be evidenced, and there are three reliable sources. The first is feedback received: appraisal comments, 360-degree feedback, a line in an email, even a blunt remark in a corridor. Quoting or closely reporting feedback gives the marker something independent of your self-assessment.

The second is decisions revisited. Show a decision as you saw it at the time, then as you see it now, and account for the gap. The distance between the two readings is visible evidence that reflection has actually happened, rather than being claimed.

The third is measurable change in practice. Something you now do differently, described concretely, with whatever indication of effect you honestly have: a survey result, a pattern in later meetings, a stakeholder's changed behaviour. None of this needs to be dramatic. A small, specific, checkable change beats a grand claim of transformation every time.

## Integrating literature without smothering your voice

The weakest way to use literature is as wallpaper: a paragraph of citations, then an unrelated story. The strongest way is as a lens: a concept brought in at the exact moment it explains, sharpens or challenges something in your own account. One idea, applied precisely to one moment of your practice, is worth more than five names listed in a row.

Let the literature argue with you as well as for you. Where writers disagree, say where your experience lands and why. Where a concept flatters your behaviour, look for the source that would question it. That friction between reading and experience is precisely what criticality looks like in reflective writing, and it keeps your voice at the centre, with theory working for the account rather than burying it.

## First person and academic rigour together

Write in the first person for reflective work: it is expected, and third-person contortions strip out the self-awareness the unit assesses. Rigour does not come from grammatical distance. It comes from evidence beside every claim, reasoning a reader can follow, honest hedging where certainty is not earned, and engagement with sources. I misjudged the timing, and the feedback that followed shows why, is both first person and rigorous. Check your centre's guidance in case it sets specific conventions, but do not mistake formality for quality.

## Building a critically reflective account, step by step

1. Choose an episode with genuine tension, such as a contested restructure, an ethical challenge or an influencing attempt that partly failed. Smooth triumphs give you nothing to interrogate.
2. Write the honest narrative privately first, including the doubt, irritation and uncertainty you would normally edit out. This raw version is your source material, not your submission.
3. Interrogate your assumptions: what did you take for granted, what did you frame as obvious, whose perspective is absent from your account?
4. Bring in two or three sources as lenses, including at least one that challenges rather than confirms your reading of events.
5. Add evidence: feedback received, the decision as seen then and now, and anything observable that changed.
6. State what you now do differently and how you would know if it is working.
7. Edit the description down until every sentence of narrative is earning a sentence of analysis.

## A worked example: leading a restructure consultation

Priya is an invented HR director at Calderbrook Foods, a fictional manufacturer, leading the consultation on restructuring a distribution centre. Here is the same episode written two ways.

A descriptive version reads like this. I led the consultation meetings over four weeks, communicated openly with affected staff, listened carefully to their concerns and worked with union representatives to agree the final structure. The process was difficult at times but ultimately successful, and it taught me the importance of clear communication during organisational change.

A critically reflective version treats the same episode very differently. Rereading my notes from the first consultation meeting, I can see that what I called listening was closer to managed transmission: the structure had already been agreed in outline with the executive team, and my openness had limits I did not acknowledge, even to myself. The union representative's comment that the consultation felt like choreography stung precisely because it was partly accurate. Mezirow's idea that real learning begins when we question our own frames helped me name the assumption I had been working from, that a smooth process and a fair process were the same thing. In the second phase I changed approach, sharing the commercial constraints and the genuinely open options before positions hardened. The pulse survey run after the change, and the same representative's unprompted comment that the later sessions felt different, suggest the shift was noticed. I now build an explicit decisions-not-yet-made section into every consultation plan I write.

Notice the differences. The critical version questions an assumption instead of asserting a virtue, uses theory as a lens at the moment it earns its place, includes evidence a marker can inspect, admits discomfort rather than editing it out, and ends with a concrete, checkable change in practice. It is also still unmistakably a person writing, not a textbook.

## Common pitfalls

- Narrative with a moral: a long story followed by one line of learning, which is description wearing a reflective hat
- The flawless account: nothing questioned, nothing changed, which markers read as a lack of self-awareness rather than excellence
- Theory wallpaper: citations that decorate the account instead of interrogating it
- Borrowed insight: conclusions that sound imported from a textbook rather than earned from the experience described
- Model worship: forcing every experience through every stage of a cycle, producing mechanical writing that buries the thinking

> Integrity note: your reflection must be built on your own lived experience. Inventing episodes, borrowing a colleague's story, or having someone else draft your reflective account defeats the purpose of the unit and breaches academic integrity. Coaching on structure and criticality is legitimate; the experience and the thinking on the page must be genuinely yours.
