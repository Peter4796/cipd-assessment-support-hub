If your 7CO01 feedback says more critical evaluation needed, or your draft reads fluently but you suspect it is describing rather than arguing, this guide is for you. 7CO01, Work and working lives in a changing business environment, assesses one skill harder than any other: the ability to build a critical argument about contested territory. Labour markets, globalisation, technology, ethics and the future of work are all live debates, and the unit wants to watch you argue inside them, not report on them from a safe distance.

The good news is that critical argument is a structure, not a talent. Practitioners who think sharply in meetings often write descriptively in assignments simply because nobody has shown them the shape a written argument takes at postgraduate level. This guide sets out that shape, from the essay level down to the individual paragraph, with a worked example showing the same material written both ways.

## In this guide you'll learn

- The difference between an argument-led essay and a topic survey
- How to build a position: claim, evidence, counterview, response, conclusion
- How to use competing perspectives as the fuel of your argument
- A paragraph-level pattern for critical writing, with a worked example on gig economy work
- The pitfalls that make capable writers sound descriptive

## Argument-led essay versus topic survey

A topic survey answers the question what is known about this. It gathers definitions, trends, frameworks and examples, organises them under tidy headings, and finishes with a summary. Surveys can be accurate, well referenced and completely unrewarded at Level 7, because nothing in them is at stake: remove any paragraph and the piece loses information but no argument, since there was no argument to lose.

An argument-led essay answers the question what should we conclude about this. It opens by taking a position on the question, and every subsequent section either supports that position, tests it against opposition, or refines it. The reader always knows what is being claimed and why the current paragraph matters to the claim. The practical test is simple: if a stranger read only your introduction and conclusion, could they state your position and your main reasons? If not, you have written a survey with a conclusion stapled on.

## Building a position: the five-part skeleton

Every sustained argument, whatever the topic, can be built from five moves. The claim states your position on the question in one clear sentence; a claim you cannot state in one sentence is not yet a position. The evidence gives the reader grounds to accept the claim: academic literature, authoritative data from sources such as the ILO, ONS or OECD, and well-chosen examples. The counterview presents the strongest opposing reading in its best form, not a strawman; markers know the debates and will notice a weak opponent. The response explains why your claim survives the counterview, whether by outweighing it, absorbing it as a qualification, or showing where its evidence is thinner than it looks. The conclusion states what follows if you are right, which for a people management unit usually means implications for organisations, workers or the profession.

You can run this skeleton at essay scale, with each move taking a section, and again at section scale, with each move taking a paragraph or two. Arguments nest. What must never happen is evidence appearing without a claim to serve, or a counterview appearing without a response: orphaned evidence reads as description, and an unanswered counterview reads as a change of mind.

## Competing perspectives are fuel, not obstacles

Learners often treat opposing views as a threat to their argument, mentioning them briefly and moving on. Reverse that instinct: in 7CO01 the competing perspectives are the argument. The unit's core topics come pre-loaded with organised disagreement, and each disagreement is a machine for generating critical writing.

Take automation. The optimist reading holds that technology reshapes work, recomposing jobs around new tasks and raising productivity; the pessimist reading holds that it displaces work, eliminating roles faster than economies replace them and concentrating the gains. Take labour market flexibility. One reading sees opportunity: autonomy for workers, agility for organisations, doors opened for people excluded from standard employment. The other sees precarity: risk transferred from employer to worker, income insecurity, and protections that lag behind the reality of working lives. In each case, your job is not to pick a side and ignore the other, nor to sit on the fence between them. It is to weigh both readings against evidence, decide where the balance falls in the contexts you are discussing, and defend that judgement while conceding what the other side gets right. An argument that concedes something is almost always stronger than one that concedes nothing, because concession shows the weighing actually happened.

## Sources are voices in the argument, not decoration

At postgraduate level, a citation is not proof of reading; it is a voice you have invited into your argument, and every voice must be doing a job. A source can supply your evidence, articulate the counterview, provide the concept you are applying, or challenge a piece of evidence you want to rely on. What a source cannot usefully do is sit in brackets at the end of a descriptive sentence, decorating a point you would have written anyway.

The difference shows in the writing around the citation. Decorative sourcing name-checks: several authors note that gig work is growing. Argumentative sourcing puts voices in relation: it sets one author's reading against another's, tells the reader which you find more persuasive in this context, and says why. As a rule of thumb, if two of your sources never disagree with each other anywhere in the essay, you have gathered a chorus rather than a debate, and a chorus gives you nothing to evaluate.

## Keeping judgement anchored when experts disagree

Contested topics tempt writers to two failures. The first is false balance: presenting every view and endorsing none, which reads as evasion. The second is false certainty: writing as though the debate were settled in your favour, which reads as naivety. The anchored middle path is a committed, qualified judgement: state where you land, specify the contexts and conditions in which your position holds, and be explicit about what evidence would change your mind. On the balance of evidence is not hedging when you then say which way the balance tips and why. Markers reward writers who can hold a position and its limits in the same paragraph, because that is what strategic judgement looks like in professional life too.

## A paragraph-level pattern for critical writing

1. Open with a claim sentence that takes a position, not a topic sentence that names a subject.
2. Bring evidence: literature, data or a concrete example that gives the reader grounds to accept the claim.
3. Introduce the strongest counterview or complication, honestly stated.
4. Respond: show why the claim survives, or refine the claim to absorb what the counterview gets right.
5. Close by linking the paragraph's result to the essay's overall argument, so the reader sees the move it has made.

Not every paragraph needs all five moves, but every paragraph needs the first and the last: a position taken and a contribution made. A run of paragraphs that all open with claims and close with links is very hard to mark as descriptive.

> Tutor tip: highlight the first sentence of every paragraph in your draft, then read only the highlights. If they form a summary of topics, you have a survey. If they form a chain of reasoning you could defend aloud, you have an argument. Fix the first sentences and the paragraphs tend to follow.

## Worked example: the same paragraph, descriptive and argument-led

Here is a fictional example on gig economy work, written both ways. The descriptive version: The gig economy has grown significantly in recent years. Platforms allow workers to choose when and where they work, which many value. However, gig workers often lack sick pay, pension contributions and employment protections. Regulators in several countries have examined the status of platform workers. This shows the gig economy is an important issue for the people profession. Every sentence is defensible, and the paragraph argues nothing: it lists features, gestures at a controversy and ends with an empty flourish.

The argument-led version: For most platform workers, the flexibility of gig work functions less as freedom than as transferred risk, and the people profession should treat it accordingly. The opportunity reading is not empty: the literature on non-standard work records genuine autonomy benefits, and for some workers gig income supplements rather than replaces secure employment. But the precarity reading fits the evidence of dependence better: where platform work is a worker's main income, the absence of sick pay, pension provision and predictable earnings means the worker carries risks an employer would traditionally hold, without the bargaining power to price that risk. The strongest counter, that regulation would destroy the flexibility workers themselves value, assumes flexibility and protection cannot coexist, which existing forms of protected flexible work contradict. On balance, the case for treating dependent gig workers as a protection gap is stronger than the case for the status quo, which is why worker status will keep rising up the strategic agenda for organisations that rely on platforms. The material is the same territory; the difference is a claim defended through evidence, counterview and response, ending with a consequence.

## Common pitfalls

The habits that keep writers descriptive are consistent: opening paragraphs with topic announcements instead of claims, saving all evaluation for a discussion section at the end instead of arguing throughout, quoting sources that all agree, presenting counterviews as strawmen, hedging every judgement into neutrality, and mistaking strong opinion for argument by asserting a position without ever testing it. Watch also for the balanced conclusion that refuses to conclude: weighing arguments is the method, but a judgement is the product, and an essay without one has done the work and declined the marks.
