## What 5HR03 is about

5HR03, Reward for performance and contribution, examines how reward supports performance, motivation and organisational goals. It covers reward strategy, the full range of financial and non-financial reward, and the factors that influence decisions about pay and benefits. The underlying question is simple to state and demanding to answer well: how should an organisation reward its people so that both the people and the organisation get what they need?

The unit treats reward as a set of choices rather than a fixed formula. Every organisation has to decide how to position pay, what to offer beyond salary, whether and how to link reward to performance, and how to keep the whole package fair and affordable. Your job in the assignment is to show you understand those choices, can weigh them against each other, and can argue for one over another in a specific context. That evaluative work, comparing approaches and justifying decisions, is where the higher marks are earned.

This connects naturally to the CIPD Profession Map, which expects people professionals to make considered, principles-led decisions rather than copying whatever other organisations do. Markers want to see that habit of mind on the page.

## How 5HR03 is assessed

5HR03 is assessed through a written assignment marked against the criteria in your brief. Briefs vary by study centre, but most combine explanatory tasks, where you set out concepts such as reward strategy or types of reward, with evaluative tasks, where you compare approaches and justify a recommendation for a given organisation or scenario.

Strong submissions share one feature: they treat reward decisions as arguments to be made, not facts to be listed. The marker can see the options considered, the criteria used to weigh them, the context that makes one option more suitable than another, and the reasoning that leads to the recommendation. Weak submissions describe reward practices in general terms, borrow conclusions without justification, or ignore the specific organisation the task asks about.

Always work from your own current assessment brief and your centre's guidance. Word counts, task formats and the exact wording of the criteria differ between centres and change over time, so treat any general description of the unit, including this one, as a map rather than the territory.

## Financial and non-financial reward, briefly

Financial reward is anything with a direct monetary value to the employee: base pay, bonuses and incentives, overtime, pension contributions, and benefits with a clear cash equivalent such as private medical cover. Non-financial reward is everything else people value about working somewhere: recognition, development opportunities, flexibility, meaningful work, career progression and a healthy working environment.

The two are not rivals. Most modern reward thinking, often described as total reward, treats them as one package, because people respond to the whole deal rather than the payslip alone. An organisation with modest salaries but genuine flexibility and strong development can compete for talent with a higher-paying rival, and the assignment expects you to reason about those trade-offs rather than assuming money always wins.

This is a compressed summary of a rich topic. The dedicated guide Reward Principles in 5HR03, Explained goes much deeper, covering total reward, the main motivation theories and how to apply them without theory-dumping.

## What shapes pay and benefits decisions

Pay decisions are never made in a vacuum, and 5HR03 expects you to show the forces acting on them from inside and outside the organisation.

### Internal factors

Inside the organisation, the biggest influences are strategy, affordability, culture, and fairness. Strategy sets what the organisation is trying to achieve and therefore what behaviour reward should encourage. Affordability sets the ceiling: a reward approach the organisation cannot sustain is not a strategy, it is a liability. Culture shapes what will be accepted, since a highly individualised bonus scheme can corrode a collaborative culture. Fairness and consistency hold the system together, because perceived unfairness in pay does more damage to motivation than modest pay levels ever do.

### External factors

Outside the organisation, pay decisions respond to the labour market, the cost of living, legislation and sector norms. If competitors pay more for the same skills, recruitment and retention suffer. Cost of living pressures change what employees need from pay and how they judge its adequacy. Legislation, such as equal pay and minimum wage requirements described in general terms, sets a legal floor and a fairness framework that no reward approach can ignore. Sector norms shape expectations, because what looks generous in one industry looks mean in another.

Strong answers link these factors to specific decisions. It is not enough to list them; show how a particular factor pushes a particular organisation towards or away from a particular choice.

## How to approach 5HR03, step by step

1. Break down the brief. List every task, note the command verb in each, and separate the explanatory tasks from the evaluative ones. Explain, compare and justify demand different kinds of writing, and answering the wrong verb is one of the quickest ways to lose marks.
2. Map the organisation or scenario your brief gives you. Note its sector, size, goals and any pressures mentioned, because your justifications must connect to this context, not to organisations in general.
3. Gather a small set of credible sources. CIPD publications, respected textbooks on reward and peer-reviewed articles carry weight. A few sources used precisely beat a long list used decoratively.
4. For each reward approach you discuss, work out what it is trying to achieve, what it costs, who it motivates, and where it can go wrong. This gives you ready-made comparison material.
5. Choose one or two motivation theories and apply them to the choices in front of you, rather than summarising every theory you have read. Applied theory earns credit; recited theory does not.
6. Draft with the decision line in view. Each evaluative answer should let a marker trace context, options, comparison criteria, judgement and justification without effort.
7. Check every claim about pay levels, figures or legal rules. If you cannot support a specific claim, state the principle instead and keep the numbers out.
8. Review your draft against the criteria in your brief before submitting, checking each requirement line by line.

> Tutor tip: whenever you name a reward practice in your draft, ask what it is trying to achieve for that organisation and how anyone would know it had worked. If you cannot answer both questions, you are describing, not evaluating.

## A worked example: Harborline Hotels

Here is a fictional scenario that shows the method in action. Harborline Hotels is an invented regional hotel group with twelve sites. It is losing experienced chefs to competitors and its general managers want a response. Two options are on the table: raise chef pay across the board to match the highest-paying rival, or hold pay near the market midpoint and invest in a structured development pathway with recognition for craft skills.

A descriptive answer would define both options and stop, which earns little. An evaluative answer works through the context. Harborline's strategy depends on food quality, so losing chefs threatens the core offer, which raises the urgency. Affordability matters: matching the top payer across twelve sites is a large permanent cost, while a development pathway costs less but takes longer to bite. The labour market for chefs is competitive, which suggests pay cannot be ignored entirely. Fairness matters too, because a large rise for chefs alone may feel inequitable to other staff working alongside them.

A justified recommendation might combine a targeted pay adjustment to remove the worst gaps with the development pathway as the longer-term differentiator, and it would say how success will be measured, for example through retention of chefs over the following year and exit conversations. Notice the shape: context, options, comparison against explicit criteria, a judgement, and a way to test it. That is the shape 5HR03 rewards, whatever your own brief asks.

## Common mistakes to avoid

- Describing reward practices without evaluating them, so the marker sees knowledge but no judgement
- Ignoring the context, so recommendations would fit any organisation and therefore convince for none
- Quoting motivation theories without applying them to the reward choices in the task
- Making claims about pay levels, statistics or legal rules without credible sources
- Treating financial reward as the whole story and dismissing non-financial reward as decoration
- Recommending an approach with no acknowledgement of its costs, risks or downsides

## 5HR03 FAQs

### Do I need to be a pay specialist to pass 5HR03?

No. The unit tests understanding and judgement, not technical reward administration. If you can explain what different reward approaches are for, weigh them against each other, and justify a choice for a context, you have what the assignment needs.

### Can I base my answers on my own organisation?

Often yes, and it can strengthen your application, but follow your brief. Some centres provide a scenario you must use. If you draw on your employer, anonymise sensitive pay information and keep the focus on analysis rather than description.

### Which motivation theories should I use?

Well-known, publicly documented theories such as Herzberg's two-factor theory, Adams' equity theory and Vroom's expectancy theory are safe ground, and one or two applied carefully beat five summarised. The reward principles guide in this series shows how to apply each to reward choices.

### What if my brief looks different from this guide?

Follow your brief every time. Centres word tasks differently and update them regularly. Use this guide for method and understanding, and your own current brief and centre guidance for the exact requirements.
