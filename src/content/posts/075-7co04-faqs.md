Here are answers to the questions learners ask most often about 7CO04, Business research in people practice, the research project of the Level 7 Advanced Diploma in Strategic People Management and usually its most demanding unit. The project asks you to formulate a research question, design and conduct a genuine study, analyse your data and present findings, recommendations and reflection, all to a masters standard. The answers below give you principles and method, but project formats, word counts, milestone dates and ethics processes vary between study centres, so always check the details against your own current brief and your centre's guidance.

### What is 7CO04?

7CO04 is the unit where you stop analysing other people's evidence and produce your own. You choose a live issue in people practice, formulate a research question and aims, review the literature, design a study, collect and analyse data, and present conclusions, evidence-based recommendations and reflection in a single coherent report. It is the diploma's fullest test of the evidence-based practice the CIPD Profession Map expects of senior people professionals.

It is best understood as a project rather than an assignment. Every stage depends on the one before it, other people's time and permissions are involved, and the decisions you make in the first month shape everything that follows.

### How big is 7CO04 compared with other Level 7 units?

Typically it is the largest single piece of work in the qualification, and the difference is as much duration as word count. Other units are mostly under your own control from start to finish; this one includes fieldwork, which depends on participants, gatekeepers and approval processes that run on their own timetables.

Check your brief for the exact requirements, then treat the elapsed time, not the word count, as the real size of the unit. A report you could draft in a few intensive weeks still needs months of question development, approval, data collection and analysis behind it.

### Do I need statistics?

Only if your question calls for them. Plenty of strong 7CO04 projects are fully qualitative, built on interviews or focus groups and analysed thematically, and where numbers do appear the analysis can often stay simple and descriptive. What markers care about is fit and justification: a method chosen because it answers the question, applied competently, beats an ambitious technique applied badly.

Be honest about your skills at the design stage. If you would struggle to run and interpret statistical analysis, do not build a project that depends on it; if you enjoy numbers, do not force interviews on a question that a well-designed survey would answer better.

### Can I research my own organisation?

Usually yes, and most learners do, because access is the hardest practical constraint in the unit and your own organisation is where you have it. Insider knowledge also helps you ask sharper questions and read context that an outsider would miss.

The trade-off is that ethics gets more demanding, not less. You may hold influence over potential participants, colleagues may be identifiable to each other in a small population, and your findings may say uncomfortable things about your employer. You still need genuine, documented organisational permission and approval through your centre's process, so start both conversations early.

### What if I cannot get access to data?

Change the project, not the standards. If the people or records your question needs are out of reach, narrow the question to the access you do have, switch to a different population you can genuinely reach, or consider a design built on secondary data, using information that already exists rather than collecting new material. Your centre can advise on what designs it will accept.

What you must not do is press on hoping access will appear, or quietly lower your evidence standards to fill the gap. Access problems discovered early are a scoping issue; discovered late, they become a crisis, which is why the feasibility of your question should be tested before it is approved.

### Should I go qualitative, quantitative or mixed methods?

Let the question decide. Questions about experience, meaning and how people interpret their situation point to qualitative methods such as interviews and focus groups. Questions about prevalence, patterns and differences across larger groups point to quantitative methods such as surveys or analysis of existing data. Mixed-methods designs combine both, which can strengthen a study but roughly doubles the design and analysis work, so the combination needs to earn its place.

Whichever you choose, the marks sit in the justification. Show why the approach fits your question better than the alternatives, and show you understand the limitations of the choice you made.

### How do research ethics work in practice?

In practice, ethics means resolving a set of concrete questions before any data exists: participants give informed consent, understanding what the study involves and that they can withdraw; anonymity and confidentiality are promised only to the extent you can genuinely deliver them; data is stored, handled and eventually disposed of in line with your obligations under data protection law; and the organisation has formally agreed to host the research. Your centre will have a process, often a formal approval step, and it must be completed before collection begins.

Treat ethics as design rather than paperwork. Questions like whether your role gives you power over participants, or whether individuals could be identified from quotations in a small team, improve the study when you face them early and undermine it when you discover them late.

### Why do 7CO04 projects get referred?

The recurring reasons are sequencing failures: a question too broad to answer within the scope available, methods chosen before the question and then thinly justified, data collected before ethics and permission were properly settled, analysis that describes the data instead of using it to answer the question, and recommendations that float free of the findings. Weak projects also tend to skimp the literature review, so the study never connects to what is already known.

The common thread is that referred projects usually went wrong months before submission. That is worth taking seriously but not fearfully: each failure has a checkpoint earlier in the process, and a project that clears question, ethics and design carefully is already unlikely to be referred.

### How far in advance should I plan?

Plan backwards from the deadline the moment the unit begins, and front-load the stages you do not control. Question development, organisational permission and ethics approval should be moving in the earliest weeks, because fieldwork cannot start until they are done and the approval clock is not yours. Assume data collection will overrun, since participants cancel and surveys need chasing, and protect real time at the end for analysis and writing.

The single most useful planning habit is a margin on every stage that involves other people. Projects rarely fail because the learner wrote slowly; they fail because six weeks of fieldwork was scheduled into a fortnight.

### What does the reflection element involve?

Reflection in 7CO04 is a critical appraisal of the project and of you as its researcher: what worked, what you would design differently, what the honest limitations of your study are, and what the process taught you about evidence, judgement and your own practice. It is assessed work, not a diary entry, so it should be specific and evaluative rather than generally positive.

Strong reflection names real decisions and their consequences, including the imperfect ones. Acknowledging that your sample skewed one way, or that a pilot would have improved your questions, does not weaken the report; it demonstrates exactly the critical self-awareness the unit is designed to develop.
