Most marker feedback on CIPD assignments includes some version of the same comment: this point needs support. The learner has made a sensible claim, the claim may even be right, but nothing on the page shows the marker why it should be believed. Evidence is what turns a plausible opinion into a creditable answer, and using it well is a writing skill in its own right, separate from knowing how to format a citation or where to search for journal articles.

This guide covers that writing skill. It is not about citation mechanics, which CIPD Harvard Referencing: The Complete Guide covers in full, and it is not about tracking sources down, which How to Find Credible Sources for CIPD (Using Google Scholar) handles. It is about what happens on the page: what counts as evidence, how to attach it to claims so the connection earns marks, and the habits that make well-researched work read as unsupported.

## What counts as evidence in a people-practice assignment

Evidence in CIPD work is broader than academic citation. Four kinds appear in strong answers, and each carries a different weight and a different limit.

- Published research and professional sources. Peer-reviewed studies, textbooks, and reports from professional bodies and reputable institutes. The heavyweight category, tested or edited before it reached you, but general by nature: it tells you what tends to be true, not what is true in your organisation.
- Organisational data. Turnover figures, absence patterns, survey results, exit interview themes from a real workplace. Powerful because it is specific, but it needs careful handling: internal data is rarely collected rigorously, and small numbers support smaller conclusions than learners usually draw from them.
- Workplace examples. Something you saw happen: a policy launched, a grievance handled, a change resisted. Vivid and persuasive when analysed, but a single example is an illustration rather than proof, and it should be presented as such.
- Practitioner experience. Your own professional judgement, built up over time. Legitimate in reflective and applied tasks, but weak as sole support for a general claim, because the marker cannot weigh it.

The strongest answers combine them: published research for what is generally known, organisational evidence for what is happening locally, and professional judgement to connect the two.

## The claim, evidence, comment pattern

The basic unit of evidenced writing has three moves. First the claim: a clear statement of what you are arguing. Then the evidence: the research finding, the data or the example that supports it. Then the comment: your own sentence or two saying what the evidence shows, how strongly it shows it, and where its limits lie.

Most learners perform the first two moves and skip the third, and the third is where the marks live. A claim plus a citation proves you read something; the comment proves you understood it and can judge it, which is exactly the analytical behaviour markers reward. It is the same logic that shapes whole answers in How to Structure a CIPD Assignment, applied at the scale of a single point.

## Matching evidence to the strength of the claim

Claims and evidence have to be weighed against each other. A strong claim, that something causes something, that an approach will work, that a practice has failed, needs strong support: multiple credible sources or robust data pointing the same way. If all you have is one study or one workplace anecdote, soften the claim to fit. This suggests, this is consistent with, one explanation may be: these are not weasel words, they are accurate reports of what your evidence can actually carry.

Overclaiming is a quiet marks-loser, because markers notice the gap even when they do not name it. A sentence announcing that the evidence clearly proves hybrid working improves engagement, resting on one practitioner survey, tells the marker your judgement of evidence is off, and the doubt spreads to everything else you cite.

## Weaving evidence in, not dropping it on

There is a visible difference between evidence integrated into an argument and evidence decorating it. Decoration looks like a paragraph of assertions with a citation in brackets at the end, or a quotation parachuted in with no sentence explaining why it is there. Integration looks like evidence doing work inside your own sentences: a finding introduced because it supports the point you just made, weighed against another that qualifies it, followed by your comment on what the two add up to.

A practical test: cover the citations and read the paragraph. If it still reads as a connected argument with reasons, the evidence is integrated. If it reads as a list of facts, you are decorating.

## Using workplace examples properly

Workplace examples are the evidence CIPD learners hold in greatest supply and use least well. Three rules turn them from anecdote into evidence. Be specific: a real situation with enough detail to analyse, not a vague line about communication being poor at your organisation. Anonymise: change or omit the names of people and, where the situation is sensitive, the organisation, and never include confidential data; your centre's guidance will say how far to go. Analyse rather than tell: the marks are not in the story but in what you draw from it, so every example needs a comment linking it back to the claim and, ideally, to what the published evidence would predict.

## Balancing sources across an answer

Markers read whole answers, and imbalance shows. An answer built entirely on one textbook reads as narrow, one built only on professional-body web pages reads as unread beyond the obvious, and one built purely on workplace stories reads as unresearched. Spread your evidence across types, and spread it across the answer too: if the citations cluster in the first section and the rest runs on assertion, the marker can see exactly where your reading ran out.

## What changes at each level

Expectations rise with level, in general terms. At Level 3, accurate examples and a small number of reputable sources are usually enough, because the skill being assessed is correct understanding, clearly supported. At Level 5, markers expect analysis built on credible published sources, organisational evidence used and interpreted, and comments that weigh what the evidence shows. At Level 7, they expect critical engagement with research: findings compared, methods and limitations acknowledged, and conclusions that take a defensible position between competing sources. These are broad patterns rather than rules, so check your current assessment brief and your centre's guidance, because requirements vary by unit and centre.

## How to evidence an answer, step by step

1. Draft the skeleton of your answer as claims: one sentence per point you intend to make, before any evidence.
2. Against each claim, note the evidence you hold: research, data, example or judgement, and be honest where the answer is nothing.
3. For every unsupported claim, either find support or soften the claim until it matches what you can show.
4. Write each point as claim, then evidence, then comment, giving the comment at least as many words as the evidence.
5. Check every workplace example is specific, anonymised and analysed, with a sentence tying it back to the claim.
6. Read the whole answer for balance: evidence types mixed, sources spread across sections, no single source carrying more than its share.
7. Cover the citations and reread; wherever the argument goes quiet, integration has failed, so add the connecting comment.

## Worked example: one claim, three ways

The example below is entirely fictional: Harwick Print is a made-up manufacturing firm, and every source and figure is invented for illustration.

Unsupported, the point reads like this. Induction at Harwick Print is poor, which is why so many new starters leave. The company should invest in a better onboarding programme, as this will fix the retention problem.

Decorated, it reads like this. Induction at Harwick Print is poor, which is why so many new starters leave (Varga, 2023). Onboarding is important for retention (Osei and Lindqvist, 2022). The company should invest in a better onboarding programme.

Properly evidenced, it reads like this. First-year leavers at Harwick Print named induction as their main reason for leaving in four of the six exit interviews held last year, a small sample but a consistent one. The pattern matches wider research: Varga (2023) found structured onboarding associated with lower early turnover across manufacturing firms, though the study measured association rather than cause. Taken together, the internal and published evidence justify piloting a structured induction programme, while the small numbers mean its effect should be reviewed after six months rather than assumed.

The first version is opinion. The second has citations, but they hang off assertions, and neither is connected to the recommendation it supposedly supports. The third makes a modest claim, brings two kinds of evidence to it, comments on the weight of each, and lets the limits of the evidence shape the recommendation. Same point, very different marks.

## Common pitfalls

- Assertion without support: paragraphs of confident claims with no evidence in sight
- Quote-stacking: strings of quotations doing the arguing while your own analysis disappears
- Decoration: citations sprinkled at the ends of paragraphs with no comment connecting them to the claim
- Evidence that quietly contradicts the claim, cited without noticing what it actually found
- Overclaiming from one source, treating a single study or survey as settled fact
- Workplace stories told at length but never analysed or linked back to the point

> Integrity note: never invent evidence, data or sources. A made-up statistic, or a citation to a study that does not exist, is academic misconduct, and it turns a weak answer into a failed one. If the evidence does not exist, soften the claim; invented material belongs only in clearly flagged fictional examples like the one above.
