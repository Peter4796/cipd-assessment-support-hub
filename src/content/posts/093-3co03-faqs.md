3CO03, Core behaviours for people professionals, prompts a different set of questions from the other Level 3 units, because it is the one that asks you to write about yourself. Learners want to know how personal to be, how honest to be, and whether a working life outside HR counts as evidence. The short answers are: professionally personal, genuinely honest, and yes it does. The longer answers are below. They give you reliable principles, but task formats, word counts and criteria wording vary between study centres and change over time, so always check the specifics against your own current assessment brief and your centre's guidance.

### What is 3CO03 about?

3CO03 covers the behaviours expected of a people professional: ethical practice, inclusive and respectful working, and a genuine commitment to your own ongoing development through CPD. Unlike the knowledge-led units, it is less about what you can explain and more about how you conduct yourself, and how honestly you can look at that conduct.

It usually includes a reflective element, so expect part of your submission to be a first-person account of your own behaviour and learning. That makes it feel unusual next to units like 3CO01 or 3CO02, but many learners end up preferring it, because the raw material is their own working life rather than a textbook.

### Can I write in the first person?

In the reflective sections, yes, and you should. Reflection is an account of your own experience, so I is the natural and expected voice there, and a reflective account written entirely in the third person has quietly dodged the task. Keep any explain or describe sections in a more neutral register.

If your brief or centre gives specific style instructions, follow those. Where the brief is silent, the working rule is simple: neutral voice for explaining ideas, first person for reflecting on your own practice.

### What if I have never worked in HR?

Then you are the typical learner for this unit, not the exception. The behaviours it examines, such as honesty, fairness, respect, including people and learning from experience, exist in every workplace, and in study and volunteering too. A retail shift, a care rota, a group project or a sports club committee all generate usable evidence.

What matters is specificity, not setting. A precise, honest example from a weekend cafe job earns more than a vague claim dressed up in HR vocabulary. Draw on whatever working life you actually have.

### What counts as ethical behaviour at this level?

Everyday things, described honestly. Owning a mistake when it was yours, applying the same rules to people you like and people you find difficult, keeping what colleagues tell you in confidence, and following through on what you promised. A useful gut check: if your conduct in a situation could be described back to you in front of the team without you wincing, you are probably in ethical territory.

You do not need dilemmas worthy of a courtroom drama. The strongest examples usually involve mild pressure: a moment where the easy option and the right option pointed in different directions, and what you did about it.

### What is CPD in simple terms?

CPD stands for continuing professional development, and it is simply the habit of deliberately getting better at your work over time. The loop is: plan what you want to improve, do something about it, record what you did, and review what changed. Then repeat, modestly, for the rest of your career.

At Level 3 nobody expects expensive courses or conferences. Reading, shadowing a colleague, asking for feedback on a task, watching a free webinar and volunteering for something that stretches you all count, provided you can say what you did and what difference it made.

### Do I need a development plan?

Most 3CO03 briefs ask for one in some form, so check yours for the exact requirement. Expect something like a short table or structured list: two or three development areas, why each one matters to you, one or two realistic actions with rough timescales, and how you will judge progress.

Keep it honest and modest. A plan with three believable actions you will actually take reads far better than an ambitious one that is obviously written for the marker. Your reflective account is a ready source of material: whatever it surfaced as a weakness is a natural development area.

### How honest should my reflection be?

Honest enough to include something that did not go well. Markers read reflective accounts precisely to see whether you can notice your own missteps and learn from them, so an account where everything went smoothly gives them nothing to credit. Admitting you handled a moment badly, then showing what you changed, is the exercise working exactly as intended.

Honesty has a boundary, though: this is professional reflection, not confession. Choose workplace moments you are comfortable discussing, keep the focus on behaviour and learning, and leave out private matters. You always control which true stories you tell.

### Why do people get referred on 3CO03?

Rarely for weak subject knowledge. The usual causes are reflective accounts that describe events without drawing any learning from them, behaviour claims with no examples attached, accounts so flawless that markers stop believing them, and CPD plans too generic to belong to anyone. Missed criteria and referencing gaps cause referrals here just as they do in every unit.

The defence is structural. Follow every what happened with a what I learned, attach one specific moment to every behaviour you claim, include something imperfect, and keep your development plan small enough to be true.

### How is 3CO03 different from 5CO03?

5CO03 is the professional behaviours unit on the Level 5 Associate Diploma, and it visits similar territory: ethical practice, professional values and personal development. The difference is depth and register. Level 5 expects more analysis and evaluation, more engagement with ideas and sources, and evidence drawn from more substantial professional experience.

At Level 3 the bar is clear explanation and genuine reflection. If you have seen 5CO03 materials online, do not measure yourself against them; answering your own brief plainly and honestly is the standard that applies to you.

### How do I manage the word count?

Start with the figures in your own brief and your centre's tolerance rules, then divide the total across the tasks in proportion to what each carries, and treat those figures as budgets while you draft.

Reflective writing has its own trap: description swallows the count. If a reflective section runs long, it is nearly always the what happened part that has sprawled, so trim the storytelling and protect the what I learned and what I will do next, because that is where the marks live. In behaviours tasks, one example explained fully beats three sketched in passing.
