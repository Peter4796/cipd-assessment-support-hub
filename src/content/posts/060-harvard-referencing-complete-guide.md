## Why referencing matters in CIPD assessments

The CIPD Profession Map puts evidence-based decision-making at the centre of what a people professional does. Your assessments are designed to test that behaviour, which means markers are actively looking for claims backed by credible sources. A paragraph that asserts something about engagement, reward or employment practice without showing where the idea came from reads as opinion, and opinion earns little at any level.

Referencing is also how you demonstrate academic integrity. When you use someone else's idea, framework or finding, the citation is what separates legitimate use from presenting the work as your own. Centres treat missing or misleading attribution seriously, and at the sharp end it can mean a referral or an academic misconduct process. Careful referencing protects you.

Finally, referencing signals the breadth of your reading. Two learners can make the same point, but the one who anchors it to a respected source shows the marker something extra: that the point is grounded, that they have read around the subject, and that they can tell credible evidence from internet noise. That is exactly the judgement the qualification is trying to develop, so markers reward it when it appears on the page.

## What Harvard referencing actually is

Harvard is an author-date referencing system. Every time you draw on a source in your writing, you give a short in-text citation containing the author's surname and the year of publication. Every in-text citation then points to a full entry in a reference list at the end of your work, which gives the reader everything they need to find the source themselves.

The two parts must always match. Every citation in the text needs a corresponding entry in the list, and every entry in the list must be cited somewhere in the text. A mismatch in either direction is one of the most common referencing errors in CIPD submissions, and one of the easiest to prevent.

One thing worth knowing early: there is no single official Harvard style. Harvard is a family of author-date conventions, and universities, awarding bodies and study centres each publish their own version with slightly different rules about punctuation, italics and ordering. Everything in this guide describes the common core, but where your centre's referencing guide differs on a fine detail, your centre's guide wins. That principle applies to every article in this series.

## In-text citations, case by case

### Paraphrasing a source

Most of your citations will sit alongside paraphrase: you express an idea from a source in your own words and credit it with the surname and year in brackets at the relevant point. Throughout this guide the authors are invented for illustration, so a sentence might end with a citation shaped like (Mensah, 2024). If the author's name appears naturally in your sentence, only the year goes in brackets, as in a sentence that begins with Mensah (2024) arguing that structured onboarding reduces early turnover.

### Direct quotations

When you quote a source word for word, add a page number so the reader can find the exact passage, giving a citation shaped like (Mensah, 2024, p. 87). Online sources often have no page numbers, in which case centres typically accept the citation without one, or ask for a section heading or paragraph indicator instead. Keep quotations short and infrequent; the section on quoting versus paraphrasing below explains why.

### Two, three or more authors

A source with two authors names both, shaped like (Mensah and Okafor, 2023). With three or more authors, most Harvard variants shorten to the first surname followed by et al., shaped like (Larsen et al., 2022), although some centres name up to three authors before shortening. This is exactly the kind of fine detail where centre guides differ, so check yours and apply one rule consistently.

### Corporate authors

Many of the most useful sources in people practice are published by organisations rather than named individuals: professional bodies, government departments, research institutes. In Harvard, the organisation is the author, so a citation is shaped like (CIPD, 2025) or (Acas, 2024). Citing the CIPD's own factsheets, reports and the Profession Map raises some specific questions about dates, updates and abbreviations, and the companion guide How to Reference CIPD Factsheets, Reports and the Profession Map covers them in full.

### Sources with no date

Some web pages carry no publication date. The standard convention is to write no date in place of the year, giving a citation shaped like (Mensah, no date). Before you settle for that, look harder: many pages carry a last-reviewed or last-updated date near the top or bottom of the page, and an undated source is generally a weaker source. If a large part of your reference list is undated, that is a prompt to find better evidence, not just a formatting question.

## Building the reference list

The reference list sits at the end of your work, arranged alphabetically by author surname, with organisational authors filed by name. Each source appears once, no matter how many times you cited it in the text. This is a reference list rather than a bibliography: it contains only the sources you actually cited, not everything you read along the way, unless your centre asks for a bibliography as well.

Different source types follow different patterns. The exact punctuation varies between style guides, so treat the following as the general shape of each entry type rather than the one true format, and note that every author, title and publisher here is invented as an illustration of the pattern.

- A book entry generally runs author surname and initials, year in brackets, the title, the edition if it is not the first, then place of publication and publisher. An entry shaped like: Mensah, K. (2024) People Analytics in Practice. 2nd edn. London: Example Press.
- A journal article generally runs author, year, the article title in quotation marks, the journal title, the volume and issue numbers, then the page range. An entry shaped like: Okafor, T. and Larsen, P. (2023) 'Line manager capability and employee voice', Journal of Illustrative People Studies, 14(2), pp. 101 to 118.
- A web page or online organisational report generally runs the organisation or author, year, the title, an indication that the source is online, then where it is available and the date you accessed it. An entry shaped like: Example Institute (2025) Workforce Resourcing Briefing. Online. Available at the Example Institute website. Accessed 10 July 2026.

The accessed date matters for online sources because web content changes and disappears. It tells the marker which version of the page you used. Missing accessed dates are among the most frequently penalised small errors, so capture the date the moment you first use the source.

## Quoting versus paraphrasing

Paraphrasing means expressing a source's idea in your own words, with a citation. Quoting means reproducing the exact words, in quotation marks, with a citation and page number. Both are legitimate, but they are not equally valuable, and the balance you strike affects your marks.

Paraphrase is worth more because it demonstrates understanding. To restate an idea accurately in your own words, you have to have processed it, and processing is what markers are assessing. A string of quotations stitched together shows only that you can copy accurately, and it crowds out the analysis that carries the marks. Over-quoting also fills your word count with words that are not yours, which is an expensive habit when limits are tight.

Save direct quotation for the rare cases where the exact wording matters: a precise definition, a piece of legislation, or a phrase so distinctive that paraphrasing it would lose something. As a working discipline, if you find more than a couple of short quotations per page of your draft, convert most of them into paraphrase and keep the citations.

## Cite what you actually read

A quiet rule sits underneath the whole system: your citations should record the path you actually took to each idea. If you read about one writer's model inside another author's textbook, citing the original writer as though you read their work first-hand misrepresents your reading, and it can trip you up if a marker probes.

Harvard handles this situation with secondary referencing, using a cited in construction in the text and listing only the source you actually read. It has its own conventions and its own pitfalls, and the companion guide Secondary Referencing in Harvard Style, Explained covers when to use it, when to avoid it, and how to track down originals so you rarely need it.

## Consistency, and why your centre's guide wins

Markers will forgive a debatable comma; what erodes their confidence is inconsistency, where one journal entry has quotation marks around the article title and the next does not, or accessed dates appear in three different formats. Pick one pattern for each source type, apply it everywhere, and when in doubt follow your centre's referencing guide, because that is the version your work will be checked against. Where your centre publishes no detailed guide, choose one mainstream Harvard reference, such as your programme's recommended text, and stick to it throughout.

## A workflow for referencing as you write

Referencing goes wrong when it is left until the end, because reconstructing sources from memory late at night is how mismatches, missing dates and guessed page numbers happen. Build it into the drafting process instead.

1. Before you write anything, create your reference list as a separate section or document, ready to receive entries.
2. Every time you find a useful source, capture its full details immediately: author, year, title, publication details, and the accessed date for anything online.
3. As you draft, insert the in-text citation at the moment you use an idea, even if the sentence around it is still rough.
4. Add the full reference list entry at the same time, so the text and the list grow together and can never fall out of step.
5. When you capture a quotation, record the page number then and there, and mark it clearly as a quotation so you never mistake it for your own words later.
6. At the end of each writing session, spend two minutes scanning the new citations against the list to confirm they match.
7. Before submission, do a final two-way check: read the text and confirm every citation has an entry, read the list and confirm every entry is cited, then check formatting consistency and alphabetical order.

> Tutor tip: for the final check, put your reference list beside your text on screen and tick each entry off as you find its citation. Anything unticked in either direction is an error you have just caught before the marker did.

## A worked example, with invented sources

Here is a short example paragraph showing citations woven into analysis rather than decorating it. Every source in it is invented for illustration.

Rising early turnover at the organisation is consistent with research linking weak onboarding to first-year exits (Mensah, 2024). Okafor and Larsen (2023) found that line manager capability shaped how new starters experienced their first months, which suggests the problem may sit with local management practice rather than recruitment. Commentary from the Example Institute (2025) points the same way, although as a practitioner publication it carries less weight than the peer-reviewed studies. Taken together, the evidence favours piloting a structured onboarding programme with line manager training at its core.

Notice what the citations are doing. Each one is attached to a specific claim rather than sprinkled at the end of the paragraph. The writer paraphrases throughout, names authors in the sentence when it helps the flow, weighs the credibility of one source against another, and then draws a conclusion of their own. That is referencing working as evidence, which is precisely what CIPD assessments reward.

## Common errors at a glance

The same referencing errors appear in CIPD submissions year after year: citations with no matching entry, entries that are never cited, inconsistent formatting, missing accessed dates, websites cited by address rather than author, and over-quoting. Each is avoidable once you can recognise it. ## Harvard referencing FAQs

### How many references does a CIPD assignment need?

There is no official number, and any figure you see online is a guess. The honest answer is enough credible sources to underpin every significant claim you make, each used meaningfully. A modest set of strong sources cited with purpose will always outscore a long list added for show. Expectations rise with level, so check your brief and ask your tutor what is typical for your programme.

### Do I need to reference CIPD factsheets and reports?

Yes. They are published sources like any other, with the CIPD as a corporate author, and using them without citation is still a referencing failure. They also raise specific questions about dates and updated pages, which the guide How to Reference CIPD Factsheets, Reports and the Profession Map answers in detail.

### Can I use a referencing generator to build my list?

Yes, as a starting point. Generators and reference managers save time, but they routinely get capitalisation, editions and accessed dates wrong, so check every entry against your centre's guide before submission. The guide Free Harvard Referencing Tools for CIPD reviews the main options and how to use them safely.

### Is Harvard the only referencing style CIPD centres use?

It is by far the most common, but not universal, and centres also differ in which Harvard variant they follow. Your assessment brief or programme handbook will state the expected style. If it is silent, ask your tutor rather than guessing, because consistency against the expected style is part of what is being marked.
