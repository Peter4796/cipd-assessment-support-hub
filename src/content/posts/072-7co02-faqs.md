Here are answers to the questions learners ask most often about 7CO02, People management and development strategies for performance, the Level 7 unit that asks whether and how strategic people management actually drives organisational performance. The answers give you principles and method, but task formats, word counts and requirements vary between study centres and change over time, so always check the details against your own current brief and your centre's guidance.

### What is 7CO02 about?

7CO02 sits within the Level 7 Advanced Diploma in Strategic People Management and examines how strategic people management and development drive organisational performance. Its themes are aligning people strategy with business strategy, high-performance working practices, the strategic role of the people function, and evaluating the impact of people strategies.

What makes it distinctive is its scepticism. The unit does not ask you to assert that people strategies work; it asks you to evaluate whether they do, under what conditions, and how anyone would know. Critical, evidence-led argument carries most of the marks throughout.

### How is 7CO02 different from 7CO01?

The two units look at the same organisational world from different altitudes. 7CO01 is concerned with the wider context in which organisations operate: the external environment, change and how organisations respond to it. 7CO02 turns inward and strategic: given that context, how should the organisation manage and develop its people to perform, and how would it evaluate whether its choices worked?

In practice, the units feed each other. Contextual analysis from 7CO01 gives your 7CO02 arguments something to fit against, because you cannot evaluate the alignment of a people strategy without understanding the strategy and environment it is supposed to serve. Check your own brief for how your centre sequences and assesses each unit.

### What is high-performance working?

High-performance working is the idea that certain bundles of people management practices, adopted together, lift organisational performance beyond what any single practice achieves: typically practices that broaden skills, increase involvement and autonomy, and link reward to contribution. The claim is that the bundle creates a workforce that is more capable, more motivated and better placed to use its capability.

For assessment purposes, the crucial word is contested. High-performance work systems are one of the most debated areas in the research literature: writers disagree about which practices belong in the bundle, how performance should be measured, and above all about causality. Your assignment should treat high-performance working as a serious but unproven proposition, not a settled fact.

### What does best fit versus best practice mean?

Best practice argues that some ways of managing people raise performance in almost any organisation, so the task is to adopt them well. Best fit argues that practices only pay off when they match the organisation's strategy and context, so the task is tailoring, not adoption. Each position exposes the other's weakness: best practice struggles to explain why the same practices produce different results in different places, and best fit struggles with organisations whose strategies shift faster than people practices can follow.

At Level 7 you are not expected to declare a winner. You are expected to use the debate as a pair of lenses, show what each reveals and misses in your chosen context, and often to bring in the resource-based view of the firm as a third perspective focused on hard-to-copy capability.

### How do I evaluate a strategy rather than describe it?

Description tells the reader what a strategy is; evaluation judges whether it is any good and shows the workings. The practical method is to interrogate every claim the strategy makes: what outcome is this practice supposed to produce, what is the evidence it produces it, what would count as success, what could explain success other than the strategy, and what are the costs and risks of being wrong?

A useful drafting test is to look at each paragraph's final sentence. If it summarises a model or restates the strategy, the paragraph is descriptive. If it delivers a judgement with reasons, and ideally a limitation, the paragraph is evaluative. Rewriting final sentences forces the analysis upstream of them.

### Do I need to use my own organisation?

It depends on your brief, so check it first: some tasks specify a case study, others invite you to draw on an organisation you know well. Where you have the choice, your own organisation is usually the stronger option, because evaluation bites hardest on a context you genuinely understand, including the gaps between what is espoused and what is enacted.

If you do write about your employer, anonymise sensitive details, avoid disclosing anything confidential, and treat the organisation as a case to be examined rather than defended. Loyalty reads as bias in academic writing; honest appraisal reads as rigour.

### How much reading is expected?

Enough to stage a real argument, which means sources that disagree with each other. A masters-level answer typically engages closely with a manageable set of perspectives per major task rather than name-checking dozens, and deliberately includes at least one credible position that challenges the line you take. Your centre's reading list is the floor, not the ceiling.

Quality of use matters more than quantity of citation. One idea applied precisely to your context, with its limits acknowledged, is worth more than a paragraph of names. Avoid the wallpaper approach where citations decorate a claim that none of them actually supports.

### Why do answers get referred?

Three patterns account for most 7CO02 referrals. Describing models instead of evaluating them: accurate summaries of best fit, best practice and the business partner idea with no comparison, testing or judgement. Treating alignment as a slogan: repeating that people strategy must align with business strategy without defining, applying or questioning it. And presenting contested evidence as settled: writing as if high-performance working were proven to drive results, which tells the marker the research debate has been missed.

None of these signals weak ability; they signal a professional register, where confident assertion works, colliding with an academic one, where every claim must show its basis. The fix is usually targeted, deepening the evaluation and evidence handling, rather than a full rewrite.

### How do I show strategic thinking in my writing?

Strategic thinking shows in altitude and consequence. Altitude means connecting people questions to how the organisation competes, earns and spends, rather than staying inside the people function's own concerns. Consequence means following choices through: if the organisation adopts this practice, what does it cost, what does it displace, what could go wrong, and how does the answer change if the market shifts?

Concretely, write about trade-offs rather than benefits, conditions rather than certainties, and time horizons rather than snapshots. A sentence that says what an organisation should do, at what cost, under what assumptions, and how it would know if it was wrong, is strategic thinking on the page.

### How do I handle the word count?

Word counts and tolerances are set by your centre, so work from your own brief rather than any figure found online, and check how reference lists, tables and appendices are treated. Allocate words across tasks in proportion to their weight before you draft, and hold some back for conclusions, because judgement sections are where 7CO02 marks concentrate.

If you are over the limit, cut description first: model summaries, background narrative and repeated context. Evaluation, evidence and justified judgement are what the criteria in your brief reward, and trimming description to protect them almost always improves the answer anyway.
