It asks you to examine your own practice: how you influence, how you make decisions under pressure, how you act on your ethical values, and how commercially sharp your judgement really is. For many experienced practitioners, writing about themselves to a postgraduate academic standard turns out to be harder than writing about strategy.

## What 7CO03 is about

7CO03, Personal effectiveness, ethics and business acumen, develops personal effectiveness, ethical practice and commercial awareness at a senior level. Where most units ask you to analyse organisations, this one asks you to analyse yourself as an instrument of change: how you influence senior stakeholders, how you build credibility, how you champion inclusion and diversity, and how you bring commercial judgement to people decisions. Assessment usually includes reflective and applied elements, and demonstrating critical self-awareness and sound judgement carries much of the weight.

The unit connects naturally to the CIPD Profession Map, which places ethical practice, professional courage and commercial thinking among the expectations of senior people professionals. Markers want to see those behaviours evidenced from your real working life, examined honestly, and connected to what you are learning as you study.

## The main themes

- Ethical values and professional courage: knowing what you stand for and being willing to challenge when it matters
- Influencing and building credibility with senior stakeholders
- Championing inclusion and diversity in everyday decisions, not just in policy documents
- Business acumen and commercial awareness in people practice

## What masters-level assessment expects

Level 7 is postgraduate study, and the jump in expectation catches many capable practitioners out. At Level 5, sound explanation backed by some reading earns credit. At Level 7, markers expect critical engagement: you weigh competing perspectives from the literature, acknowledge ambiguity, test your own assumptions, and reach a justified position rather than a safe summary.

Three words capture the standard. Criticality means questioning rather than reporting: every model, source and belief you use is examined for strengths, limits and fit to your context. Evidence means nothing is asserted without something a marker can inspect, whether published literature, documented feedback or a concrete change in your practice. Judgement means committing to a reasoned position: masters-level writing that never takes a stance reads as evasive rather than balanced.

Reflection is held to the same standard. A masters-level reflective account is not a diary entry with a conclusion attached; it is analysis in which you are the case study. It shows what happened, what you assumed, what theory and feedback reveal about those assumptions, and what you now do differently as a result.

## Why 7CO03 is one of the most commonly referred Level 7 units

Ask tutors which Level 7 unit generates the most referrals and this one is usually near the top of the list. The reasons are remarkably consistent, and they are about register rather than intellect.

The first is descriptive reflection. Learners narrate events, sometimes vividly, and attach a sentence of feeling or a line about lessons learned. There is no questioning of assumptions, no theory used as a lens, and no evidence that anything changed. It reads as a story, and stories do not meet postgraduate criteria.

The second is unevidenced claims of effectiveness. Sentences such as I am a strong influencer, or colleagues regard me as highly credible, earn nothing on their own. A marker cannot verify self-praise. Without feedback received, outcomes achieved or examples examined, a claim of effectiveness is simply an opinion about yourself.

The third is thin commercial awareness. Many learners write fluently about values, ethics and inclusion, then go quiet on the business. Commercial sections stay generic, with no engagement with how the organisation makes and spends money, what pressures it faces, or how people decisions affect performance and risk. At this level, commercial awareness has to be demonstrated in the reasoning, not declared in a paragraph.

None of these signals weak ability. They signal a register learned in professional life, where confident assertion is rewarded, colliding with an academic standard where every claim must show its working. The fix is a change of habit, not a change of person.

## What to do if you have been referred

Start by rereading your tutor feedback slowly, at least twice, and translating every comment into a specific action in a specific section. Almost every 7CO03 referral comment maps onto one of the three problems above, which means the repair is usually targeted rather than total: deepen the reflection, evidence the claims, sharpen the commercial reasoning. Resist the urge to rewrite everything from scratch before you have diagnosed exactly what the feedback is asking for.

Resubmission is a normal part of postgraduate study. Treat it as a redraft with better information, not a verdict on your capability.

## How to approach 7CO03, step by step

1. Break down the brief. List every task, note the command verbs, separate the reflective elements from the applied ones, and check what the criteria in your brief actually ask you to demonstrate.
2. Choose your examples early. Select a small number of real situations from your own senior practice that let you show influencing, ethical challenge, inclusion and commercial judgement. Depth on a few beats breadth on many.
3. Gather evidence about yourself. Collect appraisal comments, 360-degree feedback, stakeholder reactions, decisions you have revisited and development records. This is the raw material that turns claims into evidence.
4. Read widely and critically. Cover influence, ethics, inclusion and the commercial context of people work, and deliberately include perspectives that disagree with each other and with you.
5. Draft the reflective sections using a model as scaffolding. Schön, Kolb or Gibbs can structure your thinking, but the marks come from the analysis, not the template.
6. Write the commercial sections as a business partner would think. Connect people decisions to cost, risk, performance and the organisation's strategy, using the concrete realities of a sector you know.
7. Audit every claim of effectiveness. For each positive statement about yourself, check that feedback, an outcome or an examined example sits beside it on the page.
8. Review the draft against the criteria in your brief, then against register: is each section critical rather than descriptive, evidenced rather than asserted, and judged rather than hedged?

> Tutor tip: search your draft for every sentence that praises you, then look at what sits next to it. If there is no feedback, outcome or examined example a marker can inspect, the sentence is a claim, not evidence, and at Level 7 it will cost you marks.

## A worked example: Danielle at Ferngate Housing

Here is a fictional scenario that shows the register the unit rewards. Danielle is an invented people director at Ferngate Housing, a made-up housing association facing budget pressure. The executive team proposes a recruitment freeze, and Danielle believes it would fall hardest on frontline, largely part-time roles, undermining both service quality and the inclusion commitments the board has publicly made.

A weak submission handles this in one move: I challenged the board about the freeze and persuaded them to amend it, which demonstrates my professional courage and influencing skills. That is a story plus self-praise. It shows no preparation, no theory, no doubt and no evidence.

A strong submission slows down. Danielle describes how she prepared: modelling the cost of vacancies against the cost of agency cover, anticipating the finance director's objections, and choosing to frame her challenge around risk to the regulator relationship rather than values alone, a deliberate influencing choice she connects to the literature on persuasion and credibility. She is honest that she hesitated for a week before raising it, and she examines that hesitation rather than hiding it. She records what happened: the board adopted a targeted freeze exempting frontline roles, and the chief executive later told her the risk framing was what shifted the discussion, feedback she quotes as evidence. She closes by identifying what changed in her practice: she now prepares a commercial case for every ethical challenge she makes, because values arguments alone had failed her before.

Notice what changed between the two versions: the same event, but now with preparation, theory, honesty about doubt, evidence a marker can inspect, and a demonstrated change in practice. That is critical self-awareness on the page, and it is the shape to aim for whatever your own brief asks.

## Common mistakes to avoid

The recurring faults are predictable: narrating events instead of analysing them, name-dropping theory without applying it to your own behaviour, claiming strengths without evidence, presenting a flawless self in which nothing went wrong and nothing changed, ignoring the commercial dimension of every scenario, and writing in a distant third person that strips out the self-awareness the unit exists to assess. A reflective account where everything succeeded is a particular red flag: markers know real senior practice involves misjudgement, and an account without any reads as unreflective rather than impressive.

## 7CO03 FAQs

### Can I write in the first person?

Yes, and for reflective tasks it is usually expected. First person and academic rigour are not opposites: the rigour comes from evidence, reasoning and engagement with literature, not from grammatical distance. Check your centre's guidance for any specific conventions.

### How many reflective models do I need?

One model used deeply beats three used cosmetically. Choose the one that fits how you actually think about your practice, use it to structure genuine analysis, and show awareness of its limits rather than treating it as a form to fill in.

### Do my examples have to be successes?

No, and accounts of setbacks examined honestly often score better than victories, because they give you assumptions to question and changes to evidence. What matters is the quality of the analysis, not the flattering nature of the story.

### What if my brief looks different from this guide?

Follow your brief every time. Task formats, word counts and wording vary between study centres and change over time, so use this guide for method and register, and your own current brief and centre guidance for the exact requirements.
