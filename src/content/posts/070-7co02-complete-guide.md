If you are starting 7CO02, People management and development strategies for performance, you are looking at the unit that asks the most fundamental question in the Level 7 Advanced Diploma in Strategic People Management: does what people professionals do actually make organisations perform better? It sounds like a question with an obvious answer, and that is exactly the trap. The unit rewards learners who treat the claim as something to be tested, and it catches out those who write as if alignment, engagement and high-performance working were self-evidently effective.

Many capable practitioners find this unit harder than expected, because the register is new: at work, asserting that people strategy drives performance is uncontroversial, while at masters level that assertion is precisely what you are asked to interrogate. This guide is the hub of the 7CO02 series on this site: it covers what the unit is about, what postgraduate assessment expects, the big debates you will need a position on, why answers get referred, and a step-by-step method from brief to submission.

## What 7CO02 is about

7CO02 examines how strategic people management and development drive organisational performance. It covers high-performance working, the alignment of people strategy with business strategy, and the role of people professionals as strategic partners. Where some units ask you to design practices or reflect on your own behaviour, this one asks you to stand back and evaluate whole strategies: their logic, their evidence base, their fit to context, and their measurable impact. Critical, evidence-led argument is expected throughout, and it carries most of the marks.

The unit sits naturally alongside the CIPD Profession Map, which frames people professionals as contributors to organisational purpose and performance rather than administrators of process. The assessment tests whether you can make that case at a strategic level: weighing when and under what conditions people strategies actually move performance, and being honest about how hard that is to prove.

## What masters-level assessment expects

Level 7 is postgraduate study, and the standard is critical engagement rather than accurate description. For this unit, that means treating every strategy claim as a hypothesis. Does people strategy really drive performance? How would we know? What does the evidence base actually support, and where does it go quiet? A masters-level answer holds these questions open long enough to examine them, weighs competing perspectives from the literature, and then commits to a justified position rather than a safe summary.

Three habits mark out strong 7CO02 writing. The first is scepticism applied evenly: you question the fashionable claims as rigorously as the unfashionable ones. The second is evidence-awareness: you know that the research on people management and performance is genuinely contested, and you say so, rather than presenting one side as settled fact. The third is judgement: having weighed the arguments, you say what an organisation should do and why. Markers reward a defended position far more than a neutral tour of the models.

## The big debates

Three debates run through the unit, and your assignment will almost certainly require you to engage with at least one of them in depth.

### Best fit versus best practice

The best-practice position holds that some people management practices raise performance in almost any organisation, so the job is to identify and adopt them. The best-fit position replies that practices only pay off when they match the organisation's strategy, market and context, so a practice that transforms one firm may waste money in another. Each side has an obvious weakness: best practice struggles to explain why identical practices produce different results in different settings, while best fit struggles to say how managers should cope when strategy shifts faster than people practices can follow. A strong answer uses the debate as a lens rather than picking a team, and often brings in the resource-based view of the firm as a third position: that advantage comes from capabilities competitors cannot easily copy, which neither universal practices nor tight fit can fully explain.

### High-performance working and the causality problem

High-performance work systems are one of the most researched and most contested areas in the field. The claim is that bundles of practices, adopted together, lift performance beyond what any single practice achieves. The problem is causality. Studies tend to find associations between such practices and stronger performance, but association is not proof of direction: do the practices drive performance, or do already successful firms have the money, stability and confidence to adopt the practices? Add disagreement about which practices belong in the bundle, and how performance itself should be measured, and you have a genuinely open research question. You do not need to resolve it; you need to show you understand why it is open, and reason carefully about what an organisation should do under that uncertainty.

### The strategic partner aspiration and the reality

Ulrich's business partner model is the most widely debated framing of what the people function should be: a strategic contributor shaping business decisions, not a service desk answering them. The aspiration is easy to state and hard to live. Critics point out that many people functions adopted the structures and job titles of partnering without gaining real influence over strategy, and that the model can undervalue the operational work organisations still need done well. A strong 7CO02 answer asks what would have to be true for the people function to operate strategically in a specific context: credibility, data, business understanding, a seat at the table that is used rather than merely occupied.

## Why 7CO02 answers get referred

The referral patterns on this unit are consistent, and they mirror the debates above. The first is describing models instead of evaluating them. An answer that explains best fit, best practice and the business partner model accurately, one after another, is a textbook summary; the marks sit in comparing them, testing them against evidence and context, and judging between them.

The second is treating alignment as a slogan. Sentences such as the people strategy must align with the business strategy appear in almost every referred script, asserted rather than examined. What alignment means, how it is achieved, how you would recognise it, and whether tighter alignment is always better are the questions a masters-level answer takes on.

The third is ignoring the limits of the evidence. Answers that present high-performance working as proven, or engagement as a guaranteed driver of results, signal that the learner has not engaged with the research debate at the heart of the unit. Acknowledging contested evidence is not hedging; at this level it is accuracy, and markers read it as strength.

## How to approach 7CO02, step by step

1. Break down the brief. List every task, underline the command verbs, and note where you are asked to evaluate, justify or critically assess, because those verbs tell you where description will not be enough.
2. Map each task to the debates. Work out whether the question is really about fit, about the evidence for high-performance working, about the role of the function, or about measuring impact, and plan to engage with the relevant argument directly.
3. Read both sides deliberately. For every position you find persuasive, find a credible source that disagrees with it, so your answer can stage a genuine argument rather than a parade of supporting citations.
4. Choose your organisational context early. Whether you use your own organisation or a case, decide what its strategy, sector and pressures are, because evaluation only becomes concrete when it is anchored somewhere specific.
5. Draft arguments, not summaries. Give each section a claim you intend to defend, bring evidence and counter-evidence to it, and close with a judgement, rather than closing with a model neatly explained.
6. Interrogate every performance claim. Each time you write that a practice improves performance, ask how would we know, what would we measure, and what else could explain the result, and put the answer on the page.
7. Evaluate, then recommend. Let recommendations follow from your evaluation, with their limits and risks acknowledged, so the marker can see judgement rather than optimism.
8. Review against the criteria in your brief, then against register: is each section critical rather than descriptive, evidenced rather than asserted, and concluded with a defended position?

> Tutor tip: search your draft for the word align and its variants. Every time it appears, check that the surrounding sentences say what alignment means there, how it would be achieved, and how you would know it existed. If the word is doing the work on its own, the sentence is a slogan, and slogans do not earn Level 7 marks.

## A worked example: Calderbrook Foods

Here is a fictional scenario that shows the register the unit rewards. Calderbrook Foods is an invented mid-sized food manufacturer competing mainly on cost, with thin margins, high seasonal demand and a largely hourly-paid workforce. A new people director proposes a high-performance working initiative: team-based working, broader training, more autonomy on the line and a gainsharing element, arguing that it will raise productivity and cut waste.

A weak submission endorses the proposal in one move: research shows that high-performance work systems improve organisational performance, so Calderbrook should implement the initiative to align its people strategy with its business strategy. That is a slogan resting on an overstated evidence claim, and it ignores context entirely.

A strong submission slows down. It notes that the evidence on high-performance working is contested, and that the causality question matters here: the firms where such systems appear to flourish are often not thin-margin cost competitors, so Calderbrook cannot assume their results transfer. It applies the best-fit lens and asks whether high-autonomy practices fit a cost-based strategy, and the resource-based view and asks whether the initiative could build a capability, such as fast, flexible line teams, that rivals would find hard to copy. It then confronts measurement: productivity per shift, waste rates and voluntary turnover as indicators, tracked against comparable sites, with honesty that seasonal demand and ingredient prices will confound any simple before-and-after reading. It concludes with a judgement: a phased pilot in two sites with defined measures and a decision point, justified as the response that takes both the promise and the uncertainty of the evidence seriously. Same scenario, but now with debate, context, measurement and a defended position on the page.

## Common mistakes to avoid

The recurring faults are predictable: summarising models without comparing or testing them, treating alignment as self-evidently good rather than something to be defined and demonstrated, citing only sources that support the argument, presenting contested research as settled fact, making recommendations that float free of any evaluation, and writing about organisations in general when the task demands a specific context. A subtler fault is false balance: laying out both sides of a debate and refusing to judge between them. At Level 7, weighing the arguments and still committing to a reasoned position is the skill being assessed.

## 7CO02 FAQs

### Do I have to conclude that people strategy drives performance?

No, and a forced positive conclusion usually weakens an answer. The strongest submissions conclude conditionally: people strategies can drive performance under certain conditions, and the evidence is stronger in some contexts than others. What matters is that your conclusion follows from the evidence you have weighed.

### Which writers and ideas should I engage with?

Anchor your reading in the debates rather than a list of names: best fit versus best practice, the resource-based view, the research argument around high-performance work systems, and the discussion of the people function's strategic role, including Ulrich's business partner model. Your centre's reading list is the starting point; the marks come from using ideas as lenses, not from citing them.

### Can I base my answers on my own organisation?

Usually yes, and often it helps, because evaluation needs a real context to bite on. Check your brief first, since some tasks specify a case study or a sector. If you do use your employer, anonymise sensitive detail and treat its strategy as something to be examined, not defended.

### What if my brief looks different from this guide?

Follow your brief every time. Task formats, word counts and requirements vary between study centres and change over time, so use this guide for method and register, and your own current brief and centre guidance for the exact requirements.
