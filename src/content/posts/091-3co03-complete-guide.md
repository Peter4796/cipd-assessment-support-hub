Most Level 3 units ask what you know. 3CO03, Core behaviours for people professionals, asks something more personal: how do you behave at work, and how honestly can you look at yourself? For many learners it is the first assignment that has ever wanted the word I, and after years of being told to keep yourself out of formal writing, that can feel genuinely strange.

Here is the reassuring part. 3CO03 is built for people at the start of their people-practice journey. It rewards honesty, plain language and small real examples, not polished self-analysis or a spotless track record. This guide is the hub of the 3CO03 series on this site, taking you from your first read of the brief through the reflective element to your final check before submission.

## What 3CO03 is about

3CO03 focuses on the behaviours expected of a people professional rather than a body of subject knowledge. It looks at ethical practice, treating people inclusively and with respect, and keeping a genuine commitment to your own ongoing development. In other words, it is less about what you would advise an organisation to do and more about the kind of professional you are becoming while you do it.

The unit usually includes a reflective element, which is why it feels different from units like 3CO01 or 3CO02. Instead of explaining ideas at arm's length, you are asked to turn the lens on yourself: to describe something you actually did, think honestly about how it went, and say what you learned. Being able to reflect truthfully on your own behaviour and development is central to a strong submission, and this guide spends plenty of time on exactly that skill.

It also helps to know the common sticking points before you start. Learners most often struggle to write a reflective account that feels genuine and is backed by real detail, to connect the core behaviours described in the CIPD Profession Map to their own everyday work, and to strike the balance between describing what happened and honestly reflecting on it. Every section below is built around closing those three gaps.

## How 3CO03 is assessed at Level 3

Like the other Level 3 units, 3CO03 is assessed through written tasks marked against the criteria in your brief, with command verbs that lean towards explain and describe. An explain question wants you to say what something is and why it matters, in your own words; a describe question wants a clear, accurate account. Nobody is asking for extended critical debate at this level, so you do not need to sound like a textbook.

The difference is the reflective element. Part of your submission will usually ask you to write about your own behaviour and development, and here the first person is not just allowed but expected. Sentences beginning with I noticed, I decided, I learned are exactly right in a reflective section, even though the explain and describe sections stay in a more neutral register. Task formats, word counts and the precise mix of tasks vary between study centres and change over time, so always work from your own current assessment brief and your centre's guidance.

## Ethical and inclusive behaviour in plain terms

Strip away the terminology and the ethical side of 3CO03 comes down to familiar things: being honest, being fair, keeping confidences, doing what you said you would do, and speaking up politely when something does not sit right with you. The inclusive side is equally down to earth: making sure people are not left out or talked over, being respectful of differences, and noticing when a rota, a meeting habit or a turn of phrase quietly disadvantages someone.

You do not need dramatic examples. Passing on a colleague's concern accurately, refusing to gossip about someone's personal situation, or making sure a quiet team member gets heard in a briefing are all valid evidence of these behaviours at Level 3. Because this topic carries real weight in the unit, there is a dedicated deep dive in this series, Professional Behaviours in 3CO03, Explained, which works through ethical principles, inclusion and professional values with small workplace examples and a method for answering behaviours tasks.

## CPD basics: a simple, honest development plan

CPD stands for continuing professional development, and at Level 3 it is best understood as a simple ongoing habit rather than a grand programme: plan what you want to get better at, do something about it, record what you did, and review what difference it made. That loop, repeated modestly, is the whole idea.

A good development plan for this unit is small and honest. Pick two or three development areas that are genuinely yours, such as growing in confidence when speaking to groups, learning the basics of employment law, or getting more comfortable with spreadsheets. For each one, note why it matters to you, one or two realistic actions with rough timescales, and how you will know you have made progress. Free or low-cost actions are perfectly acceptable: reading, short webinars, shadowing a colleague, volunteering for a task that stretches you. A modest plan you believe in reads far better than an ambitious one that is obviously decorative.

## The reflective element, for beginners

If you have never written reflectively before, use the simplest structure there is: what happened, what I learned, and what I will do next. First, describe the situation briefly and factually, including your part in it. Second, be honest about how it went, what you felt, what worked and what did not. Third, say what you will do differently or build on next time, which is where your reflection connects naturally to your CPD plan.

Two reassurances for first-timers. Writing I is correct here, not a lapse in formality; a reflective account written entirely in the third person has missed the point of the task. And imperfection is the raw material of reflection, not something to hide. An account that says I handled this badly at first, here is what I realised, and here is what I now do differently is exactly what markers hope to read. Nobody is grading you on being flawless; they are grading your ability to notice, learn and adjust.

## How to approach 3CO03, step by step

1. Read the whole brief twice and list every task and criterion it contains, marking which parts want neutral explanation and which want first-person reflection.
2. Underline the command verb in each task so you know whether it wants an explanation, a description or a reflective account.
3. Gather your raw material: two or three real moments from work, study or volunteering where your behaviour mattered, and two or three genuine development needs.
4. For each behaviours task, pair every claim with a small real example, using the pattern of behaviour, example, what it shows.
5. Draft your reflective account using what happened, what I learned, what I will do next, writing naturally in the first person.
6. Build your CPD plan from the development needs your reflection surfaced, keeping actions small, dated and realistic.
7. Record every source as you use it and reference the ideas you borrow, even in a reflective unit.
8. Check each answer against your criterion list line by line, then reread the reflective sections aloud and ask whether they sound like you on an honest day.

> Integrity note: your reflective account must describe your own real experience. Borrowing or inventing someone else's story is a misconduct risk, and it also produces flat, unconvincing writing. Your genuine experience, plainly told, is both safer and stronger.

## A worked example: Amara and the difficult customer

Here is a fictional scenario that shows the reflective element in action. Amara is an invented learner with no HR experience beyond a part-time weekend job in a coffee shop. Asked to reflect on her own behaviour, she picks a moment she actually remembers: a customer who became loud and personal over a wrong order during a busy Saturday rush.

Her first draft was pure description: the customer shouted, I apologised, I remade the drink, the customer left. True, but not yet reflection. Her second draft turns it into a reflective paragraph: I stayed outwardly calm and fixed the order quickly, which I am pleased about, but afterwards I realised I had let the customer speak to me in a way I should not have accepted, and I never mentioned it to my shift supervisor because I did not want to seem unable to cope. I learned that staying professional does not mean absorbing poor treatment in silence, and that raising something is not the same as failing to handle it. Next time I will flag an incident like this straight away, and I want to get better at speaking up calmly in the moment.

Notice what happens at the end: the reflection produces a CPD action almost by itself. Amara adds a line to her development plan about building confidence in courageous conversations, with two small actions, asking her supervisor how she handles difficult customers, and volunteering to help with closing-time conversations she would normally avoid, plus a simple three-month check on progress. One honest moment, reflected on properly, has fed both the reflective task and the CPD task. You can do the same with a moment from any job, placement or voluntary role of your own.

## Common mistakes to avoid

The predictable failure patterns in 3CO03 are description without reflection, where the account says what happened but never what was learned; claims without examples, where a learner asserts they are ethical and inclusive but shows nothing; the perfect-person account, where everything went well and nothing was learned, which markers rarely believe; and CPD plans that are obviously written for the assignment rather than for the learner, full of grand actions that will never happen.

All of these are avoidable. Follow every what happened with a what I learned, attach a small real example to every behaviour you claim, include at least one moment that did not go well, and keep your development plan modest enough to be true. Do those four things and you have closed off the main routes to a referral.

## 3CO03 FAQs

### Is it really acceptable to write in the first person?

Yes, in the reflective parts it is expected. Reflection is about your own experience and learning, and there is no honest way to write that without I. Keep the explain and describe sections in a more neutral register, and check your brief for any specific instructions on style.

### What if I have no HR experience at all?

You do not need any. The behaviours in this unit show up in every workplace and beyond: part-time jobs, volunteering, group projects at college. Honesty, fairness, including people and learning from experience are not HR-only activities, and markers know most Level 3 learners are at the start of their careers.

### How personal should my reflection get?

Personal enough to be honest about your own behaviour and feelings at work, but this is professional reflection, not a diary. Focus on workplace situations, what you did, what you learned and what you will do next. You never need to share private matters that feel uncomfortable.

### Do I need theory in a reflective account?

Only lightly. A simple structure such as what happened, what I learned, what next matters far more than name-checking reflective models. If your centre teaches a particular model and your brief asks for it, use that; otherwise clarity and honesty carry the marks at this level.
