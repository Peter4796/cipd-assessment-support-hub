3CO03, Core behaviours for people professionals, is the unit where your assignment stops asking what you know and starts asking how you act. Tasks about professional behaviours can feel slippery for exactly that reason: there is no model to memorise and no procedure to describe, just you, your everyday conduct, and the challenge of writing about it convincingly.

This guide is the deep dive on that challenge. It unpacks what professional behaviours actually means at Level 3, translates ethical practice and inclusion into plain everyday terms, and gives you a repeatable pattern for turning ordinary working life into the specific, believable evidence that behaviours tasks reward. It sits alongside the hub for this unit, 3CO03 Assignment: The Complete Guide, which covers the whole assignment from first read of the brief to final check.

## What professional behaviours means at Level 3

The first thing to settle is what the word behaviours is doing in this unit. Knowledge is what you know: the stages of a recruitment process, the basics of employment law. Behaviours are how you act: whether you keep a confidence, whether you give the quiet colleague the same attention as the loud one, whether you do the thing you said you would do by the time you said you would do it. 3CO03 is interested in the second list.

That distinction changes how you should write. A knowledge task is answered by explaining ideas; a behaviours task is answered by showing conduct. The evidence that counts is not a definition of integrity but a small true moment where your integrity, or your fairness, or your respect for someone, was visible in what you actually did. Most of this guide is about finding and presenting those moments.

## Ethical principles in everyday terms

Ethical practice sounds grand, but at Level 3 it translates into behaviour you already recognise. Honesty means telling the truth even when a softer version would be easier, such as admitting to your manager that the error in the rota was yours. Fairness means applying the same standard to everyone, such as handling shift swaps by the same rules for the colleague you like and the one you find difficult.

Keeping confidences means treating what people tell you as theirs, not as currency: a colleague mentions a health worry and it goes no further than the person who genuinely needs to know. And reliability, the quietest ethical behaviour, means doing what you said you would do, so that other people can safely plan around your word. None of these examples needs an HR job title. They happen in shops, wards, warehouses, classrooms and call centres every day, which is exactly why they make good assignment evidence.

## Inclusive and respectful working in practice

Inclusion in 3CO03 is not a policy summary; it is everyday practice. In meetings it looks like noticing who has not spoken and making room for them, rather than letting the same two voices decide everything. In rotas it looks like sharing the unpopular shifts around instead of letting them settle on whoever complains least. In language it looks like using the name and pronunciation someone actually asked for, and dropping the in-jokes that leave newer people on the outside.

Small adjustments belong here too. Sending an agenda in advance so a colleague who likes time to prepare can arrive ready, or moving a briefing so a part-timer can attend, are modest acts with real inclusive effect. When a behaviours task asks about inclusive and respectful working, these ground-level habits are stronger evidence than any policy you could summarise, because they show what you do rather than what your organisation publishes.

## Professional values, and where they show up under pressure

Values are easy to claim on a calm Tuesday. The unit is more interested in where they show up under pressure, because pressure is where values either hold or quietly fold. It is easy to be fair when everyone agrees, and harder when a popular colleague wants an exception. It is easy to keep a confidence when nothing turns on it, and harder when sharing it would make you the interesting person in the room.

When you choose examples for a behaviours task, look for moments with a little pressure in them: a deadline, a disagreement, a temptation to take the easy path. You do not need to have behaved perfectly. An example where you felt the pull of the easy option and chose otherwise, or gave in and later put it right, says far more about your values than one where nothing was at stake.

## Connecting behaviours to your own work

The reliable pattern for behaviours evidence has three parts: name the behaviour, give a real example, and say what it shows. Name it plainly: I try to be reliable with commitments. Evidence it with one specific moment: when I promised a colleague I would cover her Saturday shift, I turned down a better offer that came along afterwards, because she had already made plans on my word. Then close the loop: keeping that commitment mattered because she needed to be able to trust what I said, and trust is slow to rebuild once spent.

Notice the scale. Nothing in that example is dramatic, and that is the point. Markers at this level are not looking for heroics; they are looking for a visible connection between a claimed behaviour and actual conduct. One behaviour, one specific true moment, one sentence of meaning. Repeat that pattern for each behaviour your task asks about and you have the spine of a strong answer.

## How this links to the CIPD Profession Map

The CIPD Profession Map is the framework the profession uses to describe what good people practice looks like, and it includes a set of core behaviours expected of people professionals at every stage of their careers, covering territory such as acting ethically, valuing people and continuing to learn. Your brief may ask you to connect your own conduct to those behaviours, so it is worth reading the behaviour descriptions on the CIPD website in your own time.

Two cautions. Describe the Map in your own words rather than copying its wording, both for referencing reasons and because paraphrasing proves you understood it. And treat the Map as a mirror, not a script: the task is not to claim every behaviour it lists but to find honest overlap between what it describes and what you actually do, including the areas where you are still developing.

## A method for answering a behaviours task

1. Read the task and write down, in your own words, exactly which behaviours it is asking about.
2. For each behaviour, jot down two or three real moments from work, study or volunteering where that behaviour was tested, not just present.
3. Pick the moment you remember in most detail, because detail is what makes written evidence believable.
4. Draft each answer using the three-part pattern: name the behaviour, describe the specific moment, and say what it shows about how you work.
5. Check each example for pressure, and if nothing was at stake, swap it for a moment where something was.
6. Add one honest development note where it fits, naming a behaviour you are still working on and the small step you are taking.
7. Reread against the task and your criterion list, cutting anything that describes policy or other people rather than your own conduct.

> Tutor tip: if you could swap your name out of a sentence and it would still read as true, it is not yet evidence of your behaviour. The fix is nearly always to add the specific moment: when it happened, who was involved, and what you actually did.

## A worked example: weak claim versus evidenced behaviour

Here is the difference in practice, using a clearly fictional learner. Dan works part time in a call centre and is answering a task about ethical behaviour. His first attempt reads: I am an honest and ethical person, I always treat customers and colleagues fairly and with respect, and I understand how important confidentiality is in the workplace. Every word may be true, and none of it is evidence. There is no moment, no detail, nothing that could not have been written by anyone.

His second attempt applies the pattern: one behaviour I try to hold to is honesty when things go wrong. Last month I quoted a customer the wrong refund amount, and the mistake was unlikely ever to be noticed. I flagged it to my team leader the same day, then called the customer back to correct the figure and apologise. It was uncomfortable, but I would rather have one awkward conversation than let someone act on wrong information I gave them. That paragraph names the behaviour, anchors it in a single specific moment with a little pressure in it, and closes with what it shows. It is also far more interesting to read, which never hurts.

## Common pitfalls

Three patterns undo most behaviours answers. The first is claiming values without examples, a paragraph of adjectives about integrity and respect with no moment attached, which a marker cannot credit because there is nothing to verify. The second is describing policies instead of behaviour: an account of your organisation's equality policy or code of conduct answers a different question, because the task is about your conduct, not the documents around it.

The third is the perfect-person account, in which every behaviour is fully formed and nothing is still developing. It reads as unconvincing because it is, and it wastes the credit available for honest development areas. A believable answer includes at least one behaviour you are still building, with a real step attached. Task wording, word counts and criteria vary between study centres and change over time, so check your own current brief and your centre's guidance before deciding how many behaviours and examples each task needs.
