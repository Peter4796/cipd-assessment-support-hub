CIPD factsheets, reports and the Profession Map are the sources CIPD learners cite more than any others, yet they cause a surprising share of referencing errors. There is no individual author on the page, the content gets reviewed and updated over time, and sometimes there is no obvious publication date at all. None of that fits the author-and-year pattern most learners are taught first, so people improvise, and improvised references leak marks.

The good news is that Harvard handles all of it through a small set of conventions for corporate authors and online sources. This guide walks through referencing CIPD factsheets, reports, web pages and the Profession Map, with illustrative examples of each shape. As with everything in this series, referencing conventions vary in fine detail between centres and style guides, and your own centre's referencing guide always has the final say.

## In this guide you'll learn

- Why CIPD materials are corporate-author sources and what that changes
- The general Harvard shape for a factsheet, a report and a web page
- How to handle undated and updated pages, and how to reference the Profession Map
- Whether CIPD sources are enough on their own at each level

## The CIPD is the author

Harvard's first question about any source is who is accountable for it. For a factsheet or report published by a professional body, the answer is the organisation itself, so the organisation takes the author position in both the citation and the reference list entry. This is called a corporate author, and it behaves exactly like a personal author: a citation shaped like (CIPD, 2025) in the text, and an entry filed under CIPD in the alphabetical reference list.

It follows that you should never cite a factsheet by its web address, or by the name of a staff writer credited in passing. The organisation published it, stands behind it and maintains it, so the organisation is the author.

## The general shape of a CIPD reference

Punctuation and ordering differ slightly between style guides, so treat these as the general shape of each entry rather than the single correct format. The years and titles below are illustrative rather than references to specific current documents.

- A factsheet generally runs organisation, year, title, the word factsheet where your centre's style includes it, an online marker, then where and when you accessed it. Shaped like: CIPD (2025) Employee Engagement. Factsheet. Online. Available at the CIPD website. Accessed 12 July 2026.
- A report generally runs organisation, year, report title, an online marker, then the availability and accessed details. Shaped like: CIPD (2024) Illustrative Working Lives Survey Report. Online. Available at the CIPD website. Accessed 12 July 2026.
- A web page follows the same pattern with the page title in the title position. Shaped like: CIPD (no date) About the People Profession. Online. Available at the CIPD website. Accessed 12 July 2026.

In each case the in-text citation is simply the organisation and year, shaped like (CIPD, 2024). If you cite two CIPD sources from the same year, distinguish them with lowercase letters after the year, shaped like (CIPD, 2024a) and (CIPD, 2024b), with the same letters attached to the matching entries in your reference list.

## Citing the CIPD in your text

In formal writing, the usual convention is to give an organisation's full name at first mention in your prose, with the abbreviation in brackets, and then use the abbreviation alone. So your first mention might read the Chartered Institute of Personnel and Development (CIPD), with CIPD by itself from then on. The citations themselves normally use the abbreviated form throughout, because that is how the entries are filed in your list.

Some centres relax the full-name rule for the CIPD, on the sensible grounds that every reader of a CIPD assignment knows the abbreviation. Check your centre's guidance, and whichever practice you adopt, apply it consistently across the whole assignment.

## Undated and updated pages

Many CIPD web pages show a last-reviewed or last-updated date rather than a traditional publication date. Standard practice is to use that date as the year, because it tells the reader which version of the content you drew on. If a page genuinely carries no date anywhere, cite it with no date in place of the year, shaped like (CIPD, no date), and mirror that wording in the reference list entry.

Because these pages are revised over time, the accessed date is doing real work: it anchors your reference to the version you actually read. Record it on the day you use the source, not from memory at the end. If a factsheet is updated between your reading and your submission, the reference you captured remains an honest record of what you used, which is all a reference is required to be.

## Referencing the CIPD Profession Map

The Profession Map is best treated as an online resource with the CIPD as its corporate author. The entry generally runs the organisation, the year or no date depending on what the site shows, the resource title, an online marker, then the availability and accessed details. Shaped like: CIPD (no date) The CIPD Profession Map. Online. Available at the CIPD website. Accessed 12 July 2026.

In your text, cite it like any other CIPD source, and name the Map in your sentence when you discuss particular areas of it, so the reader knows which part of a large resource you mean. Describe its ideas in your own words rather than copying passages across: paraphrase plus citation shows understanding, keeps you inside your word count and respects the fact that the material is the CIPD's copyrighted work.

## Are CIPD factsheets enough on their own?

Factsheets are deliberately written as accessible overviews, which makes them excellent starting points and legitimate sources at every level. At Level 3, a well-used set of CIPD materials alongside a core textbook is often close to what markers expect, though your centre's guidance is the reference point, not this paragraph.

At Level 5, and especially at Level 7, markers look for evidence of wider reading: peer-reviewed journal articles, academic texts, research reports and official statistics sitting alongside the CIPD material. An answer resting entirely on factsheets will struggle to show the critical depth those levels demand. A practical tip: treat each factsheet's own reference list as a reading map, because the sources it cites are exactly the wider literature your marker hopes to see you engage with.

## A quick routine for capturing CIPD sources

1. When you open a useful CIPD page, note the CIPD as the author and find the most recent date on the page, or note that it is undated.
2. Record the exact title as published, and what kind of source it is: factsheet, report or web page.
3. Record today's date as your accessed date.
4. Draft the full reference entry immediately, in your centre's format, and add it to your growing list.
5. Insert the in-text citation, adding a lowercase letter to the year if you already cite another CIPD source from the same year.

## Common pitfalls with CIPD sources

- Citing a web address instead of the CIPD as corporate author
- Missing accessed dates on factsheets, reports and web pages
- Two same-year CIPD sources cited identically, leaving the marker unable to tell them apart
- Quoting long passages from factsheets instead of paraphrasing and analysing
- Building an entire reference list from factsheets at a level that expects wider reading
