A clear structure does two things: it keeps your thinking organised while you write, and it makes your work easy for an assessor to mark against the criteria. A well-structured average answer often outperforms a brilliant but chaotic one, because marks can only be awarded for what the assessor can actually find. Structure is not presentation polish added at the end. It is the frame that decides whether your knowledge becomes visible.

This guide is the hub for academic writing across the CIPD qualifications. It covers why structure carries marks, how to turn a brief into a section skeleton, when a report or an essay format fits, what strong introductions, bodies and conclusions each need to do, and how paragraphs, headings, tone and evidence fit together, with the differences between Level 3, Level 5 and Level 7 along the way. It finishes with a step-by-step planning method, a worked example that turns a two-task brief into a ready-to-write outline, and the mistakes that cost learners most. Companion guides take each element deeper, and they are signposted where they help.

## Why structure carries marks

Assessors do not read a CIPD assignment the way you read an article, from start to finish, forming a general impression and then settling on a grade. They mark against assessment criteria. For every criterion in the brief, they need to locate the place in your work where it is met, judge whether the treatment reaches the required depth, and record the result. Your structure is the map they use to do that. When the map is good, everything creditworthy gets credited. When it is poor, the assessor has to hunt, and hunting is where sound material gets missed.

This is why structure deserves deliberate effort even when your knowledge is strong. An answer organised around the criteria makes every point findable in seconds. An answer organised as a flowing tour of everything you know about the topic forces the assessor to do the sorting for you, and no assessor can confidently award marks for material they cannot match to a criterion. The learners who complain that their marker missed something are usually right, and the missed material was usually buried in the wrong section. Structure is how you make sure the reader never has to guess where anything lives.

## Decode the brief into a skeleton

Every well-structured assignment starts with the brief, not with a blank document. A CIPD brief typically sets out tasks, and each task contains one or more assessment criteria, each led by a command verb that tells you the depth required. Work through the brief in that order: identify the tasks, list the criteria inside each task, then plan a section for each criterion or for a tightly related cluster of criteria. The skeleton falls out of the brief almost mechanically once you read it this way, which is exactly the point. You are building the same map the assessor will mark from.

Command verbs deserve close attention at this stage because they determine the shape of each section, not just its content. A section answering explain is built differently from a section answering evaluate, and mismatching depth to verb is one of the most common ways answers lose marks. The companion guide CIPD Command Verbs: Analyse vs Evaluate vs Explain unpacks the main verbs in detail, and a printable command verb cheat sheet is available in the site's resources if you want the definitions at your elbow while you plan.

Two habits make the decoding stage reliable. First, keep the brief open beside you and translate it line by line into draft headings, rather than trusting your memory of what it asked. Second, check your own current brief and your study centre's guidance before you commit to any structure, because requirements vary by unit, by centre and over time, and the document in front of you always beats general advice, including this guide.

## Report or essay: reading what the brief wants

Most CIPD written work falls into one of two broad formats. A report is organised under headings, often numbered, and is built to be navigated rather than read straight through: a short introduction, criteria-led sections, and, where the brief asks for them, conclusions and recommendations that a busy reader could turn to directly. Report format tends to suit briefs framed around an organisational scenario, briefs that ask for recommendations, and briefs that name a workplace audience such as a leadership team.

An essay runs as continuous prose with an argument as its spine, few or no headings, and paragraphs that build on one another towards a position. Essay format tends to suit discussion-style questions, briefs that ask you to argue for a view or critically examine a debate, and some Level 7 work where sustained argument matters more than navigability.

The brief usually tells you which format is wanted, either directly or through its wording, and where the brief is silent your centre's guidance wins. If neither settles it, criteria-led sections under clear headings are rarely wrong in CIPD assessment, because they serve the marker's actual task. What matters most is that you decide the format before you write, since a report and an essay are planned differently from the first line.

## The anatomy of a strong answer

### An introduction that sets scope

A strong introduction does three jobs in three or four sentences: it states what the response covers, it signposts the order in which the sections will come, and, where the brief involves an organisational context, it introduces that context in a sentence or two. That is all. Introductions sprawl when learners use them to warm up, opening with sweeping claims about the modern workplace or a paragraph of definitions that belong in the body. Set the scope, point the way, and get into the first section while the word count is still yours to spend.

### A body that moves with the criteria

Within each body section, move from explanation to application to evaluation as far as the level and the command verb demand. Introduce the concept or model and make it clear, then show how it plays out in a real or scenario workplace, then, where the verb asks for it, weigh its strengths and limitations and reach a judgement. Not every section needs all three moves: a Level 3 explain task may live entirely in the first, while a Level 7 critique should spend most of its words in the third. Application is where higher marks live at Levels 5 and 7, because it is where you demonstrate that the theory means something in practice rather than on a flashcard.

### A conclusion that answers rather than summarises

A weak conclusion repeats the introduction with the tenses changed. A strong conclusion answers: it states the position your analysis has earned, draws the threads of the sections into a judgement, and, where the brief asks for recommendations, makes them specific, justified and traceable to the evidence you presented. Never introduce new material in a conclusion, because there is no room to earn it. If a point matters, it belongs in the body; if it does not, it does not belong at all.

## Signposting and headings

Headings are the most visible part of your structure, and in report-format work they should map one-to-one onto the tasks and criteria. Vague headings such as Main Findings or Discussion make the assessor open every section to find out what it holds. Precise headings do the finding for them. Within sections, brief signpost sentences keep the reader oriented: a line at the start of a long section saying what it will cover in what order, or a linking sentence showing how this section builds on the last, costs a few words and buys a great deal of clarity.

> Tutor tip: build your headings from the brief's own language. If a task asks you to analyse approaches to reward, a heading such as Analysis of Reward Approaches tells the assessor exactly where that criterion is answered before they have read a word of the section.

## Paragraph discipline

Underneath the headings, the paragraph is the real unit of your answer, and the discipline is simple to state: one idea per paragraph, built as a point, an explanation, evidence or an example, and a link back to the question. A paragraph that opens by stating its point makes the whole answer skimmable, and an assessor who can skim your points is an assessor who can find your criteria. Paragraphs that carry three ideas at once, or that open with throat-clearing rather than a claim, are the most common structural fault inside otherwise well-planned work. ## Tone

Structure and tone are close cousins: both exist to make your thinking easy to receive. The tone CIPD assessors want is clear, precise, evidenced and measured, which is not the same as long words and formality for its own sake. Contractions, slang and chat undermine authority, but so does padding and thesaurus-driven pomposity, and both cost clarity. Write as a capable professional briefing another professional. The companion guide Academic Tone in CIPD Assignments, Without the Stuffiness covers the traps and the fixes, including when the first person belongs in your work and when it does not.

## Evidence

Structure also decides where your evidence sits. Claims need backing as they are made, not in a general nod to the literature at the end, so plan which models, sources and workplace examples each section will use before you write it. Cite as you go in the style your centre requires, usually Harvard, and finish with a complete, consistent reference list in which every in-text citation appears. A dedicated companion guide to using evidence in CIPD assignments covers how to choose sources, how much citation is enough, and how to weave evidence into your own argument rather than around it.

## What changes at each level

At Level 3, briefs mostly ask you to explain and describe. Structure at this level is about coverage and clarity: a section per criterion, accurate explanation in plain English, and simple workplace examples. Depth of argument matters less than completeness and organisation.

At Level 5, analyse and evaluate arrive, and structure has to make room for them. Sections need space to break ideas into parts, examine relationships and reach judgements, which is why word budgets matter more here: an analysis squeezed into an explanation-sized section reads as description. Application to a real organisational context is expected rather than optional.

At Level 7, the expectation shifts to argument and critique. Work at this level is structured around a position: sections build a case, engage seriously with counter-arguments and the limitations of the evidence, and the conclusion carries real weight. The skeleton still starts from the brief, but the connective logic between sections matters as much as the sections themselves.

These are general patterns across the qualification framework, not unit rules. Your current brief and centre guidance define what your submission actually needs, so read them as the final word.

## From brief to plan: a step-by-step method

1. Read the brief twice, once for overall shape and once with a pen, marking every task, criterion, command verb, word count and format instruction.
2. Check your centre's guidance for anything the brief leaves open, such as format, referencing style or the use of appendices.
3. List every criterion and group the list into sections, one per criterion or one per tight cluster, in the order the brief presents them.
4. Draft a heading for each section using the brief's own language, so the map between your work and the criteria is visible at a glance.
5. Allocate a word budget to each section in proportion to its weight and the depth its verb demands, and hold back a small margin for the introduction and conclusion.
6. Note under each heading the models, evidence and workplace examples that section will use, so no section starts from nothing.
7. Write the body sections first, starting wherever your confidence is highest, then write the conclusion, and write the introduction last, when you know exactly what you are introducing.
8. Edit against the skeleton: read each section asking whether its criterion is unmistakably met at the depth its verb requires, and move or cut anything that sits in the wrong place.
9. Build the reference list, check every citation appears in it, and proofread once aloud before submitting.

## Worked example: turning a two-task brief into a skeleton

The example below is entirely fictional. Priya is an invented Level 5 learner working in people services at Bramley Shaw, a made-up regional housing association, and her brief, word counts and organisation are all constructed for illustration. Her assignment allows 3,900 words across two tasks. Task one asks her to explain the factors that shape reward decisions and to analyse the approaches to reward used in her organisation. Task two asks her to evaluate two approaches to performance management and make justified recommendations for Bramley Shaw.

Working through the brief, Priya finds two command verbs in task one and two in task two, and plans six blocks around them.

- Introduction, 150 words: scope, two sentences of Bramley Shaw context, signposting.
- Factors shaping reward decisions, 700 words: explanation-led, covering internal and external factors with brief examples, cited as she goes.
- Analysis of reward approaches at Bramley Shaw, 900 words: the organisation's current approaches broken into parts, how they interact, and what they mean for attraction and retention.
- Evaluation of two performance management approaches, 1,100 words: each approach explained briefly, then strengths and limitations weighed against evidence, ending in a judgement about fit.
- Recommendations for Bramley Shaw, 700 words: specific, justified, and traceable to the evaluation above them.
- Conclusion, 250 words: the position her analysis has earned, drawn together without new material.

The budgets total 3,800 words, leaving a small margin, and they follow the verbs: the evaluate section is the largest because judgement needs room, while the explain section is the leanest. Before writing a sentence of prose, Priya knows what every section must achieve, roughly how long it will run, and which sources each will lean on. The blank page problem never arrives, because there is no blank page.

## Common mistakes to avoid

- Structuring around a story or a topic tour instead of the tasks and criteria, so the assessor has to excavate the answer
- Writing the introduction first and letting it swallow words the analysis needed
- Vague headings such as Discussion or Findings that hide where each criterion is met
- One undivided section per task, with no internal shape for the separate criteria inside it
- Word budgets ignored until the end, so the final sections are starved and the depth collapses exactly where the verbs demand it
- Essay format where the brief wanted a report, or the reverse, because the format instruction was never checked
- New material introduced in the conclusion instead of being argued in the body
- A reference list that does not match the citations, unravelling the credibility of an otherwise sound answer

## FAQs

### Should I use headings in a CIPD assignment?

In report-format work, yes: headings that map to the tasks and criteria are exactly what assessors want, because they make every criterion findable. In essay-format work, headings are usually fewer or absent, and structure is carried by paragraph openings instead. Your brief and centre guidance settle which format applies, so check them first.

### How should I split the word count between sections?

In proportion to weight and depth. Sections answering evaluate or analyse need substantially more room than sections answering explain, because judgement and analysis cannot be done briefly, and the introduction and conclusion together should stay a small fraction of the total. Set the budgets at the planning stage and check them as you draft, not after.

### Should I write the introduction first or last?

Last, in most cases. An introduction promises what the work delivers, and it is far easier to promise accurately once the body exists. Plan the introduction's job early, three or four sentences of scope, context and signposting, but write it when you know exactly what you are introducing.

### What if the brief does not say whether it wants a report or an essay?

Check your centre's guidance next, because centres often set format expectations that sit alongside the brief. If both are genuinely silent, criteria-led sections under clear headings are rarely wrong in CIPD assessment. If you remain unsure, ask your tutor before you plan, since format shapes everything that follows.
