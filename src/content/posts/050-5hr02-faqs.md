Here are answers to the questions learners ask most often about 5HR02, Talent management and workforce planning, the Level 5 unit that covers how organisations plan for, find, develop and keep the people they need. The answers below give you the method and the principles, but formats and requirements vary between study centres, so always check the details against your own current brief and your centre's guidance.

### What is 5HR02 about?

5HR02, Talent management and workforce planning, sits within the Level 5 Associate Diploma in People Management. It covers workforce planning and labour markets, resourcing, recruitment and selection, talent management and succession, and turnover, retention and their costs.

The thread running through all of it is organisational performance. The unit trains you to treat people activities as connected: planning tells you what the organisation needs, resourcing and talent management supply it, and retention protects the investment. Strong answers keep that thread visible and back it with relevant data and examples.

### Is 5HR02 hard?

Most learners find the individual topics familiar, because recruitment, retention and talent are the everyday material of people work. The difficulty sits in three places: linking workforce planning to business needs rather than describing it in the abstract, using turnover and retention data as real evidence, and applying concepts to a specific organisation.

In other words, the unit is less about learning hard content and more about learning to argue like a practitioner. If you choose an organisational context early and force every answer to connect to it, the unit becomes much more manageable.

### What is workforce planning in simple terms?

Workforce planning is how an organisation makes sure it has the right people, with the right skills, at the right time and at a cost it can afford. In practice it is a repeating loop: work out what people the strategy demands, work out what people are available inside and outside the organisation, find the gaps, act to close them, and check whether the actions worked.

The reason organisations bother is lead time. Hiring a specialist or developing one internally takes months or years, so waiting for gaps to appear is expensive. Planning moves the decisions earlier, when there are more options and better prices.

### Do I need real turnover data?

No. If your brief provides figures, use those and interpret them carefully. If it does not, you can reason with realistic patterns that you clearly frame as illustrative, or cite published sources such as ONS labour market statistics for external context.

What markers credit is interpretation, not possession of data. A simple pattern, such as leavers concentrated in the first year of service, analysed properly and connected to a recommendation, is worth far more than an impressive-looking figure with no source and no analysis. Never invent statistics or attribute numbers to sources you have not read.

### Which models or frameworks help?

A small, well-used set goes a long way. The workforce planning cycle gives you a structure for planning questions. The nine-box grid is a recognised way into talent and succession discussions, provided you note its limitations. The idea of an employee value proposition helps with attraction and retention questions, and the CIPD Profession Map is useful background on why evidence and business alignment matter.

Whatever you use, apply it to your organisation. A framework that is named but never applied earns almost nothing, while a framework applied critically, with its weaknesses acknowledged, shows exactly the thinking the unit rewards.

### Can I use my own organisation?

Usually yes, and it often makes application easier and more convincing, but follow your brief, because some centres provide a case study you must work with. If you do use your employer, anonymise sensitive details and make sure you are entitled to share any figures you include.

If your own organisation is very small or unusual, a clearly fictional organisation can work just as well. What matters is that the context is specific enough to plan for: named roles, a stated strategy and a recognisable labour market.

### Why do people get referred on 5HR02?

The most common causes are describing concepts without applying them, producing recommendations with no visible link to business needs or evidence, ignoring labour market context, and making claims about turnover or retention with no data or reasoning behind them. Misreading command verbs, such as explaining when a task asks you to evaluate, is another regular cause.

All of these are avoidable with a structured approach: fix your context, keep the business need in view, and let every recommendation trace back to evidence. The complete guide for this unit walks through that method step by step.

### How do the recruitment questions differ from the talent questions?

Recruitment and selection questions are about bringing people in: the methods an organisation uses to attract candidates and choose between them, and how those choices fit the role and the labour market. They reward evaluation, because every method has strengths, weaknesses and costs.

Talent management questions are about the people already inside: how organisations identify potential, develop it and prepare successors for critical roles. They reward a view on strategy, such as whether talent should mean a select group or everyone, and a link to business continuity and risk. The two connect through workforce planning, which decides how much of the answer should be buying talent and how much should be building it.

### How should I use labour market information?

Use it to justify choices, not to decorate them. Whether the relevant labour market is tight or loose should change what you recommend: in a tight market, development, retention and a stronger employee value proposition beat volume recruitment, while a loose market makes hiring a more reliable lever.

Distinguish internal from external. Internal labour market information is the organisation's own workforce data, such as skills, turnover and retirement patterns. External information comes from sources such as ONS labour market statistics, sector commentary and local knowledge about competitors and skills pipelines. Citing a credible external source and drawing a specific conclusion from it is a quick, visible way to lift an answer.

### How do I handle the word count?

Word counts and tolerances are set by your centre, so check your brief rather than relying on any figure you find online. Plan your allocation across tasks in proportion to their weight, and confirm how your centre treats tables, appendices and reference lists, because conventions differ.

If you run over, cut description first. Definitions and background compress well, while application, interpretation and justified recommendations are where the marks live and should be protected.
