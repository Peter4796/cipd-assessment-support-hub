If you are starting 7HR02, Resourcing and talent management to sustain success, you are taking on the unit of the Level 7 Advanced Diploma in Strategic People Management that examines the claims the people profession makes most confidently and tests them hardest. Resourcing and talent management come with an impressive toolkit: workforce plans, succession grids, high-potential programmes, employee value propositions. The unit's question is whether any of it actually sustains organisational success in a changing environment, and how anyone would know.

That question is the trap as well as the territory. Learners who know the tools tend to describe them fluently and evaluate them barely at all, and at Level 7 fluent description gets referred. Critical evaluation of approaches, supported by evidence, is central to a strong submission. ## What 7HR02 is about

7HR02 develops a strategic, critical view of resourcing and talent management. Where an operational unit might ask how to run a recruitment campaign or maintain a succession plan, this one asks whether these activities sustain organisational success, under what conditions, and at what cost. Its territory covers strategic workforce and succession planning, talent management strategies and their impact, resourcing in a global and changing context, and the evaluation and measurement of resourcing effectiveness.

The theoretical anchor many answers reach for is the resource-based view of the firm: the argument that sustained advantage comes from resources competitors cannot easily copy, including people and the capabilities built around them. A strong answer treats it as an argument to examine rather than a fact to recite, asking when people really are hard to imitate, and that habit of treating theory as a claim under scrutiny is the register of the whole unit.

## What masters-level assessment expects

The defining move at Level 7 is treating talent strategies as contested claims rather than practices to describe. Does talent management actually sustain organisational success? That is an open question in the academic literature, and your answers are expected to enter the argument. An account of how a nine-box grid works belongs at a lower level; an evaluation of whether the grid identifies anything real, and what happens to the people it labels, is postgraduate work.

The contrast with Level 5 makes the register concrete. 5HR02 teaches the mechanics of workforce planning: the cycle, the techniques, the data. 7HR02 assumes you know the mechanics and assesses something different: whether and when these strategies sustain success, and what evidence would tell you. Evidence matters throughout. Labour market data from bodies such as the ONS, the OECD and the ILO gives your context a factual footing, academic literature gives your evaluation its counter-arguments, and you must never invent findings or attach figures to studies from memory.

## The debates your answers are expected to enter

The topics of 7HR02 are structured disagreements, and strong answers argue inside them rather than reporting on them. Four tensions recur across almost every brief.

Exclusive versus inclusive talent. Is talent a select group of high-potential individuals who disproportionately drive performance, or is it everyone, with success depending on developing capability across the whole workforce? The exclusive view assumes performance is concentrated and identifiable in advance, the inclusive view assumes capability is broadly distributed and responsive to investment, and neither assumption is self-evidently true. A strong answer asks which assumptions fit the organisation in question rather than declaring a winner in the abstract.

Planning versus volatility. Strategic workforce planning promises that organisations can forecast future demand and prepare in advance. In volatile environments that promise is questionable, and a serious answer asks whether long-range planning is still feasible or has become a comforting ritual that produces precise numbers about an unknowable future. The mature position usually sits between abandonment and faith: planning as scenarios and capabilities rather than predictions and headcounts.

Standardise or localise. Global resourcing forces a choice between consistent worldwide approaches, which promise fairness, mobility and a single employer brand, and localised approaches, which respect labour markets, institutions and cultures that differ profoundly. Every global talent strategy sits somewhere on this line, and the marks lie in analysing the trade-off rather than asserting that one end is best practice.

Measurement and attribution. What does resourcing effectiveness even mean, and how would you know a talent strategy caused the success that followed it? Organisations that thrive after a talent programme were often already thriving, which is why they could afford it. The attribution problem does not make measurement pointless, but it makes naive claims of impact suspect, and markers reward answers that handle it honestly.

## Why 7HR02 answers get referred

Tutors marking this unit see four patterns again and again, and none of them is a lack of intelligence.

The first is describing planning cycles instead of evaluating them. The answer walks through demand forecasting, supply analysis and gap closing, and never asks whether the cycle works, when it fails, or what its outputs are worth in a volatile market. That is Level 5 material delivered to a Level 7 marker.

The second is treating talent as self-evidently valuable. The answer assumes talent programmes drive performance and proceeds straight to design questions, never noticing that this assumption is the very claim the unit asks you to evaluate. At this level, the value of talent management is a conclusion to be argued, not a premise to be borrowed.

The third is tool-worship. The nine-box grid, competency models and planning frameworks are presented as authorities rather than artefacts: widely used, but also widely criticised for subjectivity, for rating the recent past rather than potential, and for hardening judgements that ought to stay provisional. Answers that cite a tool as if that settles the question have the relationship backwards: the unit expects you to judge the tool.

The fourth is no engagement with volatility and uncertainty. The answer discusses planning, succession and global resourcing as if the environment holds still. The unit is explicitly about sustaining success in a changing environment, and answers that ignore volatility read as unrealistic however tidy their frameworks.

## How to approach 7HR02, step by step

1. Break down the brief. Identify every task and its command verbs, and check what the criteria in your brief actually ask you to demonstrate; critically evaluate is an instruction to weigh claims and judge, not to describe practice.
2. Fix the register before you read. For each topic, write down the evaluative question behind it: not how workforce planning works but whether it delivers, not what a talent programme contains but what it changes.
3. Map each task onto the debates. Work out whether the task sits inside the exclusive versus inclusive tension, the planning versus volatility tension, the global trade-off or the measurement problem, and note the strongest version of each side.
4. Read for disagreement. Build a reading list that includes critics of talent management as well as advocates, because a bibliography that agrees with itself cannot support critical evaluation.
5. Ground your context in citable evidence. Use labour market data from sources such as the ONS, the OECD and the ILO in general terms, and never attach specific findings or figures to studies you cannot verify.
6. Evaluate tools as tools. For every grid, model or framework you use, state what it assumes, what it is criticised for, and why it still earns its place in your argument, if it does.
7. Stress-test everything against volatility. For each recommendation, ask what would have to stay true for it to work, and what the organisation should do when those conditions fail.
8. Review the draft against register. Ask of each section: is a claim being evaluated, is evidence doing real work, and is uncertainty acknowledged? Rework anything that fails all three.

> Tutor tip: before you submit, find every sentence in your draft that asserts a resourcing or talent practice works. Each one needs evidence, an argument or a stated condition attached; an unsupported claim of impact is the single most common register fault on 7HR02.

## A worked example: Halworth and the talent programme decision

Here is a fictional scenario showing the register this unit rewards. Halworth is an invented mid-sized insurance firm facing a wave of retirements in its underwriting and leadership population, and Priya, an invented Level 7 learner in its people team, has a brief asking her to critically evaluate two options: an exclusive high-potential programme for a small identified group, or an inclusive development approach open to the whole professional workforce.

A weak answer describes both options, lists advantages and disadvantages copied from textbook summaries, and recommends the exclusive programme because leading firms run one. Nothing has been evaluated: the performance assumptions behind each option are never examined, the risk that an exclusive scheme demotivates the unchosen majority is never priced, and the recommendation rests on imitation rather than argument.

A strong answer starts with the assumptions. The exclusive programme assumes that future leadership capability is concentrated in a few people, that Halworth can identify them in advance, and that concentrating investment will retain them; Priya notes that identification tools such as the nine-box grid are criticised for rewarding visibility and recent performance, so the second assumption is the fragile one. The inclusive approach assumes capability is distributed and broad development lifts performance, which fits a professional workforce but spreads investment thinly against an urgent succession gap. She then reasons from Halworth's position: the retirement wave makes targeted succession cover urgent, but a tight labour market for underwriters, checked against published labour market data, makes the demotivation risk of an exclusive scheme expensive too. Her recommendation is a conditional hybrid: targeted succession development for the retirement-exposed roles, an inclusive development offer around it, and a measurement plan that tracks retention and internal fill rates while admitting that attribution to either strand will stay uncertain. Her conclusion states what would have to be true for the advice to hold.

The difference is not knowledge of talent management. It is that the second answer tests every claim against assumptions, evidence and conditions, and lets the recommendation carry its uncertainty honestly. That shape transfers to every 7HR02 task, whatever your brief asks.

## Common mistakes to avoid

The recurring faults are predictable: describing the planning cycle instead of evaluating it, assuming talent management works rather than arguing it, citing tools such as the nine-box grid as authorities rather than artefacts, ignoring volatility and the global dimension, inventing or half-remembering statistics instead of using citable sources, and hedging so thoroughly that no judgement is ever made. Weighing both sides is the method, but the product is a conclusion about what this organisation should do and under what conditions. An answer that ends by observing that it depends has described the debate and contributed nothing to it.

## 7HR02 FAQs

### Is it acceptable to argue that talent management does not deliver?

Yes, if you argue it. The unit treats the value of talent management as an open question, and a sceptical answer that weighs the evidence and reaches a critical conclusion can score highly. What fails is scepticism without argument, exactly as cheerleading fails without it.

### Do I need to know lots of models and frameworks?

You need a working command of the main ideas: workforce and succession planning, build, buy and borrow choices, the exclusive and inclusive positions, the employee value proposition and the resource-based view. Depth of evaluation beats breadth of coverage: better to interrogate a few frameworks properly than to catalogue many.

### Can I write about my own organisation?

Check your brief, but organisational material is usually welcome as illustration or as the subject of analysis, provided the analysis is genuinely critical. The risk is loyalty: evaluating your employer's talent strategy in the terms its designers use is the descriptive trap in miniature, so be ready to ask whether the strategy actually sustains success and how anyone would know.

### What if my brief looks different from this guide?

Follow your brief every time. Task formats, word counts and question wording vary between study centres and change over time, so use this guide for method and register, and your own current brief and centre guidance for the exact requirements.
