Here are answers to the questions learners ask most often about 7HR03, Strategic reward management, the Level 7 unit that looks at reward from a strategic perspective: reward strategy and philosophy, governance, executive and international reward, and fairness, transparency and the ethics of reward. Its assessment rewards critical evaluation of reward choices and their consequences over description of reward practice, and most of the questions below come back to that in one way or another. The answers give you principles and method, but task formats and requirements vary between study centres, so always check the details against your own current brief and your centre's guidance.

### What is 7HR03 about?

7HR03 sits within the Level 7 Advanced Diploma in Strategic People Management and develops a strategic, critical understanding of reward: how reward strategy and philosophy connect to organisational strategy, how reward decisions are governed, what financial and non-financial reward each contribute at a strategic level, and how the contested territories of executive pay, international reward, fairness and transparency should be analysed. It is the unit that asks what an organisation is really buying with its reward spend, and at what cost to fairness and legitimacy.

The standard is postgraduate throughout. Wider reading and critical analysis are essential, description of reward mechanics earns little, and the marks sit in evaluation, trade-offs and justified judgement about questions on which serious commentators disagree.

### How is 7HR03 different from 5HR03?

5HR03 teaches reward principles: what the main elements of reward are, how pay structures and benefits work, and how reward supports performance. 7HR03 assumes all of that and assesses something different: critical evaluation of strategic reward choices and their consequences, including the debates where the field genuinely disagrees with itself.

The practical test is your verbs. If your paragraphs explain what a practice is or how a scheme operates, you are writing at Level 5; if they weigh a choice against its alternatives and judge its consequences for strategy, fairness and cost, you are writing at Level 7.

### What is a reward philosophy?

A reward philosophy is an organisation's set of answers to the foundational questions about pay: where to position against the market, whether to pay for performance or for the role, how much dispersion between top and bottom to accept, and how transparent to be. Every organisation has one, because every organisation enacts answers to these questions in its decisions, whether or not anything is written down.

For assignments, the useful move is reconstruction: read an organisation's actual pay behaviour back into the philosophy it implies, then compare that implied philosophy with the strategy the organisation says it is pursuing. The gaps between the two are usually where the best analysis lives.

### How do I write about executive pay critically without ranting?

Separate the analytical question from the moral verdict. The unit asks you to weigh the justifications for high executive pay, the market for scarce senior talent and the incentive alignment arguments, against the critiques, benchmarking that ratchets pay upward, a contested link between executive pay and long-term performance, and the damage to legitimacy when pay at the top diverges from experience below. Present both sides at their strongest, then judge for the specific case in front of you.

The register to aim for is a board adviser, not a columnist. A well-argued critical position is entirely acceptable, and so is a well-argued defence; what fails is heat without analysis in either direction, because the marker learns nothing from it except your mood.

### What is reward governance, and why does it keep coming up?

Reward governance is the question of who decides pay, under what incentives and pressures, and answerable to whom. At executive level it is formalised through remuneration committees, which exist to bring independent judgement to pay decisions that executives cannot credibly make about themselves; how independent and effective those committees really are is one of the unit's live debates. Below board level, governance covers who approves pay decisions, what consistency checks exist, and how drift from the stated philosophy would ever be noticed.

It keeps coming up because design without governance is only half an analysis. A well-designed scheme governed badly will decay, and many reward failures are governance failures wearing a design costume, which is why markers reward answers that ask who decided as well as what was decided.

### How do I handle the international dimension?

Treat it as a structured tension between consistency and localisation rather than a list of country facts. Global consistency offers fairness across borders, one employment brand and administrative coherence; localisation answers that labour markets, living costs and cultural norms differ so much that identical treatment across countries can itself be unfair. Most organisations land on a hybrid, holding some elements globally consistent and flexing others locally, and the analytical work is in justifying where the line sits for a given organisation and why.

You do not need detailed knowledge of any country's pay landscape. You need to reason about the trade-off in general terms, apply it to the scenario in your brief, and acknowledge what the position you recommend costs.

### What does the transparency debate involve?

Transparency is a strategic choice, not a virtue, and the debate is about what openness changes. In its favour: disclosure builds trust, makes unjustified gaps harder to sustain, and disciplines inconsistent decision-making. Against it: openness creates compression pressure, exposes differentials that are defensible but hard to explain, and sits awkwardly with privacy. Pay gap reporting, described in general terms, has made some disclosure obligatory for many employers, which shifts the interesting question to how organisations respond beyond compliance.

A strong answer evaluates a specific degree of transparency for a specific organisation, weighing what it would fix against what it would complicate, rather than declaring for or against openness in the abstract.

### Why do people get referred on 7HR03?

Four patterns dominate. Describing reward practices instead of evaluating the choices behind them. Moralising about executive pay, in either direction, without weighing the arguments. Ignoring governance, so the answer never asks who decides pay and answerable to whom. And treating fairness as a slogan, declaring arrangements fair or unfair without unpacking fair in what sense and compared with whom.

None of these is a knowledge problem, which is why referred learners are often surprised. They are register problems, and they are fixable: the same material, reworked so that choices are evaluated, both sides of each debate are weighed, and fairness is analysed rather than invoked, usually contains everything a passing answer needs.

### Do I need pay data or statistics in my answers?

No. The unit assesses reasoning about reward choices, and every debate it contains can be argued from principles, theory and the evidence in your brief. If you do cite a figure, it must come from a named public source you have genuinely read, and it must matter to the argument; decorative statistics add risk without adding marks.

What you should never do is invent numbers, ratios or research findings to make an argument look empirical. An unsourced statistic is a credibility problem that can undermine an otherwise strong answer, and honest argument from principle is always the safer and better-marked route.

### How do I handle the word count?

Word counts and tolerances are set by your centre, so work from your own brief rather than any figure you find online, and check how reference lists, tables and appendices are treated. Allocate words across tasks in proportion to their weight before you draft.

If you are over the limit, cut description first: explanations of what reward practices are, background on the organisation, and scene-setting. The marks live in evaluation, trade-offs and judgement, and trimming description to protect argument almost always improves a 7HR03 answer anyway.
